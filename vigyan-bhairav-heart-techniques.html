The Techniques for Heart & Devotion

Five techniques that use the heart — love, devotion, music, feeling, and eternity — as doorways to the divine. The heart knows what the mind cannot grasp: that love is not something you DO but something you ARE when the defences drop.

5 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These five techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The heart and devotion sutras form the twelfth major category — using love, surrender, music, feeling, and the eternal present moment to dissolve the ego through the heart rather than through the mind.

These techniques approach awakening through LOVE rather than understanding. Where other categories use the mind (inquiry, witnessing, negation), these techniques bypass the mind entirely and go straight to the heart. The heart does not need to understand in order to open — it only needs to feel. And feeling, when it is total, IS enlightenment.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These heart techniques work through FEELING, not through understanding. The mind wants to analyse love; the heart wants to DROWN in it. Let yourself drown. Analysis can come later (or never — the heart doesn’t need it).

New to heart meditation?

Start with Technique 3 — feel love filling you from all sides or Technique 4 — enter music totally. Both are immediately accessible, require no belief system, and produce warmth and opening quickly. Spend 20–30 minutes a day for 21 days.

Technique 29 of 112
Category: Heart & Devotion · Tags: devotion · love · surrender · freedom
Devotion frees.

In Plain English

Pour total love toward any beloved — a person, God, existence. The quality of total self-giving empties the heart of ego. What remains is pure love without an object — divine love. Devotion is the heart’s way of dissolving the self: you cannot THINK yourself into egolessness, but you can LOVE yourself into it. When giving is total, the giver disappears.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your beloved: Who or what can your heart pour itself toward without reservation? A teacher, God in any form, the universe, a loved one, nature, truth, beauty, existence itself. The object matters less than the QUALITY of your devotion. Choose whatever makes your heart overflow most naturally.
  2. Create a devotional space: Light a candle, place a photo or symbol of your beloved, play soft devotional music if it helps. The space should say to your heart: “Here you are safe to love without limit.” Or go to a natural cathedral — a forest, a hilltop, a riverbank — wherever your heart opens spontaneously.
  3. Open the heart centre: Sit or kneel. Bring awareness to the centre of the chest. Feel it softening, warming, expanding. You may place a hand over the heart to connect more deeply. Breathe into the heart space. Let it become the centre of your world.
  4. Begin to pour: Now direct the heart’s energy toward your chosen beloved. Not as thought (“I love you”) but as FEELING — a warmth, a flow, a stream of devotional energy moving from your heart toward the beloved. Let it be continuous, like a river that never stops flowing.
  5. Give yourself totally: Don’t just give love — give YOURSELF. Give your thoughts, your memories, your identity, your fears, your hopes. Pour everything into the stream. The beloved is a vast ocean; you are a river emptying into it. Hold nothing back. Keep nothing in reserve.
  6. Feel the emptying: As you give and give, notice: you are becoming empty. Lighter. More spacious. The more you give, the less remains of “you.” This is not loss — it is liberation. Every ounce of self given away is an ounce of freedom gained. The ego dissolves in the acid of total love.
  7. When nothing is left: Continue pouring until there is no one left to pour. The giver has been given. The river has reached the ocean and is ocean now. In this moment, there is no “you” and no “beloved” — only love itself, without subject or object. THIS is divine love. Not love FROM someone TO someone, but love as the fabric of existence.
  8. Rest in freedom: The sutra says “devotion frees.” Feel the freedom. Not freedom FROM something (that is merely escape) but freedom AS something — freedom as your natural state when the ego is dissolved. You are free because there is no one left to be imprisoned. The cage is empty. The bird is the sky.
  9. Use devotional practices: Singing bhajans, chanting mantras, writing love letters to God, prostrating before your symbol, serving others selflessly — all are forms of pouring. Choose whichever form helps your heart pour most naturally. The form is the vehicle; the pouring is the practice.
  10. Make daily life devotional: Once you taste the freedom, bring devotion into everything. Cook as an offering. Walk as a prayer. Work as service. Each action done with devotional quality empties the ego a little more. Eventually, life itself becomes continuous devotion — a constant pouring of love toward existence.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing devotion with dependence — devotion empties the self; dependence fills it with need and clinging
  • Becoming proud of one’s devotion (“I am so devoted”) — this creates a new, subtler ego
  • Devotion to a person becoming possessive attachment — true devotion does not need response or reciprocation
  • Holding back a “watcher” who observes the devotion — total devotion has no witness; give the watcher too
  • Intellectualising instead of feeling — the head cannot do devotion; only the heart can

Questions From The Book

Can devotion be practised without believing in God?

Chapter 60, "Devotion Frees"

Osho says absolutely: devotion to truth, to beauty, to nature, to existence itself — all work. Even devotion to one’s own inquiry. What matters is the quality of TOTAL self-giving, not the theological framework. A scientist lost in wonder at the cosmos is more devoted than a believer mechanically repeating prayers. The intensity of self-giving is everything; the belief system is nothing.

Why only two words — the shortest sutra?

Chapter 60

Osho explains: because devotion cannot be taught through long explanation. Either you know the taste of total self-giving or you do not. Words cannot convey it — only experience can. Two words are sufficient: devotion frees. Try it. The experience will teach you everything that words cannot. The brevity of the sutra IS the teaching — devotion is simple, direct, and needs no elaboration. It is the heart speaking to the heart in the fewest possible words.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Devotion reverses the direction of attention. Normally all attention flows INWARD (self-referential processing, the default mode network). Devotion redirects ALL attention OUTWARD toward the beloved. When the stream of self-attention is completely redirected, the self-structure loses its fuel and temporarily dissolves. Freedom is the experiential result of this dissolution — no self means no prison.

Research on self-transcendent emotions: Studies on awe, gratitude, and love show that these emotions reliably reduce self-referential thinking and increase prosocial behaviour. The mechanism is identical to what this technique produces: when the heart opens fully toward something greater, the small self becomes transparent. This is measurable, replicable, and universal across cultures.

One-line essenceTwo words contain the entire path: Devotion frees. Pour yourself out so totally that no one remains to be imprisoned. The empty vessel IS freedom.
Technique 51 of 112
Category: Tantric Union · Tags: joy · reunion · permeation · love
On joyously seeing a long-absent friend, permeate this joy.

In Plain English

When meeting a beloved friend after long absence, CATCH the joy. Don’t let it pass unconsciously. Feel it fully — let it permeate every cell. That joy of reunion is the same quality as divine love. Permeate it, become it, and you taste the eternal.

How to Practice

  1. Understand “permeate”: Normally when joy arises — at seeing a loved one, receiving good news, experiencing beauty — we feel it briefly and then it passes. We are too distracted, too much in our heads, to let it fully FILL us. “Permeate” means: let the joy soak into every cell, every corner of consciousness. Like a sponge dropped in water — saturate completely.
  2. Watch for the moment: The technique begins BEFORE the reunion. Whenever you know you will meet someone you love after long absence (arriving at an airport, opening a door, receiving a call), prepare your awareness. Tell yourself: “When the joy comes, I will CATCH it. I will not let it pass unconsciously.”
  3. The first instant: When you see the friend — in that FIRST instant of recognition, that flash of joy — CATCH IT. Don’t let the mind immediately start talking, planning, asking questions. Stay with the pure flash of joy for even 3–5 seconds before engaging. That first flash is the purest form of the energy.
  4. Let it permeate: Instead of the joy staying in the head (as an idea: “I’m happy to see you”), let it sink downward into the body. Feel joy in the chest, the belly, the legs, the arms. Let it fill the WHOLE body. Not just “I feel happy” but the entire organism vibrating with joy. Head to toes. Every cell celebrating.
  5. Become the joy: Take it further: not “I FEEL joy” but “I AM joy.” There is no separate “you” who experiences joy — there is only joy, permeating everything. The boundary between feeler and feeling dissolves. You don’t HAVE joy; you ARE joy. This is permeation at its deepest.
  6. Don’t rush into activity: The social habit is to immediately embrace, talk, catch up, make plans. These are all fine LATER. But first, take 30 seconds to simply BE with the joy. Let it permeate. Then engage. Those 30 seconds of pure permeation teach you more about the nature of consciousness than years of philosophy.
  7. Extend to smaller joys: Once you practise with the intense joy of reunion, apply the same technique to smaller joys: seeing a flower bloom, tasting good food, hearing a favourite song, watching a sunset. CATCH the joy. Let it permeate. Become it. Every joy — however small — is a doorway to the divine when fully permeated.
  8. The teaching: What this technique reveals is that joy does not come FROM the friend, the flower, or the sunset. Joy is your natural state — these external triggers simply give you PERMISSION to feel what is always there. By practising permeation, you learn to access joy directly, without needing a trigger. The friend was the excuse; the joy is your nature.
  9. Practice without trigger: After weeks of permeating joy whenever it naturally arises, try this: sit quietly and REMEMBER the joy of reunion (similar to Technique 3). Let the memory permeate you. Then let go of even the memory — can you feel joy with NO cause? This is the ultimate goal: causeless joy, your natural state rediscovered.
  10. Daily awareness: Throughout the day, notice every moment of joy — however fleeting — and practise permeation. The barista smiles at you: permeate. A child laughs nearby: permeate. You complete a task: permeate. Each micro-permeation teaches the body that joy is always available. Over weeks, the background state shifts from neutral to joyful.

  • Clinging to joy after it naturally fades — permeate fully WHILE it is here, but let it go when it passes
  • Performing joy for others rather than genuinely feeling it — this is internal work, not external display
  • Judging yourself for not feeling joy when “you should” — sometimes sadness is present; honour that too

Questions From The Book

Why does Shiva place this technique among the tantric sutras?

Chapter 42, "Permeate This Joy"

Osho explains: because tantra is the path of using positive emotions as vehicles for transcendence. Just as sexual energy can become a vehicle (Techniques 1-3), so can JOY. Joy is love-energy in its purest, most accessible form. When joy is fully permeated, the same dissolution happens as in sexual union — the separate self dissolves into the feeling. The difference is only intensity: sex is the strongest trigger, but joy is the most available. This technique brings tantra into ordinary daily life.

What is the difference between “feeling joy” and “permeating joy”?

Chapter 42

Osho distinguishes: “feeling joy” maintains the duality — there is a “you” who feels and a “joy” that is felt. “Permeating joy” dissolves the duality — joy fills every cell until there is no space left for a separate “you.” Permeation means: there is nowhere in you where joy is NOT. The feeler becomes the feeling. The experiencer becomes the experience. THAT is the tantric method applied to joy — dissolution through total immersion.

What this is really pointing at: Positive psychology research shows that “savouring” — deliberately attending to and prolonging positive experiences — significantly increases well-being and life satisfaction. It is the difference between tasting chocolate and BECOMING the taste.

Neuroplasticity of joy: Rick Hanson’s research (“Hardwiring Happiness”) shows that deliberately dwelling in positive experiences for 10+ seconds converts them from short-term to long-term neural structure changes. This sutra asks for much more than 10 seconds — it asks for TOTAL permeation — which presumably creates much deeper neural restructuring toward a joyful default state.

One-line essenceWhen joy arises, don’t just feel it — become it. Let it permeate every cell until there is no “you” left, only joy. That joy without a feeler is the taste of the divine.
Technique 90 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: eyes · touch · feather · subtle · heart-opening
Touching eyeballs as a feather, lightness between them opens into heart and there permeates the cosmos.

In Plain English

Close your eyes. Place your palms very gently over your closed eyelids — as light as feathers. Don’t press at all. Just the softest possible contact. Feel the eyeballs receiving this gentleness. The eyes hold tremendous tension (from constant looking, focusing, judging). When touched with absolute gentleness, the eyes relax — and with them the entire nervous system softens. The heart opens because the eyes and heart are connected through the vagus nerve.

How to Practice

  1. Prepare your hands: Rub your palms together vigorously for 10–15 seconds until they feel warm and alive with energy. The friction generates both heat and a subtle electromagnetic charge. Your palms become healing instruments.
  2. Close your eyes: Sit comfortably or lie down. Close your eyes and let them settle for a moment. Don’t force them shut — let the eyelids drop naturally, like curtains falling.
  3. Place palms over eyes: Cup your warm palms gently over your closed eyes. The heels of the palms rest on the cheekbones. The fingers rest on the forehead. The cups of the palms hover over the eyeballs. Almost no pressure — just the lightest possible contact.
  4. The key instruction — FEATHER touch: Osho’s specific word is “feathers.” Imagine your palms ARE feathers. The lightest substance. Barely there. If you are pressing even slightly, you are pressing too much. Less and less pressure until it is almost nothing — just the warmth and the presence of the hands.
  5. Feel the eyes receiving: Notice what happens to your eyeballs under this gentleness. They begin to relax. The muscles behind the eyes (which are constantly tense from focusing) begin to soften. The eyes may feel like they are floating, sinking backward into the skull, becoming liquid.
  6. Notice the subtlety within: As the eyes relax, subtler perceptions arise. You may see colours (violet, gold, blue). You may feel a tingling between the eyebrows. You may feel the eyes “opening inward” rather than outward. This is the “subtlety within” the sutra mentions.
  7. The heart connection: After 3–5 minutes of palming, bring your attention to the heart. You will notice: the heart has softened too. There may be warmth in the chest, a sense of openness or tenderness. This is not imagination — the vagus nerve physically connects the eyes and heart. When the eyes relax, the signal travels directly to the heart.
  8. Stay for 5–10 minutes: Keep the palms over the eyes for the full duration. The relaxation deepens progressively. What begins as eye-relaxation becomes whole-body softening. The nervous system shifts from sympathetic (fight-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-digest-love).
  9. Remove hands slowly: When ready, remove your palms very slowly. Keep eyes closed for another minute. Notice how different the inner space feels — softer, darker, more spacious. When you finally open your eyes, the world may look clearer, more vivid, as if a film has been cleaned away.
  10. Practice before sleep: This technique is particularly powerful at bedtime. The eye-relaxation cascades into whole-body relaxation, and sleep comes quickly and deeply. Many practitioners report this as the single most effective technique for insomnia.

  • Having cold hands — always warm the palms first; cold touch creates tension rather than relaxation
  • Treating it as mechanical rather than tender — approach your own eyes with love, as you would touch a child’s face

Questions From The Book

Why does Osho say the eyes are the key to the heart?

Chapter 56, "Eyes as Feathers"

Osho explains: the eyes are the most tense part of the body because they carry the burden of constant judgment, comparison, and desire. We look at things with hunger, with fear, with analysis — the eyes never rest. All this tension is transmitted through neural pathways directly to the heart (via the vagus nerve). A person with tense, hard eyes inevitably has a tense, closed heart. Relax the eyes deeply, and the heart has no choice but to open — the tension that was keeping it closed dissolves at its source.

Can this technique help with emotional numbness?

Chapter 56

Osho says yes — emotional numbness is often a defence that originates in the eyes. We learn to “not see” painful things by hardening the eyes. But hardened eyes also cannot see beauty, love, or tenderness. When the eye-tension melts through this gentle palming technique, emotions that were frozen begin to thaw. Tears may come — let them. They are the ice melting. Behind the numbness, the heart was always feeling — it was just locked away.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is a vagal toning technique disguised as a meditation. The vagus nerve runs from the brainstem through the face (including the muscles around the eyes) to the heart, lungs, and gut. Gentle pressure on the eyeballs stimulates the oculocardiac reflex, which activates parasympathetic activity — slowing the heart, deepening the breath, and producing feelings of safety and openness.

Polyvagal Theory connection: Stephen Porges’s polyvagal theory explains that the ventral vagal complex (face-heart connection) mediates feelings of social safety and love. By relaxing the eye muscles (part of the “social engagement system”), this technique directly activates the neurological substrate of love and connection. The ancient sutra knew what modern neuroscience only recently confirmed.

Technique 98 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: heart-space · armpits · peace · pervading · centering
In an easy position, gradually pervade an area between the armpits into great peace.

In Plain English

Sit easily. Bring attention to the space between the armpits — the chest cavity, the heart-space. This is not the physical heart on the left side; it is the centre of the chest, the energetic heart. Feel that space. Let awareness gradually FILL that space like warm light filling a room. As you pervade it, peace arises naturally. The heart-space is the seat of peace — not the head (which is the seat of thought). Rest attention here and peace emerges on its own.

How to Practice

  1. Find an easy position: The sutra emphasises “easy.” Don’t strain. Sit in a chair, on cushions, or recline slightly — whatever allows your body to be completely comfortable. The body should be so relaxed that you forget it within minutes.
  2. Locate the heart-space: Bring your attention to the centre of the chest — the area between the armpits. This is a physical space: the chest cavity, the ribcage, the area behind the breastbone. Feel it as a space, a cavity, a room inside your body.
  3. Feel the space without visualising: Osho is specific: don’t visualise anything. Don’t imagine light or colour. Simply FEEL the space. Feel its dimensions. Feel the air in your lungs. Feel the aliveness in the chest. Pure feeling, no imagery.
  4. Gradually pervade it: Let your awareness expand gently to fill the entire chest cavity. Like warmth spreading through a room on a cold day. Gradual, natural, effortless. You are not pushing awareness in — you are letting it spread on its own. “Gradually” is key — don’t force.
  5. Notice peace arising: As awareness fills the heart-space, peace will arise spontaneously. You don’t need to create peace — it is the natural quality of the heart-space when attention is there. The head produces thoughts; the heart produces peace. Simply by being there, peace comes.
  6. Don’t chase the peace: When peace arises, don’t grab it or try to intensify it. That moves you to the head (the grabber). Simply continue pervading the space. Let peace deepen on its own without interference.
  7. If thoughts arise, return to the space: The mind will pull you into thinking. Each time, gently return to FEELING the chest space. Don’t fight thoughts — just relocate attention from head to heart, from thinking to feeling.
  8. Let the peace expand beyond the chest: After 10–15 minutes, if the peace is strong, let it radiate outward from the chest into the arms, the belly, the head, the whole body. The heart-space becomes the source of peace that fills everything.
  9. Great peace: The sutra says “great peace” — not small peace, not relaxation. Great peace is peace without opposite, peace that cannot be disturbed, peace that remains even when you return to activity. This is what becomes available through sustained practice.
  10. 20 minutes daily: This is a gentle technique. Practice for 20 minutes morning and/or evening. It can also be done briefly (2–3 minutes) throughout the day whenever you need to return to centre. Place attention in the chest and peace returns immediately.
