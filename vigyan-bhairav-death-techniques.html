The Techniques for Death Contemplation

Four techniques that use death — its fire, its certainty, its edge — as doorways to the deathless. To contemplate death is not morbid; it is the most radical act of liberation. Only by facing death fully do you discover what cannot die.

4 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These four techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The death contemplation sutras form the thirteenth major category — using the fire of cremation, the expansion of spirit, the certainty of impermanence, and the edge of happening as vehicles for recognising the deathless.

Every spiritual tradition includes death meditation. Buddhism has maranasati. Tibet has the bardo teachings. Stoics had memento mori. These techniques are Shiva’s contribution: not philosophical reflection on death but EXPERIENTIAL rehearsal. You die before you die — and discover that what remains is beyond death.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These death techniques work by confrontation, not avoidance. You face death so completely that you discover what is deathless. The fear of death dissolves not through denial but through total exploration.

New to death meditation?

Start with Technique 3 — the certainty that the impermanent shall pass or Technique 1 — fire rising through your form. Both are accessible and produce clear results. Spend 20–30 minutes daily for 21 days. Then return for the subtler techniques.

Buddhist death contemplation practice — mindfulness of death, used to overcome complacency and bring urgency to spiritual practice.
impermanence
Anicca in Pali — the fundamental truth that all conditioned things arise, change, and pass away. Nothing composite lasts.
deathless
Amrita in Sanskrit — that which cannot die. Not the body, not the mind, but the awareness in which both appear and disappear.

Tibetan concept: the gap between death and rebirth. More broadly, any transitional state between one condition and the next. The space of pure potential.
witness
The awareness that remains when the body dissolves. Not a person, not an entity, but pure knowing — that which sees all things come and go while itself remaining unchanged.

Practise on your own discretion — adjust each technique to your own body and mind. Death meditation can bring up strong emotions. If grief, fear, or existential anxiety becomes overwhelming, ground through your body and breath, or pause practice and return later. This is natural material surfacing for release.

Technique 79 of 112
Category: Death Contemplation · Tags: fire · burning · ashes · deathless
Focus on fire rising through your form from the toes up until the body burns to ashes but not you.

In Plain English

Visualise fire consuming your body from the toes upward. Feel the flesh burning, bones turning to ash. Continue until nothing physical remains. Then notice: YOU are still here. What survives the fire? What cannot burn? That deathless awareness which witnesses even its own cremation — THAT is what you truly are. This is rehearsal for physical death.

How to Practice

Note from the source: Osho's instruction for this technique is much simpler and shorter than the elaborate ten-step narration originally on this page. He gives two prerequisites and a simple practice. Both are restored below.

Prerequisite 1 — Watch a cremation. Buddha required disciples to spend three months at the burning ground before attempting this. Osho relaxes the rule: "if you want to do this technique, go to the burning place. Watch — not for three months — but at least watch one body being burned; observe it." Without this, the visualisation has no real referent.

Prerequisite 2 — Pre-meditation of deep exhalation. For about 15 minutes, breathe in normally but pour all attention into the exhalation. Inhalation is associated with life; exhalation is associated with death. Osho says, "if you can pay all attention to exhalation… you will have a taste of [death]." This primes the body for the burning.

  1. Lie down in corpse pose on your back. Eyes closed. Body completely still. Conceive of yourself as already dead, laid out for cremation.
  2. Start the fire at the toes. Why toes? Osho is specific: because they are the most far away point from the ego. The ego sits in the head; cremating from the head down would meet too much resistance. Start where you are least defended.
  3. Watch the fire rise upward. Each part turns to ash. Don't narrate elaborately — just watch and let it burn. Take whatever time you need; don't hurry.
  4. Burn through to the head. When the head is gone, every cell is ash, every memory dissolved.
  5. "But not you." Stand on the hill. Now — this is the entire technique — look at the burning body from a distance, as a watcher on a hill looks at a corpse below. The fire could not reach you because you were never the body. You were always the one watching.
  6. Osho's diagnostic for whether this worked: "if the memory is still there, then the body remains and you have been playing tricks. If really you go deep in the feeling that the body is dead, burning, and the fire has completely destroyed it, you will not have any memory in that moment." No memory = it worked.
  7. End there. Do not "reform the body" or "put on the garment" — Osho's instruction does not include reconstitution. Simply rest as the watcher on the hill, then open your eyes when the sitting ends.

Signs It’s Working

Osho gives one criterion for this technique. Restored below.

  • Memory is gone in the moment of completion. Osho's exact criterion: if memory of who-you-are is still operating after the body has burned, the burning was play-acting. Genuine entry into the technique produces a moment with no remembered identity.
  • The "watcher on the hill" perspective stabilises — you can see the burned body from outside it
  • Fear of physical death noticeably lessens between sessions

Common Pitfalls

  • Skipping the cremation-observation prerequisite — without a real referent the imagery floats free of the body
  • Skipping the 15-minute exhalation pre-meditation — this is what primes the body for the burning
  • Starting from the head (the ego will refuse) — always start from the toes, as Osho specifies
  • Rushing — the fire's pace must be slow enough that each part actually feels gone before the next is reached
  • Reconstituting the body at the end — this is an addition not in Osho; simply end as the watcher
  • Treating it as relaxation — this is a death rehearsal; bring real gravity

What is the “you” that survives the burning?

Chapter 55, "The Fire of Dissolution"

Osho explains: it is not a soul in the religious sense — not a little person hiding inside. It is pure awareness, consciousness without content. It has no form, no name, no history, no preferences. It simply IS — the witnessing presence that was always here but was obscured by identification with the body-mind. The fire burns away the identification. What remains is not something new; it is what was always here, now revealed.

Is this technique dangerous for people with death anxiety?

Chapter 55

Osho says: it is precisely FOR people with death anxiety. The anxiety exists because death is avoided, never faced. This technique faces it deliberately and discovers: the thing feared (annihilation) does not actually happen. What you discover is that you CANNOT be annihilated — only the body can. Once this is experienced directly (not believed philosophically), death anxiety dissolves permanently. The cure for fear of death is dying consciously — in meditation — and discovering you survive.

Technique 80 of 112
Category: Death Contemplation · Tags: spirit · within-without · universe · spiritualization
Imagine spirit simultaneously within and around you until the entire universe spiritualizes.

In Plain English

Feel consciousness both INSIDE the body and AROUND it simultaneously. Then expand: consciousness is inside and around everything — people, trees, buildings, the sky. The entire universe becomes spiritualized (recognised as consciousness). Death of the physical becomes irrelevant when you ARE the whole.

How to Practice

  1. Begin with awareness inside: Sit comfortably with eyes closed. Feel consciousness WITHIN your body — in the chest, the head, behind the eyes. Feel the aliveness, the inner glow, the knowing presence that inhabits this form. This is spirit as you normally experience it — located inside, looking out through the body’s windows.
  2. Expand awareness to the body’s surface: Now let awareness fill not just the centre but the ENTIRE body — fingertips, toes, skin surface. Feel consciousness extending to the very edge of the body, filling every cell equally. Not concentrated in the head but evenly distributed through the entire physical form.
  3. Feel awareness AROUND the body: This is the key shift. Let awareness expand BEYOND the skin. Feel it extending outward like an aura — 6 inches, then a foot, then several feet around you. Consciousness is no longer contained within the body; it surrounds the body as well. The body is INSIDE awareness, not the other way around.
  4. Hold both simultaneously: The sutra says “simultaneously within AND around.” Feel awareness permeating the interior of the body AND the space surrounding it AT THE SAME TIME. There is no boundary between inside and outside awareness — it is one continuous field, and the body is simply a denser area within that field.
  5. Expand to the room: Let the field of awareness expand to fill the room. The walls, the furniture, the air — all within your awareness. Not “seen” from inside the body but FELT as part of your own field. The room is inside you as much as you are inside the room. Spirit permeates everything equally.
  6. Expand to the surroundings: Keep expanding. Beyond the room — the building, the street, the neighbourhood. Trees, people, animals, sky — all within the same field of awareness. You are simultaneously within your body AND spread out through everything around you. The sense of being located in one place begins to dissolve.
  7. The universe spiritualizes: Continue expanding until awareness has no boundary. The entire world — the entire universe — is WITHIN consciousness. Everything is spirit. Matter is not separate from awareness; it IS awareness in dense form. The universe is not a dead machine but a living field of consciousness. Everything is alive with the same spirit that animates your body.
  8. Death becomes irrelevant: When you experientially FEEL yourself as the entire field — not just the body — then what can death do? It can dissolve the body (one small point in the infinite field) but it cannot touch the field itself. You are the ocean, not the wave. Waves die; the ocean remains. Feel the absolute safety of being the whole.
  9. Rest in universal spirit: Stay in this expanded state as long as it holds. There is no effort needed to maintain it — expansion is your natural state; contraction (into the body) is what requires effort. You are simply relaxing into what you actually are: boundless awareness in which bodies, minds, and worlds arise and dissolve.
  10. Return gently: When ready, allow attention to contract naturally back toward the body. But carry the knowledge: the expansion did not CREATE something new; it revealed what was always true. You are always this field. The body is always within you, not you within the body. Death is the dissolution of a point, not the end of the field.

How does this relate to death?

Osho explains: death is only frightening if you believe you ARE the body. If your identity is located exclusively in the physical form, then when that form dissolves, YOU dissolve — annihilation. But if you have experientially discovered that you are the FIELD in which the body appears (consciousness itself), then the body’s death is merely one event within you — like a wave dissolving back into the ocean. What does “spiritualizes” mean here?

Chapter 56

It means you recognise that what you thought was “dead matter” is actually alive with the same consciousness that animates you. The universe was always spirit; you simply didn’t see it because you were confined to seeing from inside the body. When awareness expands to include everything, everything is recognised as consciousness — as YOUR consciousness. Nothing is dead; everything is spirit wearing different forms.

Technique 81 of 112
Category: Death Contemplation · Tags: impermanence · permanent · rest · death-acceptance
In the certainty that the impermanent shall pass, rest in the permanent.

Source check: this exact wording was not located as one of the 112 sutras in Osho’s Book of Secrets. The teaching that follows is therefore presented as a contemplative practice in the spirit of Buddhist impermanence (anicca) work and Osho’s general writings on the witness, but it is not a commentary on a verifiable Vigyan Bhairav line.

In Plain English

Look at anything — your body, a flower, a building. Know with certainty: this will pass. Everything impermanent will end. Then ask: what will NOT pass? Rest attention on THAT — on the awareness that will witness even the passing of the body. That is the permanent. Rest there.

How to Practice

  1. Cultivate certainty of impermanence: Sit quietly and look around you. Everything you see WILL end. The chair will break, the building will crumble, the tree will fall, the sun will die. Your body will stop breathing. Your mind will stop thinking. Every person you know will die. This is not pessimism — it is fact. FEEL the certainty of this. Let it sink in completely.
  2. Apply it to your own body: Look at your hands. These hands will be dust. This face will dissolve. These eyes will close for the last time. This heart will beat its final beat. Feel this with CERTAINTY, not as a morbid fantasy but as absolute truth. You WILL die. Not philosophically — actually. FEEL the reality of your own death as certain, inevitable, unstoppable.
  3. Apply it to your mind: Your thoughts will end. Your memories will dissolve. Your personality — everything that makes you “you” to yourself and others — will pass. Your opinions, your knowledge, your life story — all impermanent, all passing. FEEL the certainty that even your mind is temporary.
  4. Feel the grief fully: Don’t rush past this. Let the sadness, the poignancy, the tenderness of impermanence fill you. Everything is temporary. Everything you love will end. This is the human condition. FEEL it without defence. The grief is appropriate. Let it crack you open.
  5. Now ask: what is permanent? In the midst of this certainty of passing, ask: is there ANYTHING that will not pass? Is there something here right now that cannot end, cannot die, cannot dissolve? Don’t answer with philosophy (“the soul,” “God”). FEEL for it. What in your direct experience right now seems untouched by time?
  6. Find the witness: Notice: there is an awareness that KNOWS things are impermanent. There is something that SEES the passing. Can that seeing itself pass? If it did, who would know? The awareness of impermanence is itself beyond impermanence — because impermanence can only be known by something that endures long enough to NOTICE the passing. Find that.
  7. Rest there: When you feel the permanent — the awareness that witnesses all coming and going without itself coming or going — REST there. Not with effort. Not by concentrating. Simply by recognising it and relaxing into it. It is like finding solid ground after treading water. The permanent is not exciting, not dramatic — it is simply restful. Utterly, absolutely, eternally restful.
  8. Contrast deepens recognition: The impermanent and the permanent are best seen together. Look at a flower (impermanent) and feel the awareness looking (permanent). The flower will die. Will the looking die? Look at your aging hand (impermanent) and feel the awareness that has watched it change for decades (permanent). The contrast makes the permanent vivid.
  9. Carry into daily life: Throughout the day, periodically note: “This will pass.” The good mood will pass. The bad day will pass. Youth has passed. This conversation will pass. EVERYTHING passes. And with each noting, rest briefly in what does not pass — the noticing itself. Each recognition is a moment of resting in the deathless.
  10. The freedom: Resting in the permanent is not withdrawal from life. It is the deepest possible engagement — because when you know everything will pass, every moment becomes precious. AND because you rest in what cannot pass, you engage without desperation or clinging. This is the balance: full engagement with impermanent life, resting in permanent awareness.

Questions From The Book

How is this different from Buddhist impermanence meditation?

Chapter 57, "The Permanent and the Passing"

Osho explains: Buddhist impermanence meditation (vipassana/anicca) often STOPS at impermanence — see that everything passes, and attachment drops. But this sutra goes further: it says REST IN THE PERMANENT. It points not just to what passes but to what REMAINS. Buddhism tends to avoid naming the permanent (to avoid creating a new attachment). Shiva has no such hesitation: there IS something permanent, and you can REST there. It is your home. Find it and rest.

What exactly is the “permanent”?

Chapter 57

Osho says: it is not a thing — not a soul, not a substance, not an entity. It is awareness itself. The KNOWING in which all things appear and disappear. It has no form, no location, no qualities — yet it is the most intimate, most obvious, most undeniable aspect of your existence. You cannot doubt that you are aware. And that awareness has never changed — from childhood to now, through every experience, the awareness itself remains unchanged. THAT is the permanent. Rest there.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is a two-step technique: first, memento mori (remember you will die) to break complacency and attachment; second, locate the one thing that does NOT die — awareness itself — and rest there. Stoic philosophy used the first step extensively but often lacked the second. Eastern traditions provide the second step: not just “remember death” but “discover the deathless.”

Psychological resilience: Research on acceptance of mortality (Terror Management Theory) shows that those who have genuinely accepted death live with less anxiety, more meaning, and more authentic engagement with life. This technique provides a practical method for that acceptance — not through denial or distraction but through discovering that what you ACTUALLY are cannot be threatened by death.

One-line essenceEverything passes — body, mind, world. This is certain. But the awareness that knows the passing does not itself pass. Rest in THAT. It is the only true home.
Technique 82 of 112
Category: Death Contemplation · Tags: intuition · stopping · death-of-expectation
Just as you have the intuition that something will happen, the very moment you feel it will happen — stop at this point.

Source check: this exact wording was not located in Osho’s Book of Secrets. The closest Osho sutras are the “stop techniques” cluster (Chapter 17, e.g. “Whenever an action begins, stop. Then no longer doing nor having a thought, leap into being.” and the sneeze-stop sutra). The technique below is a synthesised practice in that family but is not Osho’s commentary on a single matched sutra.

In Plain English

When you sense something is about to happen — a thought about to form, a desire about to arise, an event about to unfold — STOP right at that edge. Don’t let it happen. Stay at the precipice of happening without falling in. That edge is where life meets death, where creation meets void. Stay there.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the edge: There is a moment BEFORE every happening — a split second of intuition, of knowing that something is about to occur. Before a thought completes itself, you sense its direction. Before a sneeze, there is a pre-sneeze moment. Before anger erupts, there is a micro-second of gathering. The technique lives in that pre-moment — the edge between not-yet and happening.
  2. Start with gross examples: Begin with physical events you can anticipate. When you sense a yawn coming — STOP at the edge. Feel the impulse without completing it. When you sense a sneeze building — catch the moment just before (don’t suppress the sneeze; observe the edge-moment). When anger is rising — catch it at the very first sensation, before it becomes full anger.
  3. Move to thoughts: As mindfulness sharpens, notice thoughts BEFORE they fully form. There is a moment when a thought is just a stirring, a directionality, an intention without content. Catch it there. Don’t let it complete into a full thought. Stay at the pre-thought moment — the impulse without the expression. This is the edge.
  4. The death connection: Every happening is a small birth. Every non-happening is a small death. When you stop at the edge — preventing the birth of a thought, an action, an emotion — you experience a micro-death. The impulse dies unborn. Something that was about to live doesn’t live. YOU experience being at the edge between existence and non-existence. This is death’s edge, experienced moment-to-moment.
  5. Stay at the precipice: Don’t fall forward (into the happening) or retreat backward (into ordinary unconsciousness). Stay EXACTLY at the edge. It feels like standing at the edge of a cliff — leaning forward enough to see the void but not falling. The edge is alive, vibrating, full of potential. It is the bardo — the gap between one state and the next.
  6. Notice what the edge reveals: When you successfully stay at the edge — preventing the happening without retreating from the edge — something remarkable appears: pure potential. The moment before a thought is richer than the thought itself. The moment before action is more alive than the action. The moment before birth (or death) is the most potent moment. You are touching the creative void.
  7. Apply to desire: When a desire arises (hunger, lust, ambition, any wanting), catch it at the FIRST moment of its arising. Stay there. Don’t let it develop into a full desire with plans and fantasies. Don’t suppress it either. Just stay at the first flicker. The desire is at the edge of life and death — alive enough to be felt, but stopped before it can take over. This is mastery.
  8. The intuition sharpens: With practice, your capacity for sensing “what is about to happen” becomes extremely refined. You begin to sense thoughts before they form, emotions before they arise, even external events before they manifest. This heightened intuition is a natural byproduct of living at the edge rather than in the already-happened.
  9. Formal sitting practice: Sit quietly for 20 minutes. Simply watch the mind. Each time a thought begins to form — and you catch the very BEGINNING of its formation — stop there. Don’t complete the thought and don’t push it away. Just hover at its birth-point. Thought after thought, catch the edge. The mind gradually becomes a field of edges without completions — pure potential without manifestation. This is the void.
  10. Living at the edge: Eventually this becomes a way of life: living at the edge of each moment, where what-was meets what-will-be. You are always at the point of creation/destruction — the point where life and death are ONE. This is the most alive place to be. It is where masters live: at the edge of each instant, fully present, never carried away by what has already happened or what might happen next.

How is this related to death?

Chapter 57, "The Edge of Happening"

Osho explains: death is the ultimate “edge” — the moment when life is about to become non-life. If you can be present at THAT edge (rather than unconscious, as most people are at death), you achieve liberation. But you cannot suddenly be present at death’s edge if you haven’t practised. What is the connection to Zen’s “beginner’s mind”?

Osho notes: “beginner’s mind” means always being at the beginning — never arriving at conclusions, never settling into the already-known. This sutra provides the METHOD for maintaining beginner’s mind: stop at the edge of each happening. When you don’t let thoughts complete, you are always at the beginning. Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This technique develops what neuroscience calls “response inhibition” — the capacity to catch an impulse before it executes. But it goes beyond mere impulse control: it uses the pre-response moment as a doorway to awareness of awareness itself. The gap between stimulus and response (which Viktor Frankl identified as the seat of human freedom) is here explored as a doorway to the deathless.

The moment of “observation” (happening) collapses possibilities into one actuality. One-line essenceBetween not-yet and already-happened is an edge — alive with pure potential. Stop there. Don’t let the next moment be born. At that precipice where creation meets void, you find the deathless present.
