The Techniques for Self-Inquiry

Six techniques that use negation, expansion, and the turning of awareness back upon itself to dissolve false identifications and reveal what you truly are. These are the “Who am I?” methods — stripping away everything you are NOT until only the unnameable remains.

6 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These six techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The self-inquiry sutras form the sixth major category — using negation, expansion beyond the body, and the “subjective mood” to discover awareness prior to all content.

These techniques are the most philosophical in the tantra — they work not through visualisation or energy but through direct seeing. The method is simple: if you can observe it, you are not it. What remains when all observed objects are negated? That is the subject — that is you.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

These techniques are deceptively simple. The mind will say “I understand” after reading them once. Understanding is not the same as experiencing. Do the practice daily — feeling, not thinking.

New to self-inquiry?

Start with Technique 96 — the heart-space. It is the gentlest entry point, requiring nothing but attention and a comfortable seat. Spend 20 minutes a day for 21 days. Then explore the more demanding negation techniques.

Glossary — unfamiliar terms?

Underlined words below are also marked inline; hover or tap them anywhere on the page for a quick definition.

Vigyan Bhairav Tantra
A ~5,000-year-old Sanskrit text presented as a dialogue between Shiva and Devi, listing 112 meditation techniques.
neti-neti
Sanskrit for “not this, not this” — the method of negation. Whatever can be pointed to or described is negated as “not the self,” until only the unnameable subject remains.
self-inquiry
Turning attention toward the source of “I” rather than toward objects. The quintessential question: “Who am I?”
subjective mood
Remaining rooted in awareness rather than lost in objects. Seeing the seer, not just the seen.
no-thingness
Not blankness, not emptiness as a concept, but the living space prior to all things — the container in which objects appear and disappear.
heart-space
The cavity between the armpits — the seat of peace in tantra. Not the physical heart but the energetic centre of stillness.
pervading
Expanding awareness beyond body boundaries in all directions simultaneously, dissolving the sense of being located in one place.
Ramana Maharshi
20th century Indian sage (1879–1950) who taught “Who am I?” as the direct path to self-realisation — the quintessential self-inquiry method.

Practise on your own discretion — adjust each technique to your own body and mind. Self-inquiry can sometimes trigger existential discomfort as familiar identifications dissolve. This is normal and temporary. Ground through the body if needed.

Technique 92 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: identity · omniscience · expansion · belief · divine
Believe omniscient, omnipotent, pervading.

In Plain English

Osho is emphatic: this is NOT positive thinking. It is not affirmation. It is not “fake it till you make it.” It is the stripping away of false limitations. You ARE already omniscient — you just don’t know it because layers of conditioning have convinced you that you are small, limited, separate. This technique asks you to FEEL the truth rather than believe a lie. You are consciousness itself — and consciousness has no boundaries.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in a comfortable position: Spine erect, eyes closed. Take 5 slow, deep breaths. Let the body settle completely. Don’t begin until the surface agitation has calmed.
  2. Begin with the body boundary: Feel the edges of your body — where skin meets air. Now gently question: “Am I really limited to this body?” Don’t answer intellectually. FEEL the question. Let the boundary soften.
  3. Expand to the room: Feel yourself pervading the room you are in. Not imagining — FEELING. You are the space that contains the furniture, the walls, the air. The body is still there but you are no longer confined to it. You pervade the room like warmth pervades a heated space.
  4. Expand to the building, the street: Let the feeling of pervading grow outward. You include the building, the street, the neighbourhood. Don’t think it — feel the expansion as a loosening, a relief, a dropping of tension.
  5. Expand to the city: You pervade the entire city. Millions of people, thousands of buildings — all within you, not outside you. Feel the vastness. Notice: the bigger you become, the more peaceful you feel.
  6. Expand to the earth: You pervade the planet. Oceans, mountains, forests, deserts — all arising within your awareness. The earth is not beneath you; it is within you.
  7. Expand to the cosmos: Stars, galaxies, infinite space — all within you. You are omnipresent. There is nowhere you are not. Feel the absolute freedom of having no boundary. This is not imagination; this is removing the imagined boundary that never actually existed.
  8. Rest as the pervading: Don’t hold the expansion with effort. Simply rest as what you are when you stop pretending to be small. Omniscient means all-knowing — in this expanded state, you don’t need to seek knowledge because you ARE the space in which all knowledge arises.
  9. Feel omnipotence as relaxation: Omnipotent does not mean forcing things. It means there is nothing opposing you because there is nothing outside you. Feel the absence of opposition. That is true power — the power of wholeness.
  10. Return slowly: After 20–30 minutes, gently bring attention back to the body. But carry the flavour of expansion. Walk through your day knowing the boundary is optional, not mandatory. Practise daily for 21 days.

  • Giving up after a few days because “nothing is happening” — 21 days minimum
  • Trying too hard — expansion happens by letting go of contraction, not by forcing

Questions From The Book

Isn’t this just self-delusion? How can I be omniscient?

Chapter 63, "Start With Believing"

Osho explains: you already ARE omniscient — consciousness is infinite by nature. The delusion is not in believing you are vast; the delusion is in believing you are small. Your smallness is the learned behaviour, the conditioning, the false belief. This technique simply removes the false. It does not add anything new. A wave that believes it is separate from the ocean is deluded; a wave that knows itself as ocean is simply seeing the truth.

What is the difference between this and megalomania?

Chapter 63

Osho is clear: the megalomaniac says “I am the greatest” — that is the ego inflating itself. This technique says “there is no separate I” — the ego dissolves into the whole. The megalomaniac becomes bigger; in this technique, the “me” disappears into omnipresence. One is ego; the other is the death of ego. You can tell the difference by the feeling: ego-inflation creates tension and defensiveness; true expansion creates peace and humility.

Modern Commentary

What this is really pointing at: This technique is remarkably similar to what non-dual teachers call “recognising your true nature.” The instruction is not to become something new but to stop excluding. Awareness already includes everything — it is the mind that draws artificial boundaries.

Neuroscience parallel: The Default Mode Network (DMN) creates the sense of a bounded self. Meditation practices that dissolve self-referential processing (as measured by decreased DMN activity) are correlated with experiences of boundlessness, unity, and expanded identity — exactly what this technique describes.

One-line essenceYou are not becoming omniscient — you are dropping the pretence of being limited. What remains when all false boundaries are removed is what you have always been.
Technique 93 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: nothing · void · emptiness · negation · dissolution
Thinking no thing will limited-self unlimit.

In Plain English

This is a paradoxical instruction — closer to a Zen koan than a meditation. How can you “think” no thing? The very effort breaks the mind. Try to think no-thing and thinking exhausts itself trying. When thought has no object, when nothing remains for thought to hold, the “limited self” (which is built out of thoughts and the things they cling to) loses its boundaries and becomes unlimited. Osho stresses this is not blanking the mind by force, nor concentrating on the word “nothing.” It is using the impossibility of the instruction to dissolve the very act of thinking.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in total stillness: Find a position you can hold without moving for 30 minutes. Eyes closed. Spine erect. Let the body become so still that you forget it. Five minutes of settling.
  2. Notice that thoughts are things: A thought arises — it is a THING. It has shape, duration, content. Recognise it as a thing. Don’t fight it, don’t suppress it — simply see that it is a thing. Then let it go. It dissolves on its own when you stop feeding it attention.
  3. Notice that sensations are things: An itch, a pressure, warmth, tingling — these are all things. Recognise each one as a thing. You are not these things. Let them be without identifying with them.
  4. Notice that images are things: A mental picture, a memory, a fantasy — these are things. They arise in you but they are not you. Let each one pass like a cloud through empty sky.
  5. Notice that emotions are things: Boredom, restlessness, peace, bliss — even pleasant states are things. Don’t cling to pleasant things or push away unpleasant ones. They are all things. Let them all go.
  6. Notice that blankness is a thing: This is the crucial trap. The mind may produce a state of blankness and say “This is it! This is no-thingness!” But blankness has a quality, a texture, a dullness — it too is a thing. Let it go.
  7. Rest as the space: When all things have been released, what remains? Not nothing — YOU remain. But not “you” as a thing. You as the SPACE in which things appear. This space is alive, aware, present — but it has no form, no boundary, no content. It is no-thingness.
  8. Don’t grab the experience: The moment you think “I’ve got it!” that thought is a thing and you’ve lost it. No-thingness cannot be possessed because the possessor is also a thing. Simply rest. Don’t claim. Don’t name.
  9. FEEL rather than think: Osho emphasises — this technique fails completely if you stay intellectual. The mind cannot understand no-thingness because the mind IS things. You must feel your way into the space between thoughts, and then let even the feeler dissolve.
  10. Practice for 30 minutes daily: This technique may take months or years to bear fruit. That is normal for something this advanced. Even brief glimpses of no-thingness — even half a second — are transformative. Don’t measure progress.

Questions From The Book

How is this different from sleep or unconsciousness?

Chapter 57 — You Are Everywhere

Osho explains: in sleep, awareness is absent. In the state this technique points to, awareness is fully present but there is no object for thought to hold. Sleep is unconscious; this technique is conscious. The difference is total. In sleep you disappear. Here, things disappear but YOU remain — and the “limited self,” which was constructed out of thoughts and the things they cling to, becomes unlimited. This is the highest state possible: pure awareness without any content.

If I let go of everything, won’t I go mad?

Chapter 64

Osho says: madness comes from too many things, not from no things. The mad person is drowning in objects — thoughts they cannot stop, emotions they cannot control. No-thingness is the opposite of madness — it is supreme sanity. When all clutter is removed, what remains is pristine clarity. You won’t go mad; you’ll become sane for the first time.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is pure awareness meditation — what Tibetans call rigpa, what Zen calls shikantaza (just sitting), what Advaita calls the natural state. It is the recognition that you are not the contents of experience but the space in which experience arises. Every wisdom tradition converges on this recognition as the pinnacle of practice.

Cognitive science parallel: Research on “content-free awareness” (experienced meditators reporting awareness without any identifiable content) suggests this state is neurologically distinct from both waking cognition and sleep. It correlates with global decreases in cortical signal complexity while maintaining consciousness — a paradox that fascinates researchers.

One-line essenceLet go of every thing that arises until only the space remains. That space — alive, aware, borderless — is what you are before you became a thing.
Technique 94 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: subjective · not-lost · witness · enlightened · mood
The appreciation of objects and subjects is the same for an enlightened as for an unenlightened person. The former has one greatness: he remains in the subjective mood, not lost in things.

In Plain English

The enlightened person sees the same world as you — same trees, same people, same problems, same coffee, same traffic. The difference? They never LEAVE themselves. They remain in the subjective mood — rooted in “I am” while experiencing everything. You get lost in the object; they stay rooted in the subject. The tree is the same tree, but they see the tree AND remain as the seer simultaneously.

How to Practice

  1. Begin during a simple activity: Choose something ordinary — drinking tea, walking, looking out a window. Don’t start with complex or emotional situations.
  2. Notice the object: See the tree, taste the tea, feel the wind. Let the experience be full, vivid, complete. Don’t suppress experience — this is not about withdrawal from the world.
  3. Simultaneously feel the subject: While seeing the tree, feel the SEER. While tasting tea, feel the one who tastes. This is the key move — not either/or but BOTH. Object and subject together. The tree AND the awareness that knows the tree.
  4. Keep one foot in “I am”: As you engage with any experience, maintain a background feeling of “I am here, I am aware, I am the one experiencing this.” Not as a thought but as a FEELING — a sense of presence, of being, that never leaves even as objects change.
  5. Notice when you get lost: You WILL get lost in objects — absorbed in a conversation, caught by a thought, swept into an emotion. The moment you notice you were lost, you are found again. Don’t judge the lapse. Simply return to the subjective mood.
  6. Practise with increasingly complex situations: Start with simple sensory experiences. After a week, try during conversations. After two weeks, try during strong emotions. The ultimate test: can you remain in the subjective mood during anger, fear, or desire?
  7. Feel the difference: When you are lost in an object, there is tension, grasping, reactivity. When you remain in the subjective mood, there is ease, spaciousness, freedom. The same experience feels completely different from these two positions.
  8. The entire day is practice: Unlike other techniques that happen on the cushion, this one is for LIFE. Every moment is an opportunity to remain in the subjective mood. Eating, walking, talking, working — all can be done while remaining rooted in “I am.”
  9. Evening review: Each evening, reflect: “How often today was I lost in objects? How often did I remain as the subject?” Don’t judge — simply notice. The ratio will naturally shift over 21 days.
  10. The ultimate insight: Over time you discover: you never actually LEAVE the subject. You only THINK you leave. Awareness is always there; you just overlook it in favour of objects. The practice is not creating something new but recognising what is always already the case.

Questions From The Book

If the enlightened person sees the same world, what is actually different?

Chapter 64, "Remain in the Subjective"

Osho explains: the difference is not in WHAT is seen but in WHERE you are located while seeing. The unenlightened person is located in objects — they become the anger, they become the desire, they become the thought. The enlightened person is located in awareness — anger arises in them but they are not the anger. It is like the difference between being on a boat in a storm versus watching the storm from the shore. The storm is the same; the experience is utterly different.

Can this really be practised all day, or is that idealistic?

Chapter 64

Osho says: in the beginning, you will remember only a few times per day. That is enough. Each remembrance strengthens the muscle. After weeks, the gaps between remembrances become shorter. After months, the subjective mood becomes near-continuous. It is not idealistic — it is the natural state once the habit of getting lost is weakened. You have been practising getting lost for your entire life. Now you are practising remaining. Both are habits; one leads to suffering, the other to freedom.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is virtually identical to the practice of “self-remembering” taught by Gurdjieff, the “I am” practice taught by Nisargadatta Maharaj, and open awareness meditation in the Tibetan tradition. It is the recognition that awareness is always present as the background of all experience — we simply overlook it because we are hypnotised by content.

Attention research: Cognitive psychology distinguishes between “focal attention” (absorbed in an object) and “peripheral awareness” (the field in which objects appear). This technique cultivates peripheral awareness as primary identity — you ARE the field, not the focus.

One-line essenceSee the world exactly as it is — but never leave yourself while doing so. The enlightened person’s only secret: they never forget who is seeing.
Technique 95 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: illusion · neti-neti · negation · stripping · truth
Illusions deceive, colors circumscribe, even divisibles are indivisible.

In Plain English

This is the neti-neti (not this, not this) approach. Everything you can perceive, name, or point to is NOT you. Body — not me. Thoughts — not me. Emotions — not me. Name — not me. Past — not me. Roles — not me. Keep negating until nothing remains that you can point to as “me.” What remains when all identifications are stripped? That unnameable remainder — that which cannot be made into an object — is you.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably, eyes closed: Settle the body. Take 5 slow breaths. Bring your attention inward. You are about to systematically strip away everything false until only the true remains.
  2. Negate the body: Feel the body. Now say (feel, not just think): “This body is not me. I can observe it; therefore I am not it. It changes, grows old, gets sick — but I remain the same one who observes all these changes. Not this.”
  3. Negate thoughts: Watch thoughts arising. Say: “These thoughts are not me. They come and go, change content moment to moment, but I remain as the one watching them. Thoughts are things that happen in me, but they are not me. Not this.”
  4. Negate emotions: Feel whatever emotion is present — boredom, curiosity, peace, agitation. Say: “This emotion is not me. It arose, it will pass, but I will still be here when it’s gone. Not this.”
  5. Negate your name: Feel into the sense of your name, your personal history, your story. Say: “This name is not me. It was given to me. Before the name, I existed. The name is a label on something that has no label. Not this.”
  6. Negate your past: Feel the weight of memories, history, identity built on past events. Say: “My past is not me. It is a collection of memories — thoughts about events that no longer exist. I am here NOW, and the past is just a thought. Not this.”
  7. Negate your future: Feel the pull of hopes, fears, plans, anticipations. Say: “My future is not me. It hasn’t happened. It is imagination. I am only ever NOW. Not this.”
  8. Negate your roles: Father, mother, professional, friend, seeker — feel each role. Say: “These roles are not me. I play them, but when I take off the costume, I remain. Not this, not this, not this.”
  9. Rest in what remains: After negating everything that can be negated, something remains. It cannot be named (if you name it, negate the name). It cannot be described (if you describe it, negate the description). It is prior to all words, all concepts, all forms. Simply REST as this. Feel it. Don’t try to understand it — understanding is another thing to negate.
  10. The paradox: What remains cannot be found as an object because it is the SUBJECT. It is the eye that cannot see itself, the knife that cannot cut itself. But it IS. You know you are. That knowing — prior to all content — is the real you. Rest here for as long as possible.

Questions From The Book

If I negate everything, don’t I disappear?

Chapter 65, "Not This, Not This"

Osho explains: you disappear as a THING — yes. But you were never a thing. You only believed you were. What disappears is the false; what remains is the true. And the true cannot disappear because it was never created. It was always here, always present, only covered by layers of identification. Neti-neti removes the covers. What is underneath was never in danger of disappearing.

How is neti-neti related to Ramana Maharshi’s “Who am I?”

Chapter 65

Osho says: they are the same technique approached from different angles. Ramana asks “Who am I?” and every answer that comes is rejected — “not this.” Neti-neti starts with the negation directly. Both arrive at the same place: the unnameable subject that cannot be made into an object. Choose whichever resonates more. Some minds prefer the question; others prefer the negation.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is the via negativa — the negative path found in every mystical tradition. Meister Eckhart’s “God is nothing”, the Buddhist “emptiness of self”, Advaita’s “neti-neti” — all converge on the same insight: the ultimate cannot be described, only indicated by removing everything it is not.

Psychology parallel: Identity theory shows that the self is constructed from identifications — roles, memories, beliefs. When these are systematically released (as in acceptance-based therapies), what remains is psychological flexibility — the ability to respond freshly to each moment rather than through the filter of fixed identity. This technique is radical psychological flexibility.

One-line essenceNegate everything you can point to. What remains — the one doing the pointing, the unnameable subject — was always here, waiting for you to stop looking elsewhere.
Technique 96 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: heart-space · armpits · peace · pervading · centering
In an easy position, gradually pervade an area between the armpits into great peace.

In Plain English

Sit easily. Bring attention to the space between the armpits — the chest cavity, the heart-space. This is not the physical heart on the left side; it is the centre of the chest, the energetic heart. Feel that space. Let awareness gradually FILL that space like warm light filling a room. As you pervade it, peace arises naturally. The heart-space is the seat of peace — not the head (which is the seat of thought). Rest attention here and peace emerges on its own.

How to Practice

  1. Find an easy position: The sutra emphasises “easy.” Don’t strain. Sit in a chair, on cushions, or recline slightly — whatever allows your body to be completely comfortable. The body should be so relaxed that you forget it within minutes.
  2. Locate the heart-space: Bring your attention to the centre of the chest — the area between the armpits. This is a physical space: the chest cavity, the ribcage, the area behind the breastbone. Feel it as a space, a cavity, a room inside your body.
  3. Feel the space without visualising: Osho is specific: don’t visualise anything. Don’t imagine light or colour. Simply FEEL the space. Feel its dimensions. Feel the air in your lungs. Feel the aliveness in the chest. Pure feeling, no imagery.
  4. Gradually pervade it: Let your awareness expand gently to fill the entire chest cavity. Like warmth spreading through a room on a cold day. Gradual, natural, effortless. You are not pushing awareness in — you are letting it spread on its own. “Gradually” is key — don’t force.
  5. Notice peace arising: As awareness fills the heart-space, peace will arise spontaneously. You don’t need to create peace — it is the natural quality of the heart-space when attention is there. The head produces thoughts; the heart produces peace. Simply by being there, peace comes.
  6. Don’t chase the peace: When peace arises, don’t grab it or try to intensify it. That moves you to the head (the grabber). Simply continue pervading the space. Let peace deepen on its own without interference.
  7. If thoughts arise, return to the space: The mind will pull you into thinking. Each time, gently return to FEELING the chest space. Don’t fight thoughts — just relocate attention from head to heart, from thinking to feeling.
  8. Let the peace expand beyond the chest: After 10–15 minutes, if the peace is strong, let it radiate outward from the chest into the arms, the belly, the head, the whole body. The heart-space becomes the source of peace that fills everything.
  9. Great peace: The sutra says “great peace” — not small peace, not relaxation. Great peace is peace without opposite, peace that cannot be disturbed, peace that remains even when you return to activity. This is what becomes available through sustained practice.
  10. 20 minutes daily: This is a gentle technique. Practice for 20 minutes morning and/or evening. It can also be done briefly (2–3 minutes) throughout the day whenever you need to return to centre. Place attention in the chest and peace returns immediately.

Technique 97 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: pervading · directions · expansion · limitless · everywhere
Feel yourself as pervading all directions, far, near.

In Plain English

Close your eyes and feel yourself expanding in all directions simultaneously — north, south, east, west, up, down. Not the body expanding but AWARENESS expanding. You pervade far and near at the same time. There is no distance that is “away” from you because you ARE all directions. Continue expanding until there is no centre and no periphery — just infinite pervading awareness with no location and no limit.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably with eyes closed: Take 5–7 deep breaths to settle. Let the body become completely still. Bring awareness to the centre of your being — wherever that feels like it is. Start from a point.
  2. Feel awareness expanding forward: From the centre, feel awareness moving forward, through the space in front of you, through the wall, into the distance. Not thinking about distance — FEELING expansion in that direction.
  3. Add backward expansion: Simultaneously feel awareness expanding behind you. Forward and backward at the same time. You are not going somewhere — you already ARE in both directions.
  4. Add left and right: Now feel yourself pervading to the left and to the right simultaneously. Four directions at once. The sense of being located in one spot begins to dissolve.
  5. Add up and down: Feel awareness expanding upward through the ceiling, into the sky, into space. Simultaneously downward through the floor, into the earth, into the depths. Six directions at once.
  6. Let the directions merge: Now stop holding them as separate directions. Let the expansion become spherical — ALL directions at once, simultaneously. You are not expanding in six directions; you are expanding in infinite directions. The sphere of your awareness grows without limit.
  7. Far and near become the same: The sutra says “far, near.” Feel that far and near are equally within you. The wall across the room is as much within your awareness as the sensation of your breath. Both are equally HERE because you pervade all distances.
  8. Dissolve the centre: As expansion continues, the centre from which you started also dissolves. There is no longer a “here” from which you expand — there is only everywhere. No centre, no periphery. Just awareness pervading all directions without location.
  9. Rest as the infinite: In this state of limitless pervading, rest. Don’t try to hold it with effort. Simply allow the natural state of awareness — which has no boundary — to be as it is. You are not creating this; you are releasing the contraction that prevented you from knowing it.
  10. Return gently: After 20–25 minutes, allow attention to softly contract back toward the body. Feel the breath. Feel the body’s weight. Open eyes slowly. But carry the flavour: you are not limited to this point. You pervade. The contraction is voluntary, not necessary.
