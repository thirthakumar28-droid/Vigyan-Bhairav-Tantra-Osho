Technique 56 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: illusion · neti-neti · negation · stripping · truth
Illusions deceive, colors circumscribe, even divisibles are indivisible.

In Plain English

This is the neti-neti (not this, not this) approach. Everything you can perceive, name, or point to is NOT you. Body — not me. Thoughts — not me. Emotions — not me. Name — not me. Past — not me. Roles — not me. Keep negating until nothing remains that you can point to as “me.” What remains when all identifications are stripped? That unnameable remainder — that which cannot be made into an object — is you.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably, eyes closed: Settle the body. Take 5 slow breaths. Bring your attention inward. You are about to systematically strip away everything false until only the true remains.
  2. Negate the body: Feel the body. Now say (feel, not just think): “This body is not me. I can observe it; therefore I am not it. It changes, grows old, gets sick — but I remain the same one who observes all these changes. Not this.”
  3. Negate thoughts: Watch thoughts arising. Say: “These thoughts are not me. They come and go, change content moment to moment, but I remain as the one watching them. Thoughts are things that happen in me, but they are not me. Not this.”
  4. Negate emotions: Feel whatever emotion is present — boredom, curiosity, peace, agitation. Say: “This emotion is not me. It arose, it will pass, but I will still be here when it’s gone. Not this.”
  5. Negate your name: Feel into the sense of your name, your personal history, your story. Say: “This name is not me. It was given to me. Before the name, I existed. The name is a label on something that has no label. Not this.”
  6. Negate your past: Feel the weight of memories, history, identity built on past events. Say: “My past is not me. It is a collection of memories — thoughts about events that no longer exist. I am here NOW, and the past is just a thought. Not this.”
  7. Negate your future: Feel the pull of hopes, fears, plans, anticipations. Say: “My future is not me. It hasn’t happened. It is imagination. I am only ever NOW. Not this.”
  8. Negate your roles: Father, mother, professional, friend, seeker — feel each role. Say: “These roles are not me. I play them, but when I take off the costume, I remain. Not this, not this, not this.”
  9. Rest in what remains: After negating everything that can be negated, something remains. It cannot be named (if you name it, negate the name). It cannot be described (if you describe it, negate the description). It is prior to all words, all concepts, all forms. Simply REST as this. Feel it. Don’t try to understand it — understanding is another thing to negate.
  10. The paradox: What remains cannot be found as an object because it is the SUBJECT. It is the eye that cannot see itself, the knife that cannot cut itself. But it IS. You know you are. That knowing — prior to all content — is the real you. Rest here for as long as possible.

Technique 65 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: beyond-purity · non-judgement · tantra · acceptance · freedom
The purity of other teachings is an impurity to us. In reality, know nothing as pure or impure.

In Plain English

Drop ALL categories of pure/impure, good/bad, sacred/profane. Tantra says: these categories are the chains. Nothing is impure and nothing is pure — these are mental overlays on reality. When you drop judging (this food is “bad,” this act is “impure”), a tremendous freedom arises. The energy that was consumed by constant moral categorisation becomes available for awareness.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the context: This is the most radical sutra in the tantra. Every other spiritual tradition divides the world into sacred/profane, pure/impure, allowed/forbidden. Tantra says: THAT division is the real impurity. It splits reality and splits you. This technique heals that split.
  2. One-day experiment: Choose one full day. From morning to night, make ZERO moral judgments. Not about food (“this is healthy/unhealthy”). Not about people (“she is good/he is bad”). Not about yourself (“I was wrong/right”). Not about actions (“this is proper/improper”). Simply observe without the overlays of judgement.
  3. Notice the judge: You will be astonished how CONSTANT the judging is. The mind categorises everything: this is acceptable, that is not. This is clean, that is dirty. This person is worthy, that one is not. The judge runs 24/7 and you have never noticed because it feels like “reality.”
  4. Catch each judgement as it arises: When the mind says “impure” or “wrong” or “bad” — catch it. Don’t fight it. Just notice: “Ah, the judge is speaking again.” And let the judgement go. Return to raw perception without the overlay.
  5. See what remains without judgement: When you look at something “dirty” without the label “dirty” — what do you actually see? Colour, texture, shape. That’s all. The “dirtiness” was added by the mind. Remove it, and reality is simply what it is — neither pure nor impure.
  6. Notice the energy freed: Judging consumes enormous mental energy. Cataloguing, comparing, condemning, approving — it never stops. When it stops (even for an hour), you will feel a surge of free energy, lightness, spaciousness. This is the freedom tantra promises.
  7. Apply to the body: Notice how you judge your own body — this part is beautiful, that part is ugly. This function is “dignified,” that function is “shameful.” Drop all of it. The body is simply the body. No part is impure.
  8. Apply to emotions: Stop labelling emotions as positive or negative. Anger arises — it is not “bad.” Sadness arises — it is not “wrong.” Joy arises — it is not “better.” They are all simply energies moving through you. Without judgement, they flow freely and resolve naturally.
  9. The paradox of morality: This technique does NOT make you immoral. Osho is clear: when judgement drops, a natural morality arises that is far more authentic than the imposed kind. Without the judge, compassion flows naturally because you are no longer separated from others by categories of better/worse.
  10. Extend to one week: After your one-day experiment, extend to a full week. Notice how profoundly your relationship to reality changes when you stop dividing it into acceptable and unacceptable. Reality becomes seamless, whole, sacred in its totality.

Technique 68 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: mothers · chicks · mother · particular
As a hen mothers her chicks, mother particular knowings, particular doings, in reality.

In Plain English

A hen sits on her chicks, warms them, protects them, broods them into life. Do the same with your own, real, first-hand knowings and doings — and let go of everything borrowed. Don’t mother theories, scriptures and other people’s opinions. Mother only what you have actually tasted yourself, however small. The key phrase is “in reality”: live in the present, drop hope-based dreaming, and protect the few experiences that are genuinely yours.

How to Practice

  1. Begin with an honest audit. Sit quietly with paper. List things you “know” — about love, God, meditation, what is right and wrong. Mark which were tasted by you and which were given to you (parents, teachers, books, internet). Be ruthless. Most will be borrowed.
  2. Mark also things you do daily — ways of greeting, of being angry, of working, of expressing love. For each, ask Osho’s exact question: “Is this me, or is my father doing it through me? Is my mother angry, or am I?”
  3. Make a small set of things that survived — a real knowing, a genuine like or dislike, a feeling that is unmistakably your own. They may be tiny. That is fine. Tiny is enough.
  4. Now begin to mother them, like a hen with chicks. Spend time with them. Trust them. Build outward from them. Whatever is borrowed, let drop, even if it leaves a hole.
  5. Live in the present. Osho says hope is the door through which dreaming enters. So for one week, refuse to live for tomorrow. Do not pull yourself forward with “something is going to happen.” If today is hopeless, accept it as hopeless — but stay here.
  6. Watch for the small false acts that fill the day. The painted smile when family arrives. The polite agreement you don’t mean. The respect shown to someone you don’t respect. Stop them, one by one. Don’t replace them with rude opposites — just don’t do the false version.
  7. Save your real smile for when something real happens. Osho is specific: “Don’t smile, preserve the energy. The smile will come, and then it will change you completely. Then every cell of your body will smile.” Same with tears, same with affection.
  8. For seven days, make a single rule: do nothing false. Whatever is lost from your life because you refused to fake it, let it be lost. Osho promises: in seven days a new living current will be felt; the dead layers begin to break.
  9. Expect a gap. The false things will drop before the real ones come. There will be a dull, empty stretch — this is normal, not failure. Don’t panic and don’t fill it with new borrowed things.
  10. Distinguish trust from belief. Don’t set up a new master to follow; that is the same trap. Osho quotes Buddha to Sariputta: “If you don’t believe in yourself, how can you believe in me?” The first trust is always inward.
  11. Don’t imitate. Love a Buddha or a Jesus, but don’t become a carbon copy — carbon copies are not accepted by existence. Whatever route you take, it must be authentically your own.
  12. Continue mothering. Day after day return to your small set of real knowings, warm them, protect them. They grow. The hen does not analyse her chicks; she simply broods them. Do the same with your truth.

Technique 69 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: since · truth · bondage · freedom
Since, in truth, bondage and freedom are relative, these words are only for those terrified with the universe. This universe is a reflection of minds. As you see many suns in water from one sun, so see bondage and liberation.

In Plain English

Bondage and liberation are not opposites — they are two ends of the same stick. The world is a mirror; whatever you see in it — chains or freedom — is your own reflection. The man who runs after money and the man who runs from money are equally bound to money. Tantra says: don’t move to the opposite pole. See that both poles are reflections of one mind, and the seeking itself drops.

How to Practice

  1. This is a contemplation more than a sit-down practice. The sutra warns up front that bondage/freedom talk is “only for those terrified with the universe” — that is, for people who are running. The practice is to see through that running.
  2. Sit and review your spiritual life so far. List what you have been running from (sex, money, anger, the world, the body) and what you have been running toward (brahmacharya, simplicity, peace, liberation, the divine). Lay them in two columns.
  3. Now look at each pair carefully. Osho is sharp here: the saint who shudders at money is still obsessed with money — just in reverse. The man who cannot look at a woman is still obsessed with women — in reverse. Check whether your “spiritual” column is just the negative of your worldly column. In most cases it is.
  4. This is the recognition the sutra wants: bondage and freedom are relative, like hot and cold — degrees of one phenomenon, not different things. If hot water feels cold to a hot hand, the “cold” is in the hand, not the water. Apply the same logic to bondage and liberation.
  5. Stop creating an opposite pole. Don’t resolve to be non-greedy if greed troubles you, don’t resolve to be celibate if sex troubles you, don’t resolve to leave the world if the world feels heavy. Each resolution is just a reversed version of the same obsession.
  6. Instead, look at the one who is desiring. Osho: “Today you desire A, tomorrow B, day after C, and you think you are transformed. But you are the same. The desiring is you.” The objects change; the desire-er does not. Watch the desire-er.
  7. Practise the mirror image. Through the day notice that what disturbs you outside is a reflection of what is inside. The thief sees thieves; the lustful sees lust; the angry sees anger everywhere. When you next find yourself condemning a quality in someone, turn the condemnation around like a lamp shone at yourself: “What in me is reflected here?”
  8. Sit with the example Shiva uses. One sun reflects in many ponds. Counting reflections will give you a thousand suns. Looking up at the source gives you one. Whenever you find yourself listing “the bondages of my life” or “the freedoms I will reach,” remember — reflections, not realities.
  9. Drop the search for liberation. This is the hardest step and Osho is direct about it: desire for liberation is itself bondage, because desire is bondage. You cannot desire your way out of desiring. Stop desiring liberation. The moment desiring drops, what remains is what was always there.
  10. Don’t panic at this. Stopping the search doesn’t mean drifting into worldliness; it means standing still in the middle, neither pulled by sansara nor by moksha. Osho says: when desiring stops, the future stops, and there is nowhere to move. That stopping is the freedom — and you can no longer call it freedom, because there is no longer a bondage to contrast it with.
  11. Use Naropa’s answer when you wonder whether you have “made progress.” Asked if he was now liberated, Naropa said: “Yes and no both. Yes, I am not in bondage; and no, because that liberation was also a reflection of bondage.” Carry this answer; let it dissolve the question.
  12. This technique is for the rare mind, the sutra says. Don’t force it. If your obsession is still strong, simpler techniques will serve you better. Come to this one when seeking has worn you out and you are ready to drop the seeking itself.

Technique 82 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: identification · organs · felt-sense · body · ownership
Feel: My thought, I-ness, internal organs — me.

In Plain English

Feel into your internal organs — heart beating, lungs breathing, belly digesting. Then ask: am I these? Feel into thoughts arising. Am I these? Feel into the sense of “I.” Am I even this? Each layer is investigated and found to be an OBJECT of awareness, not awareness itself. The void opens behind all identifications.

How to Practice

  1. Find a quiet space: Sit comfortably with eyes closed. Settle the body with 5–7 deep breaths. Let the surface mind quiet. Don’t rush into the technique — give yourself 3–5 minutes of simple breathing first.
  2. Feel the internal organs: Bring awareness to your heart — feel it beating. Don’t think about it, FEEL the pulse from inside. Move to your lungs — feel them expanding, contracting. Feel the belly working, digesting. Feel the blood moving through vessels. Spend 5 minutes simply FEELING your internal machinery at work.
  3. Ask the first question: “Am I these organs?” Not intellectually — FEEL the question. The heart beats — but you can observe it beating. If you can observe it, you cannot BE it. The observer is separate from the observed. FEEL this separation. The organs are objects in your awareness.
  4. Move to thoughts: Now notice thoughts arising. Watch them come and go like clouds. Ask: “Am I these thoughts?” Again, FEEL it — if you can watch a thought appear and disappear, you are not the thought. You are the space in which thoughts move. Let this sink in deeply — not as philosophy but as felt experience.
  5. Move to the “I” feeling: This is the subtlest layer. Feel the sense of “I am” — the raw feeling of existing, of being someone. Now ask: “Am I even this?” Can you observe the I-feeling itself? If yes, then even the I-sense is an OBJECT — something witnessed, not the witness.
  6. Rest in what remains: When you have peeled away organs, thoughts, and I-ness — what remains? Don’t answer with words. Don’t conceptualise. Just REST in whatever is left. It will feel like nothing — and that nothing is the void. It is spacious, peaceful, without centre or boundary.
  7. Stay in the void: Remain in this emptiness for as long as it holds naturally. Don’t try to maintain it — if thoughts return, simply notice them as objects again, and the void reopens. Each time you notice an object AS an object, the void behind it becomes visible.
  8. The neti-neti process: This is essentially the ancient Upanishadic method — “not this, not this.” You are not rejecting anything; you are simply seeing clearly that each layer is YOURS but not YOU. The body is yours — not you. Thoughts are yours — not you. Even I-ness is yours — not you.
  9. Return gently: When ready, allow the I-sense to reconstitute naturally. Feel the body again. Open eyes slowly. The insight remains: everything you thought was “you” is actually something you HAVE. What you ARE is the void itself — the empty awareness that has all these things.
  10. Daily integration: Throughout the day, periodically ask yourself: “What am I right now?” and instead of answering, simply feel the question dissolve into spaciousness. Each inquiry is a micro-taste of the void.

Technique 83 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: before-desire · before-knowing · prior-self · dissolution · beauty
Before desire and before knowing, how can I say I am? Consider. Dissolve in the beauty.

In Plain English

Before any desire arose in you, before you knew anything — WHAT WERE YOU? Not your name (that came later), not your body-image (that developed), not your desires (they weren’t born yet). Go back before all of it. What remains? A vast, beautiful emptiness — the void from which you arose. Dissolve into that beauty. This is your original face before your parents were born.

How to Practice

  1. Create sacred space: Sit in a quiet, dimly lit room. Light a candle if it helps establish reverence. This technique requires deep inward attention — eliminate all possible distractions. Allow 30 minutes minimum.
  2. Settle deeply: Close your eyes. Breathe slowly and deeply for 5 minutes. Let the body become very still. Let the mind slow down. You need to reach a contemplative depth before this inquiry will work — surface mind cannot access these layers.
  3. Remember your earliest desire: Go back in memory. What is the earliest desire you can remember? Wanting a toy? Wanting mother’s attention? Feel that desire. Now ask: BEFORE that desire existed, what were you? You existed before the desire arose — something was there that COULD desire. What was it?
  4. Go before knowledge: What is the earliest thing you “knew”? Your name? Language? The feel of warmth? Go before ALL of that. Before you knew anything at all — before language, before concepts, before any framework of understanding existed in you — WHAT WERE YOU?
  5. Feel, don’t think: The mind will try to answer: “I was a baby,” “I was consciousness,” “I was nothing.” Reject ALL answers. This is not a question that CAN be answered by the mind. It is a question designed to short-circuit the mind. Let the question dissolve all answers.
  6. The dissolving: As you feel into this impossible question — what was I before all desire and all knowing? — something begins to dissolve. The constructed self (which is MADE of desires and knowledge) begins to melt. What’s left is not nothing — it’s a vast, beautiful, empty awareness. The sutra says: “dissolve in the beauty of THAT.”
  7. Stay with the beauty: This void is not cold or frightening. The sutra specifically calls it BEAUTIFUL. When you touch it, there is a quality of pristine, innocent beauty — like fresh snow, like a clear dawn, like a newborn’s eyes. Let that beauty hold you. Dissolve INTO it rather than observing it from outside.
  8. The original face: What you are touching is what Zen calls your “original face” — what you were before conditioning, before desire, before knowledge, before identity was constructed. It is not a thing — it is the void that holds all things. And it is YOURS. It is what you have always been, underneath everything else.
  9. Return slowly: When the meditation ends naturally (or after 30 minutes), let desires and knowledge return gradually. Notice them arriving like guests returning to an empty house. The house is not the guests — you are the house. Empty, spacious, welcoming, but not defined by what it contains.
  10. Carry the question: Throughout the day, occasionally ask: “Before this desire, what am I?” Each time, the void flashes for an instant. These micro-glimpses accumulate until the void becomes your constant background.

The Techniques for Self-Inquiry

Six techniques that use negation, expansion, and the turning of awareness back upon itself to dissolve false identifications and reveal what you truly are. These are the “Who am I?” methods — stripping away everything you are NOT until only the unnameable remains.

6 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These six techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The self-inquiry sutras form the sixth major category — using negation, expansion beyond the body, and the “subjective mood” to discover awareness prior to all content.

These techniques are the most philosophical in the tantra — they work not through visualisation or energy but through direct seeing. The method is simple: if you can observe it, you are not it. What remains when all observed objects are negated? That is the subject — that is you.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

These techniques are deceptively simple. The mind will say “I understand” after reading them once. Understanding is not the same as experiencing. Do the practice daily — feeling, not thinking.

New to self-inquiry?

Start with Technique 96 — the heart-space. It is the gentlest entry point, requiring nothing but attention and a comfortable seat. Spend 20 minutes a day for 21 days. Then explore the more demanding negation techniques.

The cavity between the armpits — the seat of peace in tantra. Not the physical heart but the energetic centre of stillness.
pervading
Expanding awareness beyond body boundaries in all directions simultaneously, dissolving the sense of being located in one place.

20th century Indian sage (1879–1950) who taught “Who am I?” as the direct path to self-realisation — the quintessential self-inquiry method.
