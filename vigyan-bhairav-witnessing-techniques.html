Technique 22 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: attention · place · where · seeing
Let attention be at a place where you are seeing some past happening, and even your form, having lost its present characteristics, is transformed.

In Plain English

Replay an event from your past, but watch it as if it happened to someone else. The figure inside the memory is your old form, not you — you are the witness. Osho calls this an unwinding of the mind: by re-running past happenings backwards, without identifying with them, the past loses its grip and the river of forms becomes visible to the one who is not a form.

How to Practice

  1. Lie in bed at night, just before sleep. Close your eyes. This is the easiest entry point Osho recommends, and it doubles as a cure for insomnia.
  2. Do not start at the morning. Start at the very last thing — you, lying here on the bed right now — and move backwards step by step through the day. Osho is emphatic: backwards only. Going forwards from the morning re-emphasises the trap; going backwards unwinds it.
  3. See each event in slow motion, like a film being run in reverse. Don’t hurry. The slower you go, the more detail you recover.
  4. Watch the figure inside the memory — the one being insulted at 3 p.m., the one praised at noon — and remember Osho’s key reminder: that is your form, not you. “You were never there. Always your form is involved; you are never involved.”
  5. If anger rises again as you watch the insult, you are identified and you have missed the point. Don’t fight the anger; just notice the identification, step back, and continue watching as a witness. If praise re-elates you, the same: step back.
  6. Continue all the way back to the first thing you remember on waking that morning. By the time you arrive there, sleep usually takes you on its own. Osho says the sleep that follows is meditative, almost dream-less, “like a very small child.”
  7. In the morning, before opening your eyes, do the same thing in reverse for the night: try to move back into the last fragment of dream, then deeper. This part takes weeks of practice; expect very little at first.
  8. To train the mind for backward movement, Osho suggests counting from 100 down to 1, slowly, once a day. The mind resists, because it is grooved to count up — that resistance is exactly what the technique is loosening.
  9. Once you are at home with one day, take a free day, go somewhere quiet, fast and stay silent. Lie on the ground or under a tree and run the same backwards process across your whole life — year by year, back into childhood, back to the earliest thing you can remember. Osho says you may eventually break through the four-or-five-year-old barrier, even reach the womb.
  10. While doing this, don’t analyse and don’t try to “heal” anything. Awareness alone is the work. Osho: “You are not to do anything, you are just to be aware.” Many psychological complexes simply dissolve as their hidden moment of origin is witnessed.
  11. The transformation the sutra promises happens slowly: you begin to feel that you are like a river of forms, and that the form changes while something behind it does not. Old grievances, old longings, old self-images quietly stop hooking you.
  12. Persist. Osho says the method is “not very difficult, only persistent effort is needed.” Even doing only the night-rewind, every night, is enough to begin.

Osho’s Key Insight

One of the great tantrikas of this age, George Gurdjieff, says that identification is the only sin. The next sutra, the tenth sutra on centering – which we are going to penetrate tonight – is concerned with identification. So first be crystal clear on what identification means.

Technique 24 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: mood · against · someone · arises
When a mood against someone or for someone arises, do not place it on the person in question, but remain centered.

In Plain English

When love, hate, anger or any strong mood arises towards someone, the ordinary mind throws it onto that person and calls them the cause. Tantra says the opposite: you are the source, the other is only a screen. Refuse to project. Use the heat of the mood as a wire that runs back inward to the place it came from, and stay there.

How to Practice

  1. This is a technique for the middle of life, not the cushion. Wait for an actual mood — sudden anger at a colleague, a wave of love for someone, a flash of hate, jealousy, irritation. The hotter the mood, the better the doorway.
  2. The instant you notice the mood, do not let it rush out toward the person. Don’t speak. Don’t act on it. Don’t even rehearse what you will say. The whole technique depends on cutting the projection at its root.
  3. Inwardly thank the person who triggered you. Osho is direct on this: “Someone has given you a chance to be aware of your own anger — thank him immediately and forget him.” They are not the source; they are only the bucket dropped into your well. What came up was already there.
  4. Close your eyes if you can (in a meeting, lower the gaze). Turn your attention 180 degrees: away from the other and back along the line of the mood — toward the place inside you it is coming from.
  5. Don’t suppress the mood and don’t express it. Feel it, hot and alive, but follow it inward. Osho calls the emotion “a hot wire” — while it is hot, it is conductive; you can ride it back to the source far more easily than you can find that source in calm.
  6. As you move inward, you reach a cooler point: the centre from which the anger or love is being thrown out. Stop there. Don’t analyse why — just rest at the source.
  7. Stay centred for as long as the mood lasts. The mood may continue to burn for a while; let it. You are no longer pouring it onto the person. It will exhaust itself in your awareness instead of in their face.
  8. If a thought tries to drag you back outward (“but they really did this…”), recognise it as the projection trying to restart, and bring attention back to the inner source.
  9. Remember Lin Chi’s empty boat. A drifting boat hit his at night and he was furious — until he opened his eyes and saw the boat was empty. There was nowhere to send the anger. He let it float backwards into himself and reached his centre. After that, even when a person came and insulted him he would say, “This boat is also empty,” close his eyes, and go within. Treat every triggering person as an empty boat.
  10. You can also do this with positive moods. When sudden affection or admiration arises for someone, don’t spill it onto them either; trace it back to the source. The same well that throws hate also throws love. Knowing the well is the point.
  11. Practise on small irritations first — a queue, a slow driver, a careless word. The big ones (close relationships) are harder because the urgency leaves no space. Build the muscle on minor moods, then it is available for the major ones.
  12. Over weeks, you will notice a shift: moods still arise, but they no longer reach the screen. They illuminate the projector instead. That is the centring the sutra promises.

Technique 25 of 112
Category: Death Contemplation · Tags: intuition · stopping · death-of-expectation
Just as you have the impulse to do something, stop.

Source check: this exact wording was not located in Osho’s Book of Secrets. The closest Osho sutras are the “stop techniques” cluster (Chapter 17, e.g. “Whenever an action begins, stop. Then no longer doing nor having a thought, leap into being.” and the sneeze-stop sutra). The technique below is a synthesised practice in that family but is not Osho’s commentary on a single matched sutra.

In Plain English

When you sense something is about to happen — a thought about to form, a desire about to arise, an event about to unfold — STOP right at that edge. Don’t let it happen. Stay at the precipice of happening without falling in. That edge is where life meets death, where creation meets void. Stay there.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the edge: There is a moment BEFORE every happening — a split second of intuition, of knowing that something is about to occur. Before a thought completes itself, you sense its direction. Before a sneeze, there is a pre-sneeze moment. Before anger erupts, there is a micro-second of gathering. The technique lives in that pre-moment — the edge between not-yet and happening.
  2. Start with gross examples: Begin with physical events you can anticipate. When you sense a yawn coming — STOP at the edge. Feel the impulse without completing it. When you sense a sneeze building — catch the moment just before (don’t suppress the sneeze; observe the edge-moment). When anger is rising — catch it at the very first sensation, before it becomes full anger.
  3. Move to thoughts: As mindfulness sharpens, notice thoughts BEFORE they fully form. There is a moment when a thought is just a stirring, a directionality, an intention without content. Catch it there. Don’t let it complete into a full thought. Stay at the pre-thought moment — the impulse without the expression. This is the edge.
  4. The death connection: Every happening is a small birth. Every non-happening is a small death. When you stop at the edge — preventing the birth of a thought, an action, an emotion — you experience a micro-death. The impulse dies unborn. Something that was about to live doesn’t live. YOU experience being at the edge between existence and non-existence. This is death’s edge, experienced moment-to-moment.
  5. Stay at the precipice: Don’t fall forward (into the happening) or retreat backward (into ordinary unconsciousness). Stay EXACTLY at the edge. It feels like standing at the edge of a cliff — leaning forward enough to see the void but not falling. The edge is alive, vibrating, full of potential. It is the bardo — the gap between one state and the next.
  6. Notice what the edge reveals: When you successfully stay at the edge — preventing the happening without retreating from the edge — something remarkable appears: pure potential. The moment before a thought is richer than the thought itself. The moment before action is more alive than the action. The moment before birth (or death) is the most potent moment. You are touching the creative void.
  7. Apply to desire: When a desire arises (hunger, lust, ambition, any wanting), catch it at the FIRST moment of its arising. Stay there. Don’t let it develop into a full desire with plans and fantasies. Don’t suppress it either. Just stay at the first flicker. The desire is at the edge of life and death — alive enough to be felt, but stopped before it can take over. This is mastery.
  8. The intuition sharpens: With practice, your capacity for sensing “what is about to happen” becomes extremely refined. You begin to sense thoughts before they form, emotions before they arise, even external events before they manifest. This heightened intuition is a natural byproduct of living at the edge rather than in the already-happened.
  9. Formal sitting practice: Sit quietly for 20 minutes. Simply watch the mind. Each time a thought begins to form — and you catch the very BEGINNING of its formation — stop there. Don’t complete the thought and don’t push it away. Just hover at its birth-point. Thought after thought, catch the edge. The mind gradually becomes a field of edges without completions — pure potential without manifestation. This is the void.
  10. Living at the edge: Eventually this becomes a way of life: living at the edge of each moment, where what-was meets what-will-be. You are always at the point of creation/destruction — the point where life and death are ONE. This is the most alive place to be. It is where masters live: at the edge of each instant, fully present, never carried away by what has already happened or what might happen next.

Technique 26 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: desire · quitting · sudden-stop · gap · freedom
When some desire comes, consider it. Then suddenly, quit it.

In Plain English

A desire arises — to eat, to speak, to move, to check your phone. Consider it fully — feel it, acknowledge it, let it build. Then SUDDENLY quit it. Drop it completely. In the instant of dropping, a gap opens. The desire was momentum; the sudden stop creates a void-moment. In that void: freedom. The technique is not suppression (which is gradual) but sudden abandonment (which is total).

How to Practice

  1. Understand the difference from suppression: Suppression says “don’t have this desire.” This technique says “HAVE the desire fully, then drop it suddenly.” The first creates conflict; the second creates freedom. You need the desire present and felt — then the sudden dropping creates the gap.
  2. Start with small desires: Don’t begin with your biggest addiction. Start tiny: the desire to scratch an itch, to shift position, to check your phone, to take another bite. These micro-desires are perfect training ground.
  3. Phase 1 — CONSIDER: When a desire arises, turn your full attention to it. Feel it in the body. Where does it live? The belly? The throat? The hands? What sensation does it create? Urgency? Pulling? Heat? Don’t resist it. Let it build to its peak intensity. CONSIDER it — feel it completely.
  4. Phase 2 — SUDDENLY QUIT: At the peak of the desire — when it is strongest and most demanding — drop it INSTANTANEOUSLY. Not gradually. Not by talking yourself out of it. Not by replacing it with something else. Simply DROP. Like releasing a held breath. Like opening a clenched fist. One moment desire; next moment nothing.
  5. Phase 3 — NOTICE THE GAP: In the instant of dropping, something extraordinary happens: a vacuum, a void, a moment of absolute freedom. The energy that was going toward the desire has nowhere to go. It pools in pure awareness. THAT moment is the meditation. That gap is the door.
  6. Practice with movement: Start walking across the room with intention. Build momentum. Then STOP. Freeze mid-stride. The body stops, and in the sudden stop, the mind stops too. Feel the gap between the momentum and the stillness.
  7. Practice with speech: You want to say something. Feel the words forming, the desire to speak building. Then — close your mouth. Don’t say it. Not because it’s wrong to say it, but for the experiment. Feel the gap when the impulse to speak is suddenly abandoned.
  8. Practice with reaching: Reach for your phone, your coffee, a book. Let the hand extend. Then STOP before contact. Pull the hand back. Feel the sudden emptiness where the grasping was. That emptiness is awareness.
  9. Evening practice (10 desires): In the evening, sit quietly and recall 10 desires that arose during the day. With each one, mentally re-create the desire, feel it build, then suddenly drop it. Feel the gap each time. Ten gaps per evening, consciously created.
  10. The mastery: Over weeks, you develop the ability to drop ANY desire at ANY moment. Not from suppression but from the understanding that desire is just energy — and when it is dropped, that energy becomes available for awareness. This is not anti-life; it is profoundly pro-freedom.

Oh lotus-eyed one, sweet of touch, when singing, seeing, tasting, be aware you are and discover the ever-living.

In Plain English

While going about any ordinary activity — singing, seeing, tasting, touching — add one extra dimension: be aware that you are. Not “I am Ram” or “I am a teacher” — just the pure, non-verbal feeling of existence. This is not thinking “I exist” but FEELING existence itself. Gurdjieff built his entire school on this single sutra and called it “self-remembering.” When you remember yourself while perceiving the world, you discover the ever-living — the deathless awareness behind all experience.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the distinction: There is a vast difference between THINKING “I am” and FEELING “I am.” Thinking is words in the head. Feeling is a non-verbal vibration in the body — a sense of presence, aliveness, hereness. The technique requires the FEELING, not the thought. If you catch yourself saying “I am” internally, you’ve already missed it.
  2. Start with touch: The sutra says “sweet of touch.” Touch someone’s hand. Feel the warmth, the pressure, the texture. Now — simultaneously — feel the ONE who is feeling. Don’t lose the touch, but add a second arrow of awareness: one arrow pointing outward to the hand you touch, one arrow pointing inward to the one who touches. This is double-arrowed awareness.
  3. Expand to seeing: Look at a flower, a tree, a face. See it fully. Now feel: who is seeing? Not as a question to answer — as a FEELING to feel. You see the flower AND you feel the seer. Two arrows: out and in, simultaneously.
  4. Expand to hearing: Listen to music, birdsong, traffic. Hear it completely. Then feel the hearer. The sound is there AND the awareness of the sound is there. Rest in both simultaneously.
  5. Expand to tasting: Take a bite of food. Taste it fully. Now feel: something is tasting. Something alive is present. That presence IS you — not the tongue, not the flavour, but the awareness in which both appear.
  6. The Gurdjieff experiment: Gurdjieff started 30 students on this practice. After 1 week, 27 fled thinking they were going mad. Why? Because when you first try to feel “I am,” you discover that normally you are NOT — normally there is no one home. The body walks, talks, eats, but there is no aware presence. Discovering this absence is shocking. Stay with the shock.
  7. After 1 month: Glimpses begin. Moments where you FEEL “I am” — non-verbally, unmistakably. These glimpses are brief (seconds) but transformative. Each one is a moment of awakening.
  8. After 2 months: The “I” begins to drop. What started as “I am” becomes simply “am-ness.” The personal pronoun dissolves. There is being, but no person claiming it.
  9. After 3 months: Even “am-ness” dissolves. What remains is pure being without any concept attached — the ever-living that the sutra promises. This is not absence. It is presence so full that it needs no label.
  10. Practice schedule: All day, every day. This is not a 20-minute sitting technique. It is a 24/7 remembrance. Set hourly alarms if needed. Each time the alarm rings, FEEL “I am” for 30 seconds. Over time the gaps between rememberings shrink.
  11. The critical instruction: Don’t make it serious or grim. Osho emphasises: it should be playful, light, a game of hide-and-seek with your own presence. The moment you strain, you’ve lost it. Self-remembering happens in relaxation, not in tension.
  12. Walking practice: Walk under trees. Stop occasionally. Feel: you ARE. The trees are. The sky is. Everything simply IS. And you are the awareness in which this is-ness registers. Stay for a breath or two. Then continue walking, carrying the feeling.

Technique 54 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: satisfaction · positivity · bliss · accumulation · contentment
Wherever satisfaction is found, in whatever act, actualize this.

In Plain English

The mind naturally accumulates negatives — it hoards pain, dissatisfaction, grievances. This technique reverses that habit. Whenever ANY satisfaction arises — however small — STOP and fill yourself with it completely. Forget the cause; dwell in the FEELING of satisfaction itself. Over time, positive experiences accumulate and your entire inner climate shifts from grey to golden.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the mechanism: The mind is negatively biased by evolution. A single insult is remembered for years; a hundred compliments are forgotten in hours. This technique deliberately reverses that bias by training attention to dwell on positive states rather than skipping past them.
  2. Begin noticing satisfaction: Throughout the day, notice every moment of satisfaction — however small. Drinking water when thirsty: satisfaction. The first bite of food when hungry: satisfaction. Sitting down after standing: satisfaction. A cool breeze on a hot day: satisfaction. A friend’s smile: satisfaction. Most of these pass unnoticed. Start noticing them.
  3. Stop and dwell: When satisfaction arises, STOP whatever you are doing — even for 5 seconds. Close your eyes if possible. Let the satisfaction FILL you. Don’t think about what caused it. Don’t analyse it. Just FEEL it spreading through the body like warmth.
  4. Forget the cause: This is Osho’s key instruction. Normally we attribute satisfaction to the object — “the water is refreshing” or “the friend is nice.” But the satisfaction is not IN the water or the friend. It is in YOU. It arises from within. When you stop attributing it to external causes, you discover: you ARE satisfaction. It is your nature. Objects merely trigger what is already yours.
  5. Expand the feeling: Once you’ve stopped and felt the satisfaction, deliberately expand it. Let it fill your chest, your belly, your limbs, your whole body. Let it radiate outward. Amplify it not by thinking but by FEELING more deeply into it. 10 seconds of this is enough.
  6. Keep a mental count: For the first week, count your satisfactions. Try to notice at least 20 per day. You’ll be surprised: once you start looking, they are everywhere. The mind just never bothered to register them before.
  7. Evening review: Before sleep, replay the day’s satisfactions. Re-feel each one. Let the body re-experience the warmth, the ease, the contentment. This programs the unconscious to seek satisfaction rather than dissatisfaction.
  8. The accumulative effect: After 2–3 weeks, something shifts: you begin to feel satisfied for NO reason. A background contentment arises that is not dependent on any particular event. This is the technique bearing fruit — you have trained the mind to rest in its natural state of ease rather than its habitual state of complaint.
  9. Advanced practice: Once you can find satisfaction in pleasant events, try finding it in neutral events: washing dishes, waiting in traffic, walking to the bathroom. There IS a subtle satisfaction in simply being alive, in the body functioning, in breath flowing. Find it. Actualise it.
  10. The deepest level: Eventually, satisfaction becomes independent of ALL events. You discover that existence itself is satisfying — that being alive is intrinsically blissful. Objects may add flavour, but the base flavour of being is already sweet. This is what the sutra means by “actualize this” — make the satisfaction real, permanent, and unconditional.

Technique 57 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: desire · witness · cyclone · centre · equanimity
In moods of extreme desire, be undisturbed.

In Plain English

When desire grips you — anger, lust, greed, jealousy — don’t suppress it and don’t express it outward toward its object. Instead, move INWARD toward the subject. Close your door. Express the emotion in a vacuum — toward nobody — and WATCH. Inside every cyclone of emotion, there is a still centre. The cyclone cannot exist without that centre. Find it, and you have found your self.

How to Practice

  1. The two wrong approaches: Society teaches two responses to extreme desire: suppress it (push it down, deny it, control it) or express it toward the object (yell at the person, indulge the lust, act on the greed). Both are wrong. Suppression stores poison inside; expression creates karma outside. This technique offers a third way: express inward, watch from the centre.
  2. When desire arises in real time: The moment you feel an extreme mood coming on — anger building, lust flaring, jealousy twisting — go immediately to a private space. Lock the door. You have 2–5 minutes before the peak passes and the opportunity is lost.
  3. Express in a vacuum: Beat a pillow. Scream into a cushion. Pace furiously. Let the body express the energy FULLY — but toward no one. There is no target, no victim, no object. The expression is pure energy without direction. This releases the energy without creating karma.
  4. Now WATCH: As you express, simultaneously step back and observe. You are both the actor AND the audience. Part of you rages; part of you watches the raging. Notice: even at the peak of fury, the watcher is not angry. The watcher is perfectly still. That still point IS you.
  5. Find the centre of the cyclone: A cyclone has a still eye at its centre. The more violent the periphery, the more still the centre. Use the violence of the emotion to FIND the centre by contrast. The question is not “How do I stop the cyclone?” but “Where is the stillness that allows the cyclone to exist?”
  6. The re-enactment method (easier): If catching real emotions in the moment is too difficult, use this variation: sit alone, close your eyes, and DELIBERATELY re-create a past moment of extreme desire. Replay it in full cinematic detail. Feel the anger/lust/greed rising again. Now WATCH it from the centre. This is easier because the real situation is gone — you can witness more easily without being swept away.
  7. Gurdjieff’s school method: Gurdjieff would create situations in his school specifically to provoke anger in students — insult them publicly, create unfair situations — then suddenly shout “REMEMBER! Be undisturbed!” The shock of the reminder would catapult students into the witness. You can ask a trusted friend or partner to do this: provoke you and then remind you “Watch!”
  8. 30-minute psychodrama session: Set aside 30 minutes daily. Sit alone. Deliberately invoke any strong emotion from the past. Let the body express it freely (scream, cry, shake, rage). After 15 minutes of full expression, suddenly become completely still. Sit motionless. Feel the contrast: the storm has passed and the stillness remains. THAT stillness is what you are. The storm was what you HAVE.
  9. The insight: After practising this enough times, you discover: you are never really angry. Anger HAPPENS in your space, like weather happening in the sky. The sky is never weather. You are never emotion. You are the space in which emotions arise and pass.
  10. Integration into daily life: Eventually, you don’t need to go to a private room. You can find the still centre WHILE the emotion is happening, WHILE you are in the situation. The witness becomes so strong that emotions continue on the surface but the centre remains undisturbed. This is equanimity — not suppression but awareness so vast it can contain anything without being shaken.

Technique 58 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: drama · illusion · play · lightness · detachment
This so-called universe appears as a juggling, a picture show. To be happy look upon it so.

In Plain English

See the entire world as a movie. You are sitting in a cinema watching a screen. People are born and die, dramas unfold, nations rise and fall — but it is all a projection on a screen. Nothing is as permanent or serious as it appears. When you can see your OWN life as a drama you are watching — when you become the audience rather than the actor — suffering drops instantly. This is not escapism; it is seeing reality as it actually is: impermanent, dream-like, a play of light and shadow.

How to Practice

  1. Go to an actual cinema: Watch the AUDIENCE, not the film. Observe how people weep, laugh, gasp, grip their seats — all over light projected on a white screen. Nothing real is happening: no one is dying, no one is falling in love. Yet the audience experiences real tears, real fear, real arousal. Notice: this is what we do with life itself.
  2. Turn it on yourself: After the cinema, walk outside and look at life with the same eyes. People rushing, arguing, falling in love, grieving. See it as a vast movie. The sky is the screen. The world is the projection. You are the awareness watching.
  3. Your own life as a film: This is the deepest and most challenging step. See YOUR life — your problems, your relationships, your career, your body — as a movie you are watching. You are not the character; you are the viewer. The character has problems. The viewer is simply watching a character with problems.
  4. The “as if” method: For one entire day, live AS IF everything is a play. You are an actor playing your role perfectly — but you know it’s a role. Go to work, talk to people, solve problems — but with the inner knowing: this is a performance. See what shifts when you hold this perspective.
  5. Notice impermanence: The sutra says “juggling.” In a juggling show, balls are constantly in the air — nothing stays still, nothing is permanent. Look at life this way: everything is in motion, everything is temporary, everything you grasp will fall. This is not depressing but liberating — because if nothing lasts, nothing needs to be clung to.
  6. Practice with small dramas first: Don’t start with your deepest suffering. Start with traffic jams, minor arguments, small frustrations. See these as scenes in a movie. “Oh, this is the scene where the character gets annoyed by traffic.” Feel the lightness that comes when you step out of the drama.
  7. Escalate gradually: Over weeks, apply this to bigger life situations: relationship conflicts, work stress, health worries. See them from the cinema seat. The character is worried about health. What does the viewer feel? Nothing — just watching.
  8. The happiness instruction: The sutra specifically says “to be happy, look upon it so.” Happiness is not found by fixing the movie (the world) but by changing your relationship to it. When you stop taking the show personally, happiness arises naturally — the lightness of the viewer, the freedom of the witness.
  9. Night review: Before sleep, replay your day as a movie. Watch your character from the outside. See the scenes unfold. Notice: from this distance, even today’s dramas look small, manageable, almost amusing. This is the perspective shift the sutra promises.
  10. Don’t lose compassion: Osho warns: seeing life as a play should not make you cold or indifferent to others’ suffering. A good audience FEELS with the characters — but they don’t forget they are watching. You can empathise fully while knowing it is all impermanent. This is compassion without attachment.

Technique 59 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: middle · neither · equanimity · zero-point · balance
Oh beloved, put attention neither on pleasure nor on pain, but between these.

In Plain English

When pleasure comes, don’t run toward it. When pain comes, don’t run away. Stay at the zero point between them — the still, alert balance that is neither attracted nor repelled. This is not numbness. It is the most ALIVE state possible: pure awareness unclouded by preference. The zero point is YOU — consciousness that is neither pleased nor pained but simply aware.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the pendulum: The mind is a pendulum swinging between pleasure and pain. When you pursue pleasure, you guarantee eventual pain (when it ends). When you flee pain, you guarantee frustration (when it persists). This technique stops the pendulum at dead centre — not by force but by withdrawing energy from both sides.
  2. Formal sitting (20 min): Sit comfortably. Close your eyes. Watch sensations arise in the body. Some are pleasant (warmth, ease, tingling). Some are unpleasant (tension, itch, pressure). DON’T prefer either. Don’t lean toward the pleasant; don’t lean away from the unpleasant. Just watch from the exact middle.
  3. The neutral zone: Between every pleasant and unpleasant sensation, there is a vast neutral space. Most of the body, most of the time, is neither pleasurable nor painful. Rest your attention HERE — in the neutral, the unremarkable, the middle ground. This is the gateway to the zero point.
  4. When praised: Someone compliments you. The mind inflates — pleasure rushes in. CATCH IT. Don’t suppress the pleasure but don’t inflate with it. Watch it arise and remain at the point BEFORE inflation. You feel the praise registering but you don’t become big.
  5. When blamed: Someone criticises you. The mind deflates — pain rushes in. CATCH IT. Don’t deny the pain but don’t collapse into it. Watch it registering but remain at the point BEFORE deflation. You feel the sting but you don’t become small.
  6. The equanimity body scan: Lie down. Scan the body slowly from head to toes. At each point, notice: is this pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral? For pleasant areas, don’t linger. For unpleasant areas, don’t rush. For neutral areas, give them the same quality of attention as the pleasant ones. This trains the mind out of its preference bias.
  7. In emotional situations: Something good happens — a raise, a date, a compliment. Before the euphoria peaks, find the still point. Something bad happens — a rejection, a loss, a failure. Before the despair deepens, find the still point. The still point is always available because it IS you. You are not the pleasure or the pain — you are the awareness in which both appear.
  8. Walking the razor’s edge: Osho calls this “walking on the razor’s edge” because the middle is extremely narrow. The moment you relax, the pendulum swings to one side. Stay alert. Stay balanced. Stay precisely in the middle — not rigidly (that would be tension) but dynamically (like a tightrope walker who is constantly adjusting).
  9. Duration and patience: This is a lifelong practice. In the beginning, you may hold the middle for only seconds before toppling into pleasure or pain. That’s fine. Return to the middle again and again. Over months, the balance becomes more stable. Over years, it becomes your default.
  10. The discovery: At the zero point, something extraordinary is discovered: when there is no movement toward pleasure or away from pain, there is no suffering. Not because sensation stops — but because resistance stops. And in that stillness, a profound peace arises that is deeper than any pleasure and untouched by any pain. This peace IS you.

Technique 60 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: acceptance · non-judgement · transformation · universality · compassion
Objects and desires exist in me as in others. So accepting, let them be transformed.

In Plain English

When a desire arises — anger, lust, greed, jealousy — don’t fight it. Recognise: this same desire exists in ALL beings. You are not special in having it, nor are you sinful. It is simply human. This acceptance — “I am human, and all human things live in me” — removes the guilt and shame that STRENGTHEN desires. What you accept and witness without judgement transforms on its own. What you fight grows stronger.

How to Practice

  1. Notice the desire: When any desire arises — anger at a colleague, lust for a stranger, greed for more money, jealousy of a friend’s success — the first step is simply to NOTICE it without immediately acting on it or condemning it.
  2. Apply the universal formula: Say internally (not as mantra but as recognition): “This desire exists in me as it does in all beings.” Anger exists in me as it does in the saint, the criminal, the child, the sage. Lust exists in me as it does in monks and prostitutes alike. This is human. I am human. There is nothing wrong with me.
  3. Feel the shame dissolve: Notice what happens when you accept the desire as universal rather than personal. The shame drops. The guilt drops. The desperate need to HIDE it drops. And with the shame gone, the desire itself loses much of its power. Shame was feeding it; acceptance starves it.
  4. Watch without judgement: Now simply watch the desire. Don’t suppress it (that’s fighting). Don’t indulge it (that’s identification). Just watch it with the neutral curiosity of a scientist observing a phenomenon. “Ah, anger. Interesting. It has heat, tension in the jaw, constriction in the chest. Interesting.”
  5. Compassion for self: If guilt persists, add compassion: “Just as I would not condemn another human for having this desire, I do not condemn myself.” This is not self-indulgence; it is the recognition that cruelty toward yourself does not produce transformation — it produces repression, which produces explosion later.
  6. Compassion for others: Extended practice: when you see someone acting from anger, lust, or greed, recognise “this exists in me too.” Your judgement of them dissolves. In its place: understanding. This is the birth of real compassion — not pity from above but recognition from the same level.
  7. The transformation principle: Osho’s key insight: desires transform when accepted, not when fought. Lust, when fully accepted and witnessed without judgement, gradually transmutes into love. Anger, fully accepted, transmutes into compassionate assertiveness. Greed, fully accepted, transmutes into natural abundance-consciousness. The transformation is not YOUR doing — it happens by itself when the pressure of judgement is removed.
  8. Daily reflection: Each evening, review moments of desire that arose during the day. For each one, apply the formula: “This exists in me as in others. I accept it.” Feel the release. Feel the lightness. Notice: desires you accept have less grip the next time they arise.
  9. The radical acceptance: Eventually, expand to EVERYTHING inside you: fear, rage, pettiness, selfishness, laziness, vanity. ALL of it is human. ALL of it exists in everyone. When you can embrace the totality of your inner world without judgement, a profound self-love arises that is not narcissism but wholeness.
  10. When desires visit less: After months of this practice, you notice: certain desires visit less frequently. Not because you pushed them away but because you stopped giving them the energy of resistance. A desire that is fully accepted has no drama, no charge, no interest. It simply dissolves for lack of fuel. This is natural transformation — not the violent transformation of willpower but the gentle transformation of acceptance.

The Techniques for Witnessing

Ten techniques that use the observer itself as the doorway — watching desire, watching sleep, watching satisfaction, watching the watcher. The witness is the oldest meditation in existence: simply step back and see. What sees cannot be seen; what knows cannot be known. That which remains when all objects dissolve is you.

10 techniques · ~16 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These ten techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The witnessing sutras form the fifth major category — using the observer, the watcher, the awareness-behind-experience as the direct path to self-realisation.

Witnessing is the most universal meditation technique. Every tradition has a version of it: Buddhist vipassana, Gurdjieff’s self-remembering, Zen’s “just sitting,” Advaita’s self-inquiry. These ten sutras offer different angles of entry into the same essential practice: stepping back from experience to discover what remains.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

The witnessing path is subtle: These techniques don’t produce fireworks. They produce silence. The sign of progress is not dramatic experience but the quiet disappearance of suffering.

New to witnessing meditation?

Start with Technique 2 — wherever satisfaction is found or Technique 9 — as waves come with water. Both are gentle, positive, and accessible to anyone. Spend 20–40 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.

Glossary — unfamiliar terms?

Underlined words below are also marked inline; hover or tap them anywhere on the page for a quick definition.

Vigyan Bhairav Tantra
A ~5,000-year-old Sanskrit text presented as a dialogue between Shiva and Devi, listing 112 meditation techniques.
witness
The observing awareness behind all experience — that which sees thoughts, emotions, and sensations without being any of them.
self-remembering
Gurdjieff’s term for non-verbal awareness of one’s own being — feeling “I am” without thinking it.
Gurdjieff
20th century mystic (1866–1949) who used this sutra as the basis of his entire school of “the Fourth Way.”
double-arrowed awareness
Attention simultaneously on the object AND on the one who is aware — seeing the flower AND feeling the seer.
psychodrama
Therapeutic technique of re-enacting emotions in a safe container — expressing anger, grief, or desire without a real target.
equanimity
Balanced awareness that neither grasps pleasure nor rejects pain — the still point between attraction and aversion.
hypnagogia
The threshold state between waking and sleep — when external perception fades but sleep has not yet arrived.
non-duality
The recognition that subject and object are one undivided field — there is no separate knower apart from the knowing.
turiya
The fourth state beyond waking, dreaming, and sleeping — pure awareness that witnesses all three without being any of them.

Practise on your own discretion — adjust each technique to your own body and mind. Witnessing techniques are generally safe for all levels but can surface suppressed emotions. If overwhelm arises, ground through your body, breathe normally, and return to ordinary activity.
