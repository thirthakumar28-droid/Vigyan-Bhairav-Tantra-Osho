Technique 15 of 112

Closing the seven openings of the head with your hands, a space between your eyes becomes all-inclusive.

In Plain English

Block the ears, eyes, nostrils, and mouth with your fingers. With all outer input sealed, attention naturally falls inward to the space between the eyes — the third eye area — and a vast inner world opens.

How to Practice

Note from the source: Osho is unusually emphatic that this technique "is not to be practiced; only suddenly is it helpful." Repeating it daily as a routine, he says, turns it into a mechanical habit and produces nothing. The instruction below is therefore for the rare, sudden use of the gesture — not a daily breath-practice.

  1. Reserve this technique for a moment of inner urgency — when you feel cornered, when the mind is unbearably noisy, when "ordinary practice" cannot reach. Then suddenly close all seven openings.
  2. Thumbs into the ears. Index fingers lightly over closed eyelids. Middle fingers closing the nostrils. Ring fingers above the upper lip and little fingers below the lower lip, sealing the mouth.

  5. In that suffocation moment the breath/thought association breaks. Attention is thrown to the space between the eyebrows, which becomes "all-inclusive."
  6. Release when the moment is genuinely unbearable. Then sit. Don't repeat tomorrow.

Category: Centering · Tags: look · lovingly · object · another
Look lovingly at some object. Do not go to another object. Here in the middle of the object – the blessing.

In Plain English

Pick one thing — a flower, a face, a candle, a stone, anything. Look at it not as a viewer but as a lover. Forget yourself completely. Want only its happiness, its flowering. Do not move on to a second thing. Stay with this one. When the looking has become love and the self has dissolved into concern for the other, the blessing comes — you are suddenly centred without ever having tried to be.

How to Practice

  1. Choose one object you will stay with for the whole session. A rose-flower is Osho’s favourite example, but anything will work — a candle flame, a tree, a beloved’s face, a small stone, even a car you love. The object doesn’t matter; the love does.
  2. Sit before the object at a comfortable distance. Eyes open. No special posture is required.
  3. First instruction (Osho’s exact words): “Forget yourself completely.” The “I” that is looking has to step back. Let the object fill your awareness so completely that you, the looker, become absent.
  4. Look at it not to study it but to love it. Let a feeling of warmth begin to flow from your chest toward the object. Don’t fake the feeling — let it arise naturally as you keep looking.
  5. Now hold a single thought (this is Osho’s specific instruction): “What can I do to help this flower flower more, to be more beautiful, more alive, more blissful?” Let this question be the only thought in you.
  6. Don’t actually try to do anything. The point is the feeling, the ache of caring, not the action. Whether or not you can “do” anything is irrelevant — what matters is that every fibre of you is leaning toward the object’s well-being.
  7. Do not let your eyes wander to other objects. Osho is firm on this: “Do not go to another object. Remain with one.” The whole world should fall away — only this one thing remains in your consciousness.
  8. You will reach a strange point: the wish to make this thing more beautiful, more happy, will collide with the realisation that there is nothing you can actually do. This helplessness is the doorway. Don’t resist it. Stay in it.
  9. In that helplessness the mind has nothing to chew on. Self-centred thought stops. There is no “I” thinking, only the silent looking and the silent caring. You are empty.
  10. This is the moment Shiva calls “the blessing.” Don’t try to grab it. Just stay where you are. The bliss will come on its own, as a by-product of the self’s absence.
  11. Stay as long as you can. When the session ends, don’t break it sharply. Look down, sit quietly, then go on with the day.
  12. Practise with the same object, or with the same kind of object (always a flower, always a candle, always a face), so that mood-shifts of attention don’t pull you. Osho says: love itself is the greatest method — if you can do this, no other technique is needed.

In Plain English

Sit on the floor with only your buttocks touching the ground — legs lifted, arms lifted or free. The moment you find physical balance, the inner centre reveals itself. The body's search for equilibrium becomes awareness's search for stillness.

How to Practice

Note from the source: Osho describes a different physical setup than the boat-pose this page originally suggested. The sutra word "buttocks" refers to weight-bearing — sit in a normal seated meditation posture (lotus, half-lotus, siddhasana, or simply cross-legged) where your weight rests on the buttocks alone, with the legs folded and the arms not bracing. The technique is then a balance of weight between the two buttocks, not a v-sit.

  1. Prerequisite: Osho says the buttocks must first be made sensitive. Spend a week or two simply noticing them — against the bed, against a chair, while sitting. Most adults have lost feeling in this area. Without that sensitivity the technique cannot land.
  2. Sit on a firm, flat surface in your normal cross-legged posture. Spine upright. Hands resting on knees or in lap — not bearing weight.
  3. Close the eyes. Feel which buttock currently bears more of your weight. It is almost never even.
  4. Without moving externally, shift weight slightly to the lighter side. Then back. Find the exact balance where weight rests evenly between the two buttocks.
  5. The instant the balance is true — "suddenly, the centering" — awareness drops to the navel/hara. The mind stops because the body has nothing to hold against.
  6. Stay there. If you wobble out, find balance again. Each finding is a fresh centering.

The Techniques for Centering

Ten techniques that bring awareness to the still point within the body — the spine, the navel, the heart-space, the middle between any two opposites. The centre is always present; these methods simply show you where to look.

About these techniques

These ten techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. These centering sutras come from the body-awareness and posture chapters — the techniques that use the physical form as a doorway to the formless.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don't shop, don't switch, don't combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn't working — that is the work. Stay.

New to centering?

Start with Technique 4 — feel awareness filling your spine. It is the most iconic centering sutra and needs no special posture. Spend 20 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.

A ~5,000-year-old Sanskrit text presented as a dialogue between Shiva and Devi, listing 112 meditation techniques.
centering
The act of bringing awareness to a still point inside the body or mind — a location where movement pauses and the witness can rest.
sushumna
The central energy channel running along the spinal cord in yogic anatomy. When prana flows through it, one enters a state of balance.
hara
The Japanese term (tanden in Chinese) for the energy centre two inches below the navel — the body's gravitational and vital centre.
chakra
Literally "wheel" — an intersection of energy channels in the subtle body. The seven main chakras correspond to the spine from base to crown.
kundalini
Dormant energy said to rest at the base of the spine. When awakened, it rises through the sushumna toward the crown.
witness
The observing awareness behind all experience — the "I" that watches thoughts, sensations, and emotions without being any of them.
samadhi
A deep meditative state of unification; absorption beyond ordinary self-consciousness.
proprioception
The body's internal sense of its own position and movement — the "sixth sense" that knows where your limbs are without looking.
interoception
The sense of the internal state of the body — heartbeat, gut feeling, breath, temperature. Key to embodied awareness.

Practise on your own discretion — adjust each technique to your own body and mind.
