The Techniques for Emptiness & Void

Eight techniques that use emptiness, surrender, exhaustion, and dissolution as doorways to the void. Shunyata is not nothingness — it is pregnant emptiness, the womb from which all existence arises and returns.

8 techniques · ~10 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These eight techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The void and emptiness sutras form the seventh major category — using deprivation, exhaustion, devotion, sky-gazing, and the investigation of identity to dissolve the ego into the pregnant emptiness that underlies all things.

These techniques approach awakening through subtraction rather than addition. Where other categories build something (light, sound, centering), these techniques REMOVE everything until only the void remains. The void is not frightening — it is the most peaceful, the most liberating, the most total state available to consciousness.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These void techniques work by REMOVAL, not by addition. You are not creating emptiness — you are discovering it was always there, hidden behind identifications, desires, and knowledge.

New to void meditation?

Start with Technique 6 — without support, sit on buttocks or Technique 8 — looking into blue sky. Both are simple, physical, and produce quick results. Spend 20–40 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for the deeper techniques.

Technique 23 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: single-object · empty-place · filling · knower · void-behind
Feel an object before you. Feel the absence of all other objects but this one. Then leaving aside the object-feeling and the absence-feeling, realize.

In Plain English

Three steps. (1) Feel one object — a flower, a stone, a flame — so completely that it fills consciousness. Don’t just look at it; feel it through every sense. (2) Feel the absence of every other object — the whole world has dropped away; only this one thing remains. (3) Now drop both: drop the feeling-of-the-object, and drop the feeling-of-everything-else-being-absent. What remains is pure consciousness without content — the void, your own nature. Osho calls this an “abyss”: when there is no object and no absence-of-other-objects to hold, only awareness is left, like a flame burning without anything to illuminate.

How to Practice

  1. Choose a quiet, sparse place: The sutra itself does not specify, but Osho recommends an environment with as little visual clutter as possible. A simple room, a quiet garden, an empty corner. The fewer competing objects in the field, the easier it is for the chosen object to fully fill consciousness.
  2. Place one object: In this empty space, place (or find) a single object. A stone. A candle flame. A dot on the wall. A flower. It should be simple, singular, and visually distinct from the empty background. The contrast between the ONE object and the EMPTY space is essential.
  3. Fix attention totally: Sit comfortably facing the object. Now fix your mind on it with total concentration. See nothing else. Think of nothing else. Let the object fill your entire field of awareness. Not forcefully — lovingly. Let the object become the whole world.
  4. Let it fill you: As concentration deepens, the object begins to “enter” you. The boundary between you (the seer) and it (the seen) thins. You become FULL of the object — saturated with its presence, its form, its reality. There is nothing else in consciousness except this stone, this flame, this dot.
  5. Feel the absence of all other objects: Now register that the rest of the world has fallen away. Just this object exists; everything else is absent. This absence-feeling is itself a felt experience — not just intellectual knowledge but the lived sense of “only this is here.” Stay with both feelings simultaneously: this object is here AND everything else is absent.
  6. Now leave both aside: This is the pivot Osho calls the most difficult part. Having reached the state where you feel one object fully and feel the absence of all else, drop BOTH feelings — the object-feeling and the absence-feeling. Don’t replace them with anything. Don’t ask “who is filled?” (that would be a different technique). Simply let go of the object-feeling, let go of the absence-feeling, and what remains is what the sutra calls realisation — pure consciousness with nothing in it.
  7. Remove the object: Now close your eyes (or turn away from the object). The object is gone from awareness. What remains? The void — the empty container. But now you KNOW it is there because the object revealed it. Without the object, the void might seem like “nothing.” After the object, the void is clearly felt as the SPACE in which objects appear.
  8. Rest in the void: Stay in this awareness of the void — the empty, open, knowing space that contains all experience but is itself empty. It is serene, peaceful, without boundary. It is what you actually ARE — the space, not the objects that pass through it.
  9. Alternate: For deeper practice, alternate: open eyes, fill with object, feel fullness. Close eyes, feel the void that was filled. Open eyes again, fill again. Close, void again. Each alternation makes the void more clearly felt. The object is the finger pointing at the moon — the void is the moon itself.
  10. Integration: Once this is understood experientially, you can do it anywhere. Any object can reveal the void behind it. Look at a tree and ask: “Who sees?” The seeing is happening in void-space. The tree reveals the void. Every perception becomes a doorway to emptiness.

  • Skipping the filling phase and jumping straight to the void — the contrast of fullness is necessary; the object is the vehicle

Questions From The Book

Why does Osho recommend an empty environment?

Chapter 50 — Moving to the Roots

The sutra itself does not specify location, but Osho’s commentary stresses: if the environment is full of objects, the mind bounces between them and cannot achieve the singular concentration needed for the “feel one object” phase. More subtly: when the outer field has only one thing in it, the inner field can mirror that condition more easily. Practical (fewer distractions) and supportive (one object in an empty visual field is itself a small enactment of the technique — one thought in empty awareness).

What is the difference between this and normal concentration meditation?

Chapter 61

Osho clarifies: in normal concentration, the goal IS the object — you want to become one with it (samadhi with seed). Here, the object is merely a TOOL. The real target is what remains when even the object-feeling and the absence-of-other-objects-feeling are dropped — the awareness that received them. Normal concentration ends with fullness; this technique uses fullness as a stepping-stone to the void. The dropping of both feelings is what transforms concentration into self-knowledge.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This technique uses the figure-ground relationship in perception. Normally we focus on figures (objects) and ignore the ground (space/awareness). By deliberately concentrating on ONE figure until it saturates attention, and then shifting to ask “what is the ground?” — you discover that the ground of all experience is empty awareness. Every object rests in the void; the void holds all objects without being any of them.

Perceptual psychology: Gestalt psychology shows that figure and ground are complementary — you cannot have one without the other. By saturating attention with figure (the object), you make ground (the void) maximally apparent by contrast. This is an elegant use of perceptual mechanics for spiritual investigation.

One-line essenceFeel one object completely; feel the absence of all else; then drop both. What remains is the realisation.
Technique 27 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: exhaustion · dropping · surrender · wholeness · effort-to-effortlessness
Roam about until exhausted and then, dropping to the ground, in this dropping be whole.

In Plain English

Move the body vigorously — dance wildly, run, spin, do Osho’s Dynamic Meditation. Continue until the body is utterly exhausted and DROPS of its own accord. In that dropping — when effort is impossible — the ego also drops. You become whole because the doer has been exhausted into silence. The void reveals itself when there is no energy left to maintain the ego.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your method of exhaustion: Wild dancing, running, vigorous shaking, spinning (Sufi-style), Osho’s Dynamic Meditation, or simply roaming/walking intensely. The specific movement doesn’t matter — what matters is that it exhausts the body TOTALLY.
  2. Create a safe space: You need a space where you can move wildly without self-consciousness or danger. A private room with padded floor, a secluded garden, a beach at dawn. Remove obstacles you could trip over. Wear minimal, comfortable clothing.
  3. Begin with intensity: Start moving with TOTAL intensity. Not half-hearted — give 100%. If dancing, dance as if possessed. If running, run full-speed. If shaking, shake every cell. The key is: hold nothing back. The ego maintains itself through reserves of energy; exhaust ALL reserves.
  4. Continue past comfort: When the body says “enough,” continue. When the mind says “I can’t,” continue. The first “I can’t” is a lie — the body has reserves. Push through the first barrier. And the second. Continue until the body GENUINELY cannot continue — not out of choice but out of true physical impossibility.
  5. The dropping: When exhaustion is total, LET yourself drop. Don’t lower yourself carefully — DROP. Fall to the ground. Collapse. The dropping must be total, sudden, without control. Like a puppet whose strings are cut. This is the critical moment.
  6. In the dropping, be whole: The moment you drop — in that instant of total surrender to gravity — FEEL the wholeness. The ego, which requires energy to maintain its divisions and tensions, simply cannot function when all energy is spent. In that powerless state, you are whole because there is no energy to maintain the fragments.
  7. Lie still on the ground: After dropping, lie completely still. Face down or face up — however you fell. Don’t adjust, don’t make yourself comfortable. The body will feel like it’s melting into the earth. Let it. Breathe heavily — that’s natural. But don’t move. Stay 15–20 minutes in this absolute stillness.
  8. Observe the void: In the stillness after exhaustion, notice: the mind is empty. Not because you forced it to be empty, but because there is simply no energy left to think. Thoughts require energy. When energy is spent, thoughts stop. This is the most effortless meditation — emptiness achieved through total effort.
  9. Feel the wholeness: Without the ego’s constant dividing activity, you feel whole — undivided, unfragmented, simply and purely present. No inner conflict, no “I should” or “I shouldn’t,” no past or future. Just this: the ground, the body, the breath, the void. Total.
  10. Rise slowly: When energy begins to return, rise very slowly. Notice: as energy returns, the ego also returns. Watch it reassembling itself — first the body-sense, then preferences, then memories, then the “I.” Seeing this reassembly AFTER having been without it is deeply instructive. The ego is clearly seen as a construction, not a given.

Questions From The Book

Why does exhaustion lead to wholeness?

Chapter 59, "Dropping Into Wholeness"

Osho explains: the ego is an activity, not a thing. It requires constant energy to maintain — dividing experience into “me” and “not-me,” judging, controlling, resisting. When all energy is exhausted, the ego simply cannot perform its activity. It stops. And when the ego stops, what remains is wholeness — because the only thing preventing wholeness was the ego’s constant dividing. You don’t ACHIEVE wholeness; you simply exhaust the obstacle to it.

Is this why Osho created Dynamic Meditation?

Chapter 59

Osho confirms: Dynamic Meditation is a modern systematisation of this ancient sutra. The first three stages (chaotic breathing, catharsis, jumping with “Hoo!”) exhaust the body and release emotional blocks. The fourth stage (freeze/stop) IS the dropping. The fifth stage (celebration) is the wholeness expressed. The sutra gives the principle; Dynamic Meditation gives the method. For modern people with sedentary lives and repressed energy, the structured approach of Dynamic Meditation may be more effective than simply “roaming about.”

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The ego/self-model requires metabolic energy to maintain. Research on ego-depletion shows that self-control, decision-making, and identity-maintenance all consume glucose. When physical exhaustion depletes these resources, the self-model temporarily simplifies or dissolves. This technique uses that biological reality deliberately — exhaust the fuel that powers the ego, and the ego stops.

Parallel practices: Sufi whirling, ecstatic dance traditions, marathon running (“runner’s high” as ego-dissolution), and various indigenous dance-until-you-drop ceremonies all use the same principle. The body becomes the vehicle for transcendence when pushed beyond its normal operating limits.

One-line essenceThe ego needs energy to maintain its illusion of separateness. Exhaust all energy, drop to the ground, and discover: without the effort of being someone, you are whole.
Technique 28 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: deprivation · transcend · letting-go · weakness · surrender
Suppose you are gradually being deprived of strength or of knowledge. At the instant of deprivation, transcend.

In Plain English

Imagine strength leaving the body — muscles dissolving, power fading. OR imagine knowledge leaving — certainties dissolving, opinions evaporating, all you “know” disappearing. At the MOMENT nothing remains — no strength, no knowledge — something transcends. The void is reached not by gaining but by losing everything.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your path — strength or knowledge: This sutra offers two approaches. Try both over different sessions, then commit to whichever resonates more deeply. Strength-deprivation works better for body-identified people; knowledge-deprivation for intellectuals.
  2. PATH A — Deprivation of strength: Lie down comfortably. Close your eyes. Feel your body as it is — its solidity, its musculature, its capacity for action. Now begin to FEEL that strength draining away. Start with the feet — feel them becoming empty, powerless, weightless.
  3. Let weakness rise: The emptying of strength rises slowly through the body — calves dissolving, thighs becoming hollow, pelvis emptying of power. Feel the belly losing its strength, the chest becoming light and insubstantial. Arms losing their grip, hands becoming feather-light, unable to hold anything.
  4. Complete deprivation: Continue until the entire body feels utterly powerless — as if you couldn’t lift a finger if your life depended on it. Not tense weakness but RELEASED weakness — like a rag doll, like a deflated balloon. No strength anywhere. Completely helpless.
  5. PATH B — Deprivation of knowledge: Sit comfortably, eyes closed. Begin by feeling all your certainties. Your name, your age, your beliefs, your opinions, your skills, your memories. FEEL how much you “know.” Now begin to let it dissolve. Start with opinions — feel them evaporating. Then beliefs. Then facts. Then memories.
  6. Feel knowledge dissolving: Let your name dissolve — you no longer know what you are called. Let language dissolve — words lose meaning. Let all concepts dissolve — categories become meaningless. Let even the knowledge of WHERE you are dissolve. Who you are. WHAT you are. All dissolving into emptiness.
  7. The instant of total deprivation: In BOTH paths, arrive at a point where NOTHING remains. No strength to act. Or no knowledge to orient. In that instant — that precise moment of complete deprivation — something transcends. Not “you” transcend — the “you” has been deprived of everything. SOMETHING ELSE transcends. The void itself becomes alive.
  8. Don’t try to transcend: The sutra says “at the instant of deprivation, transcend” — but this is a description, not a command. You cannot DO transcendence. You can only create the conditions for it. The conditions are: total deprivation. When nothing remains, transcendence happens on its own.
  9. Rest in whatever opens: After the moment of transcendence (which may be subtle or dramatic), rest in whatever space has opened. Don’t analyse it. Don’t grab at it. Let it be what it is. It may feel like floating in space, like being nothing, like infinite peace, like silent aliveness.
  10. Return gradually: Allow strength or knowledge to return naturally. Don’t rush back. Notice: as they return, the ego reassembles around them. Strength and knowledge are the building blocks of the ego. Without them, there is only the void. With them, there is a “someone.” You now know the choice is available.

Questions From The Book

Why does Shiva say “suppose” — is this just imagination?

Chapter 60, "Deprivation and Transcendence"

Osho explains: the word “suppose” is a skillful device. If Shiva said “be deprived,” the ego would resist violently. But “suppose” gives the mind permission — “it’s only hypothetical, only imagination.” The ego relaxes. And then the supposing becomes real. Because when you FEEL yourself truly without strength, even in imagination, the body responds as if it were real. The nervous system doesn’t distinguish between vividly imagined and actual deprivation. So “suppose” is the Trojan horse that gets past the ego’s defences.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The ego maintains itself through two pillars: the capacity to act (strength) and the capacity to know (knowledge). Remove both, and the ego structure cannot sustain itself. This is a guided approach to what naturally happens in deep surrender states, near-death experiences, and moments of total overwhelm — but here it is done deliberately, safely, and reversibly.

Neuroscience parallel: Research on “flow states” shows that when the sense of agency (strength) and the internal narrative (knowledge/stories) both quiet down, people report timelessness, egolessness, and transcendent states. This technique achieves the same result through imaginative deprivation rather than optimal performance.

One-line essenceThe void is not reached by gaining but by losing. Lose all strength. Lose all knowing. At the moment nothing is left, something beyond both is revealed.
Technique 61 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: waves · universal · oneness · movement · flow
As waves come with water and flames with fire, so the universal waves with us.

In Plain English

Feel yourself as a wave in the ocean of existence — not separate, not permanent, but a temporary arising of the universal energy. Water doesn’t “do” waves; it IS waves. Fire doesn’t “do” flames; it IS flames. You don’t “do” life; you ARE life waving. Rest in this feeling: you are not a separate entity doing things in the universe. You are the universe doing things through this particular wave-form called “you.”

How to Practice

  1. Contemplate the wave: Sit by water if possible — ocean, river, lake, even a fountain or bathtub. Watch waves forming, rising, moving, and dissolving. Notice: each wave seems individual, but no wave is made of different water than the ocean. The wave IS the ocean, temporarily taking a form, then returning.
  2. You are a wave: Close your eyes. Feel yourself as a wave. You arose from the universal ocean of existence. You have a temporary form — this body, this personality, this life. But you are not SEPARATE from the ocean. You are the ocean in wave-form. The same energy that flows through all beings flows through you.
  3. When walking: Feel: this is the universe walking. Not “I am walking in the universe” but “the universe is walking AS me.” The legs move by the same energy that moves planets. The breath flows by the same force that moves tides. You are not the doer; you are the doing itself.
  4. When breathing: Feel: the universe breathes through me. This air was in trees, in clouds, in other lungs. It flows through this form temporarily and then returns to the whole. There is no “my breath” — there is only breathing happening through this body-wave.
  5. When eating: The food was sunlight, rain, soil, seed, plant. It enters this wave-form and becomes temporarily “me.” Then it returns to the earth. There is no “my body” separate from the world — just a constant flow of the universe through this temporary pattern.
  6. Feel the impermanence: A wave rises and falls. It has no anxiety about falling because it knows it is water. Similarly: this body-mind will dissolve. But if you FEEL yourself as the ocean rather than the wave, what is there to fear? The wave was always the ocean. Nothing is lost when the wave subsides.
  7. 20-minute sitting: Sit quietly. Feel the boundary of your skin dissolving. You are not a container of life; you are life flowing. Energy streams in and out with every breath, every sensation, every perception. Let the sense of being a fixed, separate entity soften into being a flowing process — a wave in the ocean of existence.
  8. Walking meditation: Walk slowly. With each step, feel: the ground pushes up, the body pushes down — it is one movement. The air enters, the breath leaves — it is one movement. Where do “you” end and the world begin? Nowhere. You are a wave; the world is the ocean. One movement, not two.
  9. The key feeling: The technique is not about THINKING “I am a wave.” It is about FEELING the universal energy flowing through you. Feel it in the heartbeat (the universe pumping blood). Feel it in the thoughts (the universe thinking through this brain). Feel it in emotions (the universe feeling through this form). Nothing is personal; everything is universal expressed personally.
  10. Gratitude naturally arises: When you feel the universal flowing through you, gratitude comes spontaneously. Not gratitude TO anyone — just gratitude for being a form through which the universe experiences itself. You are the universe’s way of looking at the sunset, tasting the fruit, hearing the music. What a privilege to be this particular wave.

  • Expecting the feeling to be constant immediately — like all techniques, this builds gradually. Moments of connection become longer over time.

Questions From The Book

If I am just a wave, do I have free will?

Chapter 40, "Universal Waves"

Osho says: the question of free will arises only for someone who believes they are separate from the ocean. A wave has no “free will” separate from the ocean because it IS the ocean. But the ocean itself is free — infinitely creative, infinitely spontaneous. When you realise you ARE the ocean (not separate from it), you don’t lose freedom — you gain the ocean’s freedom. It’s not that you have no will; it’s that your will and the universal will are discovered to be one. The conflict between “my will” and “fate” dissolves because there is only one movement, one will, one flow — and you are it.

Modern Commentary

What this is really pointing at: This is the experience of “flow state” (Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi) made into a permanent perspective rather than a temporary state. In flow, the sense of separate self dissolves and action happens effortlessly. This technique cultivates that dissolution as a continuous way of being — not just during peak performance but during all of life.

Systems theory: Modern systems thinking confirms that no organism is truly separate from its environment. You are a “dissipative structure” (Ilya Prigogine) — a temporary pattern maintained by constant flow of energy and matter. The metaphor of “wave in the ocean” is not poetic licence; it is closer to physical reality than the commonsense notion of being a separate, self-contained entity.

Technique 84 of 112
Category: Movement · Tags: bodyless · everywhere · joy · disidentification · expansion
Toss attachment for body aside, realizing I am everywhere. One who is everywhere is joyous.

In Plain English

Deliberately relax the grip on “I am this body.” Feel: I am not contained in this body. I am the awareness that USES this body but is not limited to it. Feel yourself as the space the body moves in, not as the body itself. One who is everywhere has nothing to gain, nothing to lose, nothing to protect. That is why joy arises — joy is the natural state when there is no fear, and fear only exists for something that can be harmed. Awareness cannot be harmed.

How to Practice

  1. Find a quiet, private place: This is an eyes-closed practice. Sit or lie comfortably. You need 25 minutes of undisturbed time. The body should be relaxed, comfortable, and forgotten as much as possible — no tight clothing, no uncomfortable posture.
  2. Scan and release the body: Spend 5 minutes doing a body scan from head to toes. With each area, deliberately RELEASE your identification with it. Not “I am relaxing my arm” but “this arm is not me.” Feel each body part becoming “not-self” — present, but not who you are.
  3. Feel the space around the body: Now shift attention from the body to the SPACE the body occupies. Feel the air touching your skin. Feel the room around you. Let your sense of “I” move from being inside the skin to being the space surrounding the skin.
  4. Expand the sense of “I”: Slowly, feel yourself as bigger than the body. First, expand to fill the room. Then the building. Then the neighbourhood. Don’t THINK it — FEEL it. Feel yourself as vast, spacious, unbounded. The body becomes a tiny point within you — you are no longer within the body.
  5. “Toss attachment aside”: The sutra says “toss” — not gently release but actively throw away. There is a decisiveness here. It’s like removing a costume: just take it off and toss it. The body is a costume awareness is wearing. Toss the attachment to it. Not the body itself — the ATTACHMENT.
  6. Realise: I am everywhere: When the body-identification loosens sufficiently, a realisation dawns: if I am not this body, then where am I? Everywhere. Awareness has no location. It is the space in which everything appears. YOU are that space. Feel it: I am everywhere. Not metaphorically — literally. Awareness is here, there, everywhere simultaneously.
  7. Notice the joy: As the sense of being everywhere stabilises, notice: joy arises by itself. Not happiness about something — causeless joy. This is because fear has dissolved. Fear requires a thing that can be harmed. If you are everywhere, you cannot be harmed — you are too vast, too all-pervading. Without fear, the natural state is joy.
  8. Rest in the joy: Don’t try to intensify the joy or hold onto it. Just notice it and let it be. It is your natural state — what you feel when the artificial contraction of body-identification relaxes. Stay here for 5–10 minutes.
  9. Return with awareness: Slowly, bring attention back to the body. Feel the breath. Feel the weight. Open your eyes. But carry the knowing: the body is IN you — you are not in the body. This knowing can remain even as you return to normal activity.
  10. Daily integration: Throughout the day, whenever you feel fear or contraction, ask: “Am I really just this body?” The question alone loosens the grip. Even a moment of remembering “I am everywhere” restores spaciousness and dissolves the contraction of fear.

Technique 85 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: nothing · void · emptiness · negation · dissolution
Thinking no thing will limited-self unlimit.

In Plain English

This is a paradoxical instruction — closer to a Zen koan than a meditation. How can you “think” no thing? The very effort breaks the mind. Try to think no-thing and thinking exhausts itself trying. When thought has no object, when nothing remains for thought to hold, the “limited self” (which is built out of thoughts and the things they cling to) loses its boundaries and becomes unlimited. Osho stresses this is not blanking the mind by force, nor concentrating on the word “nothing.” It is using the impossibility of the instruction to dissolve the very act of thinking.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in total stillness: Find a position you can hold without moving for 30 minutes. Eyes closed. Spine erect. Let the body become so still that you forget it. Five minutes of settling.
  2. Notice that thoughts are things: A thought arises — it is a THING. It has shape, duration, content. Recognise it as a thing. Don’t fight it, don’t suppress it — simply see that it is a thing. Then let it go. It dissolves on its own when you stop feeding it attention.
  3. Notice that sensations are things: An itch, a pressure, warmth, tingling — these are all things. Recognise each one as a thing. You are not these things. Let them be without identifying with them.
  4. Notice that images are things: A mental picture, a memory, a fantasy — these are things. They arise in you but they are not you. Let each one pass like a cloud through empty sky.
  5. Notice that emotions are things: Boredom, restlessness, peace, bliss — even pleasant states are things. Don’t cling to pleasant things or push away unpleasant ones. They are all things. Let them all go.
  6. Notice that blankness is a thing: This is the crucial trap. The mind may produce a state of blankness and say “This is it! This is no-thingness!” But blankness has a quality, a texture, a dullness — it too is a thing. Let it go.
  7. Rest as the space: When all things have been released, what remains? Not nothing — YOU remain. But not “you” as a thing. You as the SPACE in which things appear. This space is alive, aware, present — but it has no form, no boundary, no content. It is no-thingness.
  8. Don’t grab the experience: The moment you think “I’ve got it!” that thought is a thing and you’ve lost it. No-thingness cannot be possessed because the possessor is also a thing. Simply rest. Don’t claim. Don’t name.
  9. FEEL rather than think: Osho emphasises — this technique fails completely if you stay intellectual. The mind cannot understand no-thingness because the mind IS things. You must feel your way into the space between thoughts, and then let even the feeler dissolve.
  10. Practice for 30 minutes daily: This technique may take months or years to bear fruit. That is normal for something this advanced. Even brief glimpses of no-thingness — even half a second — are transformative. Don’t measure progress.

Technique 109 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: suppose · passive · form · empty
Suppose your passive form to be an empty room with walls of skin – empty.

In Plain English

Sit completely still — not as a doer, but as a body that has nothing inside. The skin is a wall; the room within those walls is empty. Thoughts may keep coming, like clouds passing through an empty sky — they do not belong to anyone. Don’t try to be empty by an act; be empty by suspending all action. As Buddha put it, do not meditate — be in meditation.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in a passive, locked posture. Osho recommends siddhasana — cross-legged with one foot tucked, hands folded one on top of the other in the lap, eyes closed, spine straight. Any closed cross-legged posture works. The body should be hung on the spine, not held by muscle.
  2. Spend a few minutes settling. Don’t set a goal. Don’t intend to achieve anything. The whole technique depends on passivity — the moment you start doing, a doer comes into being and the technique is lost.
  3. Now imagine the body as a room with walls of skin. The skin is the only boundary; behind it, where you usually feel a self, is empty space.
  4. Don’t imagine a person in the room imagining the empty room. There is no one inside. The room is just a room.
  5. Thoughts will keep arising. They will. Osho says this is just “old habit, old momentum.” Don’t fight them and don’t identify. Treat each thought as a cloud crossing an empty sky — rootless, belonging to no-one. Watch them pass without owning them.
  6. Resist the mind’s standard moves: catching a thought, expanding it, fighting it, judging it. Each of these requires a doer; bring no doer. Just “there is no one to do anything with this thought.”
  7. Stay long enough that the thoughts begin to slow on their own. The clouds thin out. Gaps begin to appear — a thought, an interval, a thought, an interval. In those gaps, for the first time, you taste the emptiness.
  8. Don’t name the gap. The moment you say “I am empty” the gap has refilled with you. Just rest in the cloudless space, without commentary.
  9. Notice that disturbances need a self to be disturbing. Traffic, voices, a child playing — these only become disturbance because there is someone inside the room reacting. With the room empty, sound passes through and leaves nothing wounded. Test this with whatever sounds your room contains.
  10. Don’t hurry to end. The longer you can stay, the more reliably the gaps appear. With time the proportion shifts: more empty room, fewer clouds.
  11. If you find yourself trying to “be empty,” you have started doing again. Drop the trying. Simply continue not interfering with anything.
  12. End the sitting slowly. Open the eyes. Notice that for some moments after, the world too looks “through” you rather than at you — the empty room has not yet refilled. Carry this flavour as long as it lasts.

Osho’s Key Insight

These techniques are concerned with emptiness – they are the most delicate, the most subtle. Even to conceive of emptiness seems impossible. Buddha used all these four techniques for his disciples and bhikkhus, and because of these four techniques he was totally misunderstood. Buddhism got completely uprooted from Indian soil just because of these four techniques.

Source: 79. The Philosophy of Emptiness, The Book of Secrets

Technique 110 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: gracious · play · universe · empty
Gracious one, play. The universe is an empty shell wherein your mind frolics infinitely.

In Plain English

You can sit empty for an hour, but the rest of the day is action. So Tantra adds a companion technique: take the same empty universe into activity — by playing rather than working. Play has no goal; the doing is its own meaning. Drop the result, drop the seriousness, and the same emptiness that you found in passive sitting becomes the medium of your day.

How to Practice

  1. This technique pairs with sutra #109 (the empty room). One is for passive sitting; this one is for the rest of life. Use them together.
  2. Start with the recognition. Look at children playing. There is no profit, no destination, no calculation. The football match could be decided by tossing a coin and the result would be the same — but they would not get to play. The play is the point. Hold this image as the standard.
  3. Pick one daily task and treat it as play for one whole day. Cooking, walking, working at a desk, talking with a colleague. Do it with the quality of a child throwing pebbles into a pond — engaged, absorbed, but with no eye on the result.
  4. Notice the difference: when the same activity becomes work, you carry the future as a weight; when it becomes play, the present is the whole event. Future may still come, but you are no longer dragged forward by it.
  5. Drop seriousness, not engagement. Osho is precise: the player is intensely engaged — the children are throwing themselves fully — but they are not serious. Be intense without being serious.
  6. Watch the mind do its thing as you play. It will frolic with thoughts: profit fantasies, worries, judgements. The sutra calls these mind-frolics infinite. Don’t crush them; just don’t take them as commands. Let them play too.
  7. Don’t feel guilty about “bad” thoughts and don’t feel proud of “great” ones. Both are mind-froth, frolicking. Osho says even the noble thought of saving humanity is an overflow, not a fact about you.
  8. Apply this to relationships too. The spouse is not a project; the friend is not a strategy. Be playful with them — present, light, engaged, without calculation.
  9. Apply it to ambitions you cannot drop. You may still need to earn money, finish a thesis, complete a deal — do them. But change the quality from work to play. Krishna’s instruction to Arjuna in the Gita, says Osho, is exactly this: act fully, leave the result in the divine’s hands.
  10. Watch what happens to fatigue. Work tires; play does not, even when it lasts longer. The same act, done with the empty-shell quality, refreshes instead of draining.
  11. Pair this technique with the previous one. Sit some part of the day in the empty-room sutra; spend the rest of the day in this empty-shell, playful sutra. The two are sides of one coin: emptiness in stillness, emptiness in movement.
  12. Don’t turn this into a new seriousness. The very moment you say “I am playing my way through life as a discipline,” play has died. Stay loose. The technique is light by nature; carry it lightly.

Technique 112 of 112
Category: Movement · Tags: space · support · eternal · stillness · absolute
Enter space, supportless, eternal, still.

In Plain English

This is the final movement technique — movement into absolute stillness. Enter SPACE — not physical space but the space of awareness itself. It is supportless (nothing holds it up — it holds everything). It is eternal (it was here before birth and will be here after death). It is still (thoughts move within it but it itself never moves). Just enter it. Don’t look for support, don’t look for ground. Be groundless, supportless, floating in the eternal still space that you are.

How to Practice

  1. Understand what is meant by “space”: Not the physical space between objects. Not outer space. But the inner space of awareness — the infinite field in which all experience appears. Thoughts appear in it. Sensations appear in it. Emotions appear in it. But IT is always still, always present, always unchanged. This is what you are entering.
  2. Sit in absolute stillness: This technique requires physical stillness as a gateway to inner stillness. Sit in a comfortable position and resolve: I will not move for 30 minutes. Not a finger. Not an eyelid if possible. Let the body become a statue. Physical stillness creates the conditions for discovering the stillness that is always present within.
  3. Release all support: Normally, awareness clings to supports: the body (“I am here”), thoughts (“I am thinking this”), identity (“I am so-and-so”), time (“it is Tuesday”). One by one, release these supports. Stop relying on the body as your anchor. Stop relying on thoughts as your identity. Stop relying on time to orient you. Let go of EVERY support.
  4. The terror of supportlessness: When supports are released, fear may arise — a vertigo, as if falling into infinite space with nothing to hold. This is normal. It is the ego’s panic at losing all its reference points. Breathe through it. The fear is temporary; what lies beyond it is eternal peace. You are not falling — you are discovering that you never needed support in the first place.
  5. Discover: you ARE the support: The fear passes when you realise: awareness does not NEED support because it IS the support. Everything else rests in awareness — the body, thoughts, the world. Awareness rests in nothing. It is self-existing, self-luminous, self-sufficient. It is the ground of all grounds. When you stop looking for support OUTSIDE awareness, you discover that awareness is supportless — and that this is freedom, not terror.
  6. Feel the eternity: This space — this awareness — has no beginning and no end. It was not born with the body and will not die with the body. Feel this: not as a belief but as a direct recognition. This awareness that is present right now has ALWAYS been present. It never changes. Bodies change, thoughts change, worlds change — but this awareness is eternal, unchanging, still.
  7. Enter the stillness: Awareness itself never moves. Thoughts move within it (like fish in an ocean) but the ocean itself is still. Sensations arise and pass (like waves on a surface) but the depths are motionless. Enter those depths. Sink below the waves of thought and sensation into the absolute stillness of pure awareness. It is always here — you have only to stop being distracted by the surface activity.
  8. Rest without reference: In this space there is no time, no location, no identity. You cannot say “I am here” because there is no “here.” You cannot say “it has been 10 minutes” because there is no time. You cannot say “I am [name]” because there is no personal identity. Just pure being. Pure awareness. Pure space. Rest here.
  9. Duration: Stay as long as the stillness holds — 5 minutes, 30 minutes, an hour. When the surface mind begins to stir again (and it will), gently let it. You have tasted the eternal. It doesn’t go anywhere. You can return anytime.
  10. Return: When ready to return to ordinary activity, do so slowly. Feel the body. Feel time returning. Feel identity reassembling. But now you know: beneath all of that, the supportless, eternal, still space remains — always. You can rest in it at any moment by simply releasing your grip on supports.
