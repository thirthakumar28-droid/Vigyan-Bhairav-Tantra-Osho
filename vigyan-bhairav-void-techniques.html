The Techniques for Emptiness & Void

Eight techniques that use emptiness, surrender, exhaustion, and dissolution as doorways to the void. Shunyata is not nothingness — it is pregnant emptiness, the womb from which all existence arises and returns.

8 techniques · ~10 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These eight techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The void and emptiness sutras form the seventh major category — using deprivation, exhaustion, devotion, sky-gazing, and the investigation of identity to dissolve the ego into the pregnant emptiness that underlies all things.

These techniques approach awakening through subtraction rather than addition. Where other categories build something (light, sound, centering), these techniques REMOVE everything until only the void remains. The void is not frightening — it is the most peaceful, the most liberating, the most total state available to consciousness.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These void techniques work by REMOVAL, not by addition. You are not creating emptiness — you are discovering it was always there, hidden behind identifications, desires, and knowledge.

New to void meditation?

Start with Technique 6 — without support, sit on buttocks or Technique 8 — looking into blue sky. Both are simple, physical, and produce quick results. Spend 20–40 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for the deeper techniques.

Finding the still point within the body — the axis around which all movement happens but which itself never moves.

Releasing the ego’s grip — not passivity but active letting-go. The ego cannot surrender itself; surrender happens when effort is exhausted.

Technique 82 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: identification · organs · felt-sense · body · ownership
Feel: My thought, I-ness, internal organs — me.

In Plain English

Feel into your internal organs — heart beating, lungs breathing, belly digesting. Then ask: am I these? Feel into thoughts arising. Am I these? Feel into the sense of “I.” Am I even this? Each layer is investigated and found to be an OBJECT of awareness, not awareness itself. The void opens behind all identifications.

How to Practice

  1. Find a quiet space: Sit comfortably with eyes closed. Settle the body with 5–7 deep breaths. Let the surface mind quiet. Don’t rush into the technique — give yourself 3–5 minutes of simple breathing first.
  2. Feel the internal organs: Bring awareness to your heart — feel it beating. Don’t think about it, FEEL the pulse from inside. Move to your lungs — feel them expanding, contracting. Feel the belly working, digesting. Feel the blood moving through vessels. Spend 5 minutes simply FEELING your internal machinery at work.
  3. Ask the first question: “Am I these organs?” Not intellectually — FEEL the question. The heart beats — but you can observe it beating. If you can observe it, you cannot BE it. The observer is separate from the observed. FEEL this separation. The organs are objects in your awareness.
  4. Move to thoughts: Now notice thoughts arising. Watch them come and go like clouds. Ask: “Am I these thoughts?” Again, FEEL it — if you can watch a thought appear and disappear, you are not the thought. You are the space in which thoughts move. Let this sink in deeply — not as philosophy but as felt experience.
  5. Move to the “I” feeling: This is the subtlest layer. Feel the sense of “I am” — the raw feeling of existing, of being someone. Now ask: “Am I even this?” Can you observe the I-feeling itself? If yes, then even the I-sense is an OBJECT — something witnessed, not the witness.
  6. Rest in what remains: When you have peeled away organs, thoughts, and I-ness — what remains? Don’t answer with words. Don’t conceptualise. Just REST in whatever is left. It will feel like nothing — and that nothing is the void. It is spacious, peaceful, without centre or boundary.
  7. Stay in the void: Remain in this emptiness for as long as it holds naturally. Don’t try to maintain it — if thoughts return, simply notice them as objects again, and the void reopens. Each time you notice an object AS an object, the void behind it becomes visible.
  8. The neti-neti process: This is essentially the ancient Upanishadic method — “not this, not this.” You are not rejecting anything; you are simply seeing clearly that each layer is YOURS but not YOU. The body is yours — not you. Thoughts are yours — not you. Even I-ness is yours — not you.
  9. Return gently: When ready, allow the I-sense to reconstitute naturally. Feel the body again. Open eyes slowly. The insight remains: everything you thought was “you” is actually something you HAVE. What you ARE is the void itself — the empty awareness that has all these things.
  10. Daily integration: Throughout the day, periodically ask yourself: “What am I right now?” and instead of answering, simply feel the question dissolve into spaciousness. Each inquiry is a micro-taste of the void.

Signs It’s Working

  • A clear felt sense that you can observe your own organs “from outside”
  • Thoughts begin to feel like weather — passing through, not personal
  • The I-sense becomes lighter, less solid, more transparent
  • Moments of spacious emptiness that feel profoundly restful
  • A background sense of “I am not this body” that persists into daily life
  • Reduced reactivity — emotions arise but are seen as objects, not as “me”

Common Pitfalls

  • Making it intellectual rather than felt — philosophising about identity instead of FEELING into it
  • Creating a new identity as “the void” — the void is not another thing to become; it is what remains when becoming stops
  • Dissociating from the body (numbing out) instead of clearly seeing it as an object — you should feel MORE, not less
  • Rushing through the layers without really settling into each one
  • Expecting a dramatic experience — the void is often experienced as profound ordinariness

Questions From The Book

How is this different from dissociation or depersonalisation?

Chapter 58, "Investigating Identity"

Osho clarifies: in dissociation, awareness CONTRACTS away from experience — you feel numb, disconnected, frightened. In this technique, awareness EXPANDS to include experience — you feel everything more clearly but recognise it as object rather than self. The test is simple: are you feeling MORE or LESS? If less, you are dissociating. If more, you are awakening. True void is full of feeling — it simply doesn’t identify with what it feels.

What does “I-ness” mean here?

Chapter 58

Osho explains: “I-ness” (ahamkara in Sanskrit) is not any particular thought but the background FEELING of being a separate entity. It is the feeling “I exist as someone distinct.” Even when all particular thoughts stop, this subtle feeling of separate existence usually continues. This sutra asks you to investigate even THAT — to ask whether even the sense of being “someone” is just another object that arises in the void of awareness.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is the core method of Advaita Vedanta — self-inquiry. Everything that can be observed cannot be the observer. By systematically recognising each layer of experience as observed rather than observer, you arrive at the observer itself — which turns out to be empty of all qualities, yet aware. Modern neuroscience calls this “minimal phenomenal experience” — awareness stripped of all content.

Research parallel: Studies on meditation-induced ego dissolution show that experienced practitioners can reliably enter states where the sense of self-boundary disappears while awareness remains. fMRI shows reduced default mode network activity (the “self-network”) during these states. This technique provides a systematic path to that dissolution.

One-line essenceYou are not your thoughts, not your organs, not even your I-ness. Peel everything away and what remains — the void that witnesses all — is what you truly are.
Technique 83 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: before-desire · before-knowing · prior-self · dissolution · beauty
Before desire and before knowing, how can I say I am? Consider. Dissolve in the beauty of that.

In Plain English

Before any desire arose in you, before you knew anything — WHAT WERE YOU? Not your name (that came later), not your body-image (that developed), not your desires (they weren’t born yet). Go back before all of it. What remains? A vast, beautiful emptiness — the void from which you arose. Dissolve into that beauty. This is your original face before your parents were born.

How to Practice

  1. Create sacred space: Sit in a quiet, dimly lit room. Light a candle if it helps establish reverence. This technique requires deep inward attention — eliminate all possible distractions. Allow 30 minutes minimum.
  2. Settle deeply: Close your eyes. Breathe slowly and deeply for 5 minutes. Let the body become very still. Let the mind slow down. You need to reach a contemplative depth before this inquiry will work — surface mind cannot access these layers.
  3. Remember your earliest desire: Go back in memory. What is the earliest desire you can remember? Wanting a toy? Wanting mother’s attention? Feel that desire. Now ask: BEFORE that desire existed, what were you? You existed before the desire arose — something was there that COULD desire. What was it?
  4. Go before knowledge: What is the earliest thing you “knew”? Your name? Language? The feel of warmth? Go before ALL of that. Before you knew anything at all — before language, before concepts, before any framework of understanding existed in you — WHAT WERE YOU?
  5. Feel, don’t think: The mind will try to answer: “I was a baby,” “I was consciousness,” “I was nothing.” Reject ALL answers. This is not a question that CAN be answered by the mind. It is a question designed to short-circuit the mind. Let the question dissolve all answers.
  6. The dissolving: As you feel into this impossible question — what was I before all desire and all knowing? — something begins to dissolve. The constructed self (which is MADE of desires and knowledge) begins to melt. What’s left is not nothing — it’s a vast, beautiful, empty awareness. The sutra says: “dissolve in the beauty of THAT.”
  7. Stay with the beauty: This void is not cold or frightening. The sutra specifically calls it BEAUTIFUL. When you touch it, there is a quality of pristine, innocent beauty — like fresh snow, like a clear dawn, like a newborn’s eyes. Let that beauty hold you. Dissolve INTO it rather than observing it from outside.
  8. The original face: What you are touching is what Zen calls your “original face” — what you were before conditioning, before desire, before knowledge, before identity was constructed. It is not a thing — it is the void that holds all things. And it is YOURS. It is what you have always been, underneath everything else.
  9. Return slowly: When the meditation ends naturally (or after 30 minutes), let desires and knowledge return gradually. Notice them arriving like guests returning to an empty house. The house is not the guests — you are the house. Empty, spacious, welcoming, but not defined by what it contains.
  10. Carry the question: Throughout the day, occasionally ask: “Before this desire, what am I?” Each time, the void flashes for an instant. These micro-glimpses accumulate until the void becomes your constant background.

  • Treating it casually — this technique requires deep contemplative absorption; surface attention won’t reach it
  • Confusing philosophical understanding with experiential dissolution — THINKING about emptiness is not dissolving into it

Questions From The Book

Why does the sutra say “dissolve in the beauty” rather than just “dissolve”?

Chapter 59, "Before Desire"

Osho explains: the word “beauty” is essential because it prevents the common misunderstanding that the void is ugly, frightening, or nihilistic. When you actually experience the state before desire and knowledge, it is not cold emptiness — it is the most beautiful thing you have ever encountered. It is pristine, innocent, fresh, alive. The sutra uses “beauty” to reassure the seeker: what you will find is not death but the most alive, most beautiful state possible. It is the beauty of absolute purity.

What is the connection to the Zen koan “original face”?

Chapter 59

Osho notes this sutra is essentially the same teaching as the famous Zen koan: “What was your original face before your parents were born?” Both point to the same reality — the unconditioned awareness prior to all identity. But where Zen delivers this as a paradox to break the mind, the tantra delivers it gently: “Consider. Dissolve in the beauty.” Same destination, different approaches — Zen is the lightning method, tantra is the river method.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Identity is constructed from desires (motivation) and knowledge (cognition). If you remove both, the constructed self dissolves. What remains is what developmental psychologists call the “core self” — the pre-verbal, pre-conceptual sense of being that exists before language and socialization shape it into a personality. This technique is a direct route to that primordial ground.

Developmental parallel: Research on infant consciousness suggests that before the “narrative self” develops (around age 2-3), infants experience what some researchers describe as “pure presence” — awareness without story, identity, or temporal continuity. This technique asks the adult to touch that same quality of raw, storyless presence.

One-line essenceBefore your first desire, before your first knowing — you were already here. That pristine emptiness before the story began is still here, underneath everything. Dissolve into its beauty.
Technique 84 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: exhaustion · dropping · surrender · wholeness · effort-to-effortlessness
Roam about until exhausted and then, dropping to the ground, in this dropping be whole.

In Plain English

Move the body vigorously — dance wildly, run, spin, do Osho’s Dynamic Meditation. Continue until the body is utterly exhausted and DROPS of its own accord. In that dropping — when effort is impossible — the ego also drops. You become whole because the doer has been exhausted into silence. The void reveals itself when there is no energy left to maintain the ego.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your method of exhaustion: Wild dancing, running, vigorous shaking, spinning (Sufi-style), Osho’s Dynamic Meditation, or simply roaming/walking intensely. The specific movement doesn’t matter — what matters is that it exhausts the body TOTALLY.
  2. Create a safe space: You need a space where you can move wildly without self-consciousness or danger. A private room with padded floor, a secluded garden, a beach at dawn. Remove obstacles you could trip over. Wear minimal, comfortable clothing.
  3. Begin with intensity: Start moving with TOTAL intensity. Not half-hearted — give 100%. If dancing, dance as if possessed. If running, run full-speed. If shaking, shake every cell. The key is: hold nothing back. The ego maintains itself through reserves of energy; exhaust ALL reserves.
  4. Continue past comfort: When the body says “enough,” continue. When the mind says “I can’t,” continue. The first “I can’t” is a lie — the body has reserves. Push through the first barrier. And the second. Continue until the body GENUINELY cannot continue — not out of choice but out of true physical impossibility.
  5. The dropping: When exhaustion is total, LET yourself drop. Don’t lower yourself carefully — DROP. Fall to the ground. Collapse. The dropping must be total, sudden, without control. Like a puppet whose strings are cut. This is the critical moment.
  6. In the dropping, be whole: The moment you drop — in that instant of total surrender to gravity — FEEL the wholeness. The ego, which requires energy to maintain its divisions and tensions, simply cannot function when all energy is spent. In that powerless state, you are whole because there is no energy to maintain the fragments.
  7. Lie still on the ground: After dropping, lie completely still. Face down or face up — however you fell. Don’t adjust, don’t make yourself comfortable. The body will feel like it’s melting into the earth. Let it. Breathe heavily — that’s natural. But don’t move. Stay 15–20 minutes in this absolute stillness.
  8. Observe the void: In the stillness after exhaustion, notice: the mind is empty. Not because you forced it to be empty, but because there is simply no energy left to think. Thoughts require energy. When energy is spent, thoughts stop. This is the most effortless meditation — emptiness achieved through total effort.
  9. Feel the wholeness: Without the ego’s constant dividing activity, you feel whole — undivided, unfragmented, simply and purely present. No inner conflict, no “I should” or “I shouldn’t,” no past or future. Just this: the ground, the body, the breath, the void. Total.
  10. Rise slowly: When energy begins to return, rise very slowly. Notice: as energy returns, the ego also returns. Watch it reassembling itself — first the body-sense, then preferences, then memories, then the “I.” Seeing this reassembly AFTER having been without it is deeply instructive. The ego is clearly seen as a construction, not a given.

Questions From The Book

Why does exhaustion lead to wholeness?

Chapter 59, "Dropping Into Wholeness"

Osho explains: the ego is an activity, not a thing. It requires constant energy to maintain — dividing experience into “me” and “not-me,” judging, controlling, resisting. When all energy is exhausted, the ego simply cannot perform its activity. It stops. And when the ego stops, what remains is wholeness — because the only thing preventing wholeness was the ego’s constant dividing. You don’t ACHIEVE wholeness; you simply exhaust the obstacle to it.

Is this why Osho created Dynamic Meditation?

Chapter 59

Osho confirms: Dynamic Meditation is a modern systematisation of this ancient sutra. The first three stages (chaotic breathing, catharsis, jumping with “Hoo!”) exhaust the body and release emotional blocks. The fourth stage (freeze/stop) IS the dropping. The fifth stage (celebration) is the wholeness expressed. The sutra gives the principle; Dynamic Meditation gives the method. For modern people with sedentary lives and repressed energy, the structured approach of Dynamic Meditation may be more effective than simply “roaming about.”

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The ego/self-model requires metabolic energy to maintain. Research on ego-depletion shows that self-control, decision-making, and identity-maintenance all consume glucose. When physical exhaustion depletes these resources, the self-model temporarily simplifies or dissolves. This technique uses that biological reality deliberately — exhaust the fuel that powers the ego, and the ego stops.

Parallel practices: Sufi whirling, ecstatic dance traditions, marathon running (“runner’s high” as ego-dissolution), and various indigenous dance-until-you-drop ceremonies all use the same principle. The body becomes the vehicle for transcendence when pushed beyond its normal operating limits.

One-line essenceThe ego needs energy to maintain its illusion of separateness. Exhaust all energy, drop to the ground, and discover: without the effort of being someone, you are whole.
Technique 85 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: deprivation · transcend · letting-go · weakness · surrender
Suppose you are gradually being deprived of strength or of knowledge. At the instant of deprivation, transcend.

In Plain English

Imagine strength leaving the body — muscles dissolving, power fading. OR imagine knowledge leaving — certainties dissolving, opinions evaporating, all you “know” disappearing. At the MOMENT nothing remains — no strength, no knowledge — something transcends. The void is reached not by gaining but by losing everything.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your path — strength or knowledge: This sutra offers two approaches. Try both over different sessions, then commit to whichever resonates more deeply. Strength-deprivation works better for body-identified people; knowledge-deprivation for intellectuals.
  2. PATH A — Deprivation of strength: Lie down comfortably. Close your eyes. Feel your body as it is — its solidity, its musculature, its capacity for action. Now begin to FEEL that strength draining away. Start with the feet — feel them becoming empty, powerless, weightless.
  3. Let weakness rise: The emptying of strength rises slowly through the body — calves dissolving, thighs becoming hollow, pelvis emptying of power. Feel the belly losing its strength, the chest becoming light and insubstantial. Arms losing their grip, hands becoming feather-light, unable to hold anything.
  4. Complete deprivation: Continue until the entire body feels utterly powerless — as if you couldn’t lift a finger if your life depended on it. Not tense weakness but RELEASED weakness — like a rag doll, like a deflated balloon. No strength anywhere. Completely helpless.
  5. PATH B — Deprivation of knowledge: Sit comfortably, eyes closed. Begin by feeling all your certainties. Your name, your age, your beliefs, your opinions, your skills, your memories. FEEL how much you “know.” Now begin to let it dissolve. Start with opinions — feel them evaporating. Then beliefs. Then facts. Then memories.
  6. Feel knowledge dissolving: Let your name dissolve — you no longer know what you are called. Let language dissolve — words lose meaning. Let all concepts dissolve — categories become meaningless. Let even the knowledge of WHERE you are dissolve. Who you are. WHAT you are. All dissolving into emptiness.
  7. The instant of total deprivation: In BOTH paths, arrive at a point where NOTHING remains. No strength to act. Or no knowledge to orient. In that instant — that precise moment of complete deprivation — something transcends. Not “you” transcend — the “you” has been deprived of everything. SOMETHING ELSE transcends. The void itself becomes alive.
  8. Don’t try to transcend: The sutra says “at the instant of deprivation, transcend” — but this is a description, not a command. You cannot DO transcendence. You can only create the conditions for it. The conditions are: total deprivation. When nothing remains, transcendence happens on its own.
  9. Rest in whatever opens: After the moment of transcendence (which may be subtle or dramatic), rest in whatever space has opened. Don’t analyse it. Don’t grab at it. Let it be what it is. It may feel like floating in space, like being nothing, like infinite peace, like silent aliveness.
  10. Return gradually: Allow strength or knowledge to return naturally. Don’t rush back. Notice: as they return, the ego reassembles around them. Strength and knowledge are the building blocks of the ego. Without them, there is only the void. With them, there is a “someone.” You now know the choice is available.

Questions From The Book

Why does Shiva say “suppose” — is this just imagination?

Chapter 60, "Deprivation and Transcendence"

Osho explains: the word “suppose” is a skillful device. If Shiva said “be deprived,” the ego would resist violently. But “suppose” gives the mind permission — “it’s only hypothetical, only imagination.” The ego relaxes. And then the supposing becomes real. Because when you FEEL yourself truly without strength, even in imagination, the body responds as if it were real. The nervous system doesn’t distinguish between vividly imagined and actual deprivation. So “suppose” is the Trojan horse that gets past the ego’s defences.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The ego maintains itself through two pillars: the capacity to act (strength) and the capacity to know (knowledge). Remove both, and the ego structure cannot sustain itself. This is a guided approach to what naturally happens in deep surrender states, near-death experiences, and moments of total overwhelm — but here it is done deliberately, safely, and reversibly.

Neuroscience parallel: Research on “flow states” shows that when the sense of agency (strength) and the internal narrative (knowledge/stories) both quiet down, people report timelessness, egolessness, and transcendent states. This technique achieves the same result through imaginative deprivation rather than optimal performance.

One-line essenceThe void is not reached by gaining but by losing. Lose all strength. Lose all knowing. At the moment nothing is left, something beyond both is revealed.
Technique 86 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: devotion · surrender · heart · letting-go · love
Devotion frees.

In Plain English

The shortest sutra in the entire tantra — just two words. Devotion to WHAT? Osho says: it doesn’t matter. Devotion itself frees — the object is irrelevant. Devotion to a master, to God, to a flower, to existence. The quality of devotion — total self-giving — creates the void because when you give yourself totally, there is no self left. The “I” empties into the beloved.

How to Practice

  1. Find your object of devotion: Choose something or someone you can pour yourself into without reservation. A master, God (any form), nature, the sky, music, a beloved person, existence itself, truth. The WHAT is irrelevant — what matters is the QUALITY of giving. Choose whatever opens your heart most naturally.
  2. Begin with the heart: Sit or kneel. Bring attention to your heart centre. Feel whatever love, gratitude, or reverence naturally lives there. Don’t manufacture feelings — find what is already present and amplify it by giving it your full attention.
  3. Pour yourself out: Now begin to GIVE. Not giving something you have — giving yourself. Feel yourself flowing outward toward your chosen object. Like water pouring from a vessel. Like incense becoming smoke. Like a candle giving itself to the flame. Hold nothing back.
  4. Total self-giving: Continue pouring until there is nothing left to pour. Give your thoughts — let them flow toward the beloved. Give your feelings — let them empty into devotion. Give your body-sense — let even the sense of physical existence dissolve into the giving. Give your “I” — let the giver itself be given.
  5. Notice the void: When the giving is total — when you have poured yourself out completely — what remains? The vessel is empty. The “I” has been given away. There is no one left. THIS is the void. Not cold emptiness but warm, loving emptiness — the emptiness of having given everything.
  6. Don’t hold back a “witness”: The subtle trap is maintaining a watcher who observes the devotion. Real devotion has no witness — you are so totally given that there is no one left to watch. If you notice yourself watching, give THAT too. Give the watcher.
  7. Practise with prayer, music, or dance: For many, pure sitting devotion is too abstract at first. Sing devotional songs, pray aloud, dance in ecstasy, bow in prostration — whatever physical form helps the heart pour itself out. The body is a powerful vehicle for devotion.
  8. The paradox of devotion: Devotion frees because the “I” that needs freeing IS the problem. You cannot free yourself because the self IS the prison. But you can GIVE yourself away. And when the self is given away, freedom is what remains. Freedom is not something you GET — it is what is LEFT when the self is gone.
  9. Daily practice: Throughout the day, practise micro-devotion. When you see beauty, pour yourself into it. When you hear music, disappear into it. When you are with a loved one, give yourself completely. Each act of total self-giving is a taste of the void.
  10. For the intellectual: If devotion feels foreign or “unscientific,” try devotion to TRUTH. Pour yourself into the pursuit of truth with such totality that you disappear into it. Scientists at their best are in devotion — the ego dissolves into the inquiry. Any total absorption is devotion.

Technique 87 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: balance · unsupported · centering · sudden · body
Without support for feet or hands, sit only on the buttocks. Suddenly, the centering.

In Plain English

Sit balanced on buttocks alone — legs and arms free in the air, not touching anything. The moment physical balance is found, mental activity STOPS. In that stop is the void — brief but total. The body’s demand for balance creates an involuntary mind-gap. That gap is emptiness. Use it.

How to Practice

  1. Find a firm, flat surface: The floor works best. A mat or carpet provides slight padding for the sit-bones. You need a surface firm enough to balance on — a soft bed will not work.
  2. Sit and lift: Sit on the floor. Now lean back slightly and lift both feet off the ground. At the same time, lift your hands so they touch nothing — not the floor, not your legs, not anything. You are balanced entirely on your buttocks/sit-bones.
  3. Find the balance point: This is the key moment. The body will wobble. You will tip forward, backward, sideways. Keep adjusting. The body searches for equilibrium. During this search, notice: the mind becomes completely focused. ALL attention goes to the body’s balance. There is no room for thoughts.
  4. The moment of balance: When you find the exact balance point — when the body is still, unsupported, perfectly centred — the mind STOPS. Not gradually but SUDDENLY. The effort of finding balance occupies the mind completely, and when balance is found, the effort drops but the mind-gap remains. In that gap is the void.
  5. Stay in the gap: The mind-gap may last only seconds at first. That’s fine. Even a half-second of TOTAL mental silence is transformative. With practice, the gap extends. The body learns to balance more quickly, and the void-gap that follows balance lasts longer.
  6. V-sit variation: For a stronger challenge, lean back further and extend legs upward at roughly 45 degrees — the classic “boat pose” in yoga. Arms extended forward parallel to the floor. This demands more core strength and more precise balance, creating a more intense mind-stop.
  7. Repeat multiple times: One attempt may only give seconds of void. Do 10–15 attempts in a session. Each time: sit, lift, wobble, balance, STOP. Over time, you learn to “ride” the gap — to stay in the void even as the body maintains its position.
  8. Eyes open or closed: Try both. Eyes open makes physical balance easier; eyes closed makes it harder but produces a deeper mind-stop. Start with eyes open; progress to eyes closed as your core strength develops.
  9. The principle: When the body demands total attention for a physical challenge, the thinking mind has no spare capacity. And when the challenge is MET (balance found), the mind finds itself empty with nothing to do. That emptiness is the void. This technique uses PHYSICS to produce METAPHYSICS.
  10. Use throughout the day: Any moment of physical balance — standing on one foot, walking a curb, balancing on tiptoes to reach something — can become a micro-practice. Notice: balance demands presence, and presence is the doorway to emptiness.

Technique 88 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: single-object · empty-place · filling · knower · void-behind
In an empty place, fix mind on one object. You will not only be full of that object but will also know who is filled.

In Plain English

Go to an empty room or a desert or an open field — minimal sensory input. Fix attention on ONE object (a stone, a mark, a candle). Let it fill you completely. Then notice: WHO is filled? Behind the fullness of the object, there is a void — the empty awareness that RECEIVED the object. The object reveals the void by contrast. After filling, remove the object from attention — the void remains.

How to Practice

  1. Find an empty place: This is crucial. Not just “quiet” but EMPTY — minimal objects, minimal visual complexity, minimal sensory input. A white-walled room with nothing in it. A desert. A snow field. An empty beach at dawn. The emptiness of the environment mirrors and supports the emptiness you are seeking within.
  2. Place one object: In this empty space, place (or find) a single object. A stone. A candle flame. A dot on the wall. A flower. It should be simple, singular, and visually distinct from the empty background. The contrast between the ONE object and the EMPTY space is essential.
  3. Fix attention totally: Sit comfortably facing the object. Now fix your mind on it with total concentration. See nothing else. Think of nothing else. Let the object fill your entire field of awareness. Not forcefully — lovingly. Let the object become the whole world.
  4. Let it fill you: As concentration deepens, the object begins to “enter” you. The boundary between you (the seer) and it (the seen) thins. You become FULL of the object — saturated with its presence, its form, its reality. There is nothing else in consciousness except this stone, this flame, this dot.
  5. Now ask: WHO is filled? This is the pivot of the entire technique. You are full of the object — but WHO is full? There must be a SPACE that is filled, a CONTAINER that holds the object in awareness. Turn attention from the CONTENT (the object) to the CONTAINER (awareness itself). Who is receiving this object?
  6. Discover the void behind: When you turn attention to the “who,” you find... nothing. No one. Just empty, open awareness. The void. It was always there — the object merely made it visible by contrast. A room is visible because of the objects in it, but the SPACE of the room exists independent of objects. Similarly, the void of awareness exists independent of its contents.
  7. Remove the object: Now close your eyes (or turn away from the object). The object is gone from awareness. What remains? The void — the empty container. But now you KNOW it is there because the object revealed it. Without the object, the void might seem like “nothing.” After the object, the void is clearly felt as the SPACE in which objects appear.
  8. Rest in the void: Stay in this awareness of the void — the empty, open, knowing space that contains all experience but is itself empty. It is serene, peaceful, without boundary. It is what you actually ARE — the space, not the objects that pass through it.
  9. Alternate: For deeper practice, alternate: open eyes, fill with object, feel fullness. Close eyes, feel the void that was filled. Open eyes again, fill again. Close, void again. Each alternation makes the void more clearly felt. The object is the finger pointing at the moon — the void is the moon itself.
  10. Integration: Once this is understood experientially, you can do it anywhere. Any object can reveal the void behind it. Look at a tree and ask: “Who sees?” The seeing is happening in void-space. The tree reveals the void. Every perception becomes a doorway to emptiness.

  • Not concentrating deeply enough on the object — it must FILL you completely before the “who” question works
  • Making the “who is filled” question intellectual rather than felt — turn attention inward, don’t just think about it
  • Expecting to see or find SOMETHING behind the object — the void is not a thing; it is the absence of things
  • Skipping the filling phase and jumping straight to the void — the contrast is necessary; the object REVEALS the void

Why does the sutra specify “an empty place”?

Chapter 61, "The Void Behind Fullness"

Osho explains: if the environment is full of objects, the mind bounces between them and cannot achieve the singular concentration needed. But more subtly: the outer emptiness mirrors the inner emptiness you are seeking. In a cluttered environment, the mind feels cluttered. In an empty environment, the mind is already half-empty. The empty place is both practical (fewer distractions) and symbolic (it teaches by analogy). Chapter 61

Osho clarifies: in normal concentration, the goal IS the object — you want to become one with it (samadhi with seed). Here, the object is merely a TOOL. The real target is the void BEHIND the object — the awareness that receives it. Normal concentration ends with fullness; this technique uses fullness as a stepping-stone to the void. The “who is filled” question is what transforms concentration into self-knowledge.

Technique 89 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: sky · blue · serenity · beyond-clouds · simplicity
Simply by looking into the blue sky beyond clouds, the serenity.

In Plain English

Look at the sky — but specifically BEYOND the clouds, into the pure blue. The clouds are thoughts; the blue is the void. Don’t focus on clouds (thoughts) — see PAST them to the infinite blue emptiness. Applied internally: look past your thoughts to the space they move in. That space is the inner void. It is always serene because nothing disturbs it. Thoughts disturb themselves; the space is untouched.

How to Practice

  1. Find a sky with clouds: Unlike the pure-blue-sky technique (which requires cloudless sky), this one NEEDS clouds. The clouds are essential — they are the analogy for thoughts. You need both clouds AND blue to practise this. A partly cloudy day is ideal.
  2. Lie down or sit comfortably: Position yourself where you can see a large expanse of sky without straining your neck. Lying on your back is ideal. Let the body be completely relaxed and supported.
  3. Notice the clouds: First, see the clouds. Watch them move, change shape, dissolve, and reform. They are constantly shifting — never stable, never permanent. This is exactly like your thoughts: constantly shifting, reforming, dissolving.
  4. Now look BEYOND them: Shift your gaze — and your attention — from the clouds to the blue sky BEHIND them. The blue is always there, whether clouds cover it or not. It doesn’t move, doesn’t change, doesn’t react to the clouds. It simply IS. The clouds pass through it without affecting it.
  5. Feel the analogy: The blue sky is your awareness — the void. The clouds are your thoughts. Thoughts pass through awareness like clouds pass through sky. The sky is never disturbed by clouds, no matter how dark or turbulent. Similarly, the void of awareness is never disturbed by thoughts, no matter how intense.
  6. Apply it inwardly: Close your eyes. Now see your THOUGHTS as clouds. Don’t engage with them — notice them moving across the inner sky. And now look PAST them — look at the space they move in. The inner blue sky. The void. It is always there, always serene, always untouched. Thoughts cannot damage it any more than clouds can damage the sky.
  7. Rest in the blue: Both externally (staring past clouds) and internally (seeing past thoughts), REST your attention in the blue. Let the clouds/thoughts do whatever they do — they are not your concern. Your home is the sky, not the clouds. The void, not the thoughts.
  8. The serenity: The sutra says “the serenity” — note the article “the.” Not “a” serenity (one among many) but THE serenity — the absolute, the only, the ultimate. The void is by nature serene. Nothing can disturb it because it has no form to be disturbed. When you rest there, you share its nature. You BECOME serene.
  9. Duration: Start with 20 minutes and extend naturally. Many practitioners find this so enjoyable that they naturally stay 40 minutes or more. There is no upper limit — the sky doesn’t run out of blue, and the void doesn’t run out of serenity.
  10. Daily reminder: Throughout the day, whenever you notice thoughts becoming turbulent (anxiety, anger, worry), remember: you are the sky, not the clouds. Look past the thoughts to the space they move in. Even a 3-second recognition of the inner sky brings instant relief. The void is always accessible — it is closer than your next thought.
