The Techniques for Breath

Of the 112 meditation methods Shiva gave to Devi, the first nine arrive through the breath — because the breath is the one bridge that touches both the body and the formless, both this moment and whatever lies beyond it.

8 techniques · ~10 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These eight techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The eight techniques here are the breath-based sutras — the doorway most teachers consider the most accessible to a modern mind.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don't shop, don't switch, don't combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn't working — that is the work. Stay.

New to meditation?

Start with Technique 1 — watching the gap after the in-breath. It is beginner-level and requires no prior practice. Spend 10 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.

Technique 1 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: gap · awareness · pause · beginner
Radiant one, this experience may dawn between two breaths. After breath comes in — down — and just before turning up — out — the beneficence.

In Plain English

Become aware of the small, unforced pause that happens after the in-breath ends, before the out-breath begins. In that pause, thought has not yet started; awareness is naked.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably. Spine upright, shoulders soft, eyes closed.
  2. Don't change anything about your breath — let it be exactly as it is.
  3. Bring attention to the moment the in-breath finishes.
  4. Stay with the small natural pause that follows. Do not try to lengthen it.
  5. The out-breath will start by itself. Let it.
  6. On the next breath, do the same. Continue for 20 minutes.
  7. If you forget, simply return — the next gap is one breath away.

Signs It's Working

  • A subtle "drop" feeling, like settling onto a deeper floor
  • Thoughts space out — not stopped, just less continuous
  • The gap feels longer than the second or two it actually is
  • A quiet warmth or aliveness in the chest or belly

Common Pitfalls

  • Holding the breath on purpose — the gap must come by itself, not be manufactured
  • Counting breaths or measuring gaps — turns meditation into arithmetic
  • Trying to make the gap "bigger" — extension is the mind's reflex; let it stay tiny
  • Treating boredom as failure — the boredom IS the doorway thinning

Questions From The Book

How can simply being aware of a tiny gap in the breath lead to enlightenment?

Asked by a seeker · Chapter 4, "Overcoming the Deceptions of the Mind"

Osho's answer, in essence: spirituality is not an attainment but a recognition. You are already that. So even the smallest doorway is enough — the size of the door has nothing to do with the size of what lies beyond it. A door six inches wide opens onto the same sky as a door six feet wide.

The reason such a small gap can do so much: in that gap, the mind is not. The mind is a movement, a flow forward; the gap is the only place in your day where forward motion stops without sleep, without unconsciousness. There you are awake AND mindless. Even one second of that, fully tasted, is enlightenment in miniature — and that taste, repeated, becomes the standing flavour of life.

It feels improbable only because we have been taught that big results need big effort. Tantra's discovery is the opposite: the deepest things are reached not by force but by noticing what was always already happening.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The mind is a continuous machine of language and prediction. The breath is the only thing that interrupts itself naturally, twice per cycle. The sutra says: in that interruption, the machine briefly halts — and what remains is what you actually are.

A modern analogy: Like the silence between two notes in music. You don't hear the silence, but it's what holds the music up. The gap between two breaths is the silence that holds your aliveness up.

Why neuroscience cares: The end-inhale moment correlates with a brief uptick in vagal tone and a transient quieting of the default mode network in fMRI studies. You are not imagining the stillness — there is a measurable neural pause that lines up with the breath's natural pause.

EssenceThe pause is not the absence of breath. It is presence without thought. Stop looking for a door — you have been walking through it 20,000 times a day.

Technique 2 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: turning point · in-out · out-in · beginner
As breath turns from down to up, and again as breath curves from up to down — through both these turns, realize.

In Plain English

The first technique uses one pause per breath. This one uses both: the moment the in-breath becomes the out-breath, AND the moment the out-breath becomes the in-breath. Two doorways every cycle.

How to Practice

  1. Same setup as Technique 1: comfortable seat, eyes closed, breath untouched.
  2. Notice the moment the in-breath stops becoming "in" and starts becoming "out". That is the upper turn.
  3. Notice the moment the out-breath stops becoming "out" and starts becoming "in". That is the lower turn.
  4. You are not staying in one or the other — you are alert to whichever is arriving next.
  5. Do this for 15 to 20 minutes.

A modern analogy: Like the apex of a thrown ball. Mid-air, before it falls, there is a frame of perfect stillness. The breath has two such frames, every few seconds, free of charge.

EssenceThere are two doors per breath, not one. Which one will you walk through?

Technique 3 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: navel · fusion · centre · intermediate
Or, whenever in-breath and out-breath fuse, at this instant touch the energy-less, energy-filled centre.

In Plain English

There is a point near the navel where the energy of the in-breath and the energy of the out-breath meet and cancel. The sutra says: rest attention there. Osho calls it "energy-less" because your fuel-energy doesn't reach it, and "energy-filled" because cosmic energy does.

How to Practice

  1. Lie on your back or sit upright. Body soft.
  2. Breathe deeply but naturally — let the breath go all the way to the belly. (Most adults breathe shallowly; let the belly rise and fall.)
  3. Place attention near the navel.
  4. Feel the in-breath travelling down to meet the out-breath travelling up. Find the point where they meet.
  5. That meeting point is what to settle into. Stay there.
  6. Begin with 15 minutes. Build to 30 over time.

Questions From The Book

Why has my breathing become shallow as I grew up? What blocks belly breathing?

Implied throughout Chapter 3 · "Breath — A Bridge to the Universe"

Osho's answer, in essence: a child breathes with the whole belly, naturally. Adults breathe shallowly because deep breathing reaches the navel — and the navel is near the sex centre. Civilisation's discomfort with sex has, generation after generation, conditioned the breath to stay above the chest. To recover this technique, you also recover a body that is not afraid of itself.

Watch a sleeping baby: the whole abdomen rises and falls. Watch a stressed adult at a desk: the breath barely moves the collarbones. The sutra is asking you to become, again, the way you began.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Where two opposite movements meet, neither exists. The sutra is using breath as a way to point you to a place in the body where the dualities you live in (in/out, give/take, expand/contract) cancel — and what remains is undifferentiated awareness.

A modern analogy: Where two opposing waves cross in a tank, the water at the crossing point becomes momentarily still. That stillness is not absence of energy — it is two energies in perfect balance.

Why neuroscience cares: Slow diaphragmatic breathing measurably increases vagal tone, lowers heart rate, and shifts the autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance. The "warm centred feeling" practitioners describe lines up with measurable physiology, not just metaphor.

EssenceThe deepest stillness in the body is not where nothing is happening — it is where two opposites are perfectly cancelling.

Technique 4 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: stoppage · universal pause · surrender · advanced
Or, when breath is all out (up) and stopped of itself, or all in (down) and stopped — in such universal pause, one's small self vanishes. This is difficult only for the impure.

In Plain English

Sometimes, naturally, the breath stops at full out or full in — not because you held it, but because it just stops. The sutra says: in that moment, the sense of being a separate "you" can drop. The phrase "difficult only for the impure" means: the resistance is not technical, it is psychological. Anything you cling to will refuse this pause.

How to Practice

Note from the source: Osho is unusually direct about this technique — he says, "It is difficult for you; you cannot practice it. But you can feel it sometimes suddenly." The pause it speaks of cannot be manufactured by sitting down to do it; it arrives in shock-moments (a near-accident, a sudden stunning sight) when breath naturally stops. So this is less a routine and more an alertness.

  1. Build a foundation in Techniques 1–3 first. Those train you to recognise breath-gaps when they appear.
  2. Through the day, watch for moments when something stops you — beauty, shock, surprise, sudden silence. In those moments breath often pauses by itself.
  3. When you notice it has paused, don't grab. Don't extend it by holding. Don't rush back to inhale. Just remain in whatever the pause is.
  4. If, while sitting in Technique 1, the breath stops longer than usual on its own — that is this technique arriving. Stay alert; do nothing.
  5. "Difficult only for the impure" — Osho explains this is not a moral judgement. Whatever you cling to (a thought, an identity, a plan) refuses to let go in the pause. Notice what refuses.

  • Holding the breath. This kills the whole technique. The pause is given, not taken.
  • Panicking when the next breath is slow to return — the body breathes itself; trust it
  • Trying this advanced technique before Techniques 1–3 feel natural

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: When breath stops, "you" stop. Identity is not a stable thing — it is a process refreshed every breath. Catch the gap and the process is briefly suspended. What remains is the simplest thing there is.

If this feels too advanced — the simpler version: Try Technique 1 (gap after in-breath) for a few weeks first. The natural stoppage of Technique 4 will start arriving on its own, without you having to do anything special.

About "difficult only for the impure": Not a moral judgement. It means: whatever you are still gripping — a resentment, a plan, an identity — will refuse to let go in the pause. Nothing to fix, just notice what refuses, and let it refuse.

EssenceWhen breath stops, the small self stops. That is the door. It opens by itself for those who do not push.

Technique 5 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: third eye · visualisation · light · advanced
Attention between eyebrows, let mind be before thought. Let form fill with breath essence to the top of the head and there shower as light.

In Plain English

Focus attention at the point between the eyebrows (the third eye). Become aware of the subtle essence of the breath — not the air, but the aliveness it carries. Imagine that essence rising up the head, reaching the crown, and pouring back over you as light. This is the technique that, according to Osho, was given to Pythagoras in Egypt; he carried it back and seeded all Western mysticism with it.

How to Practice

  1. Sit upright. Eyes gently closed.
  2. Bring both eyes to look softly toward the middle of the forehead — the spot between the eyebrows. Don't strain. The eyes will lock there if you've found the spot.
  3. Stay focused there until thoughts begin to feel like clouds passing in front of you, rather than things you are inside of.
  4. Now feel the breath as more than air — feel its aliveness. (Tantra calls this prana, modern terms might be vitality or charge.)
  5. Imagine that aliveness rising from the breath up to the crown of the head.
  6. At the crown, let it shower back down over you as soft light.
  7. 20–30 minutes. Best done with a stable foundation in Techniques 1–4.

Questions From The Book

What is the third eye, scientifically? Is the pineal gland really involved?

Discussed in Chapter 5, "Five Techniques of Attentiveness"

Osho identifies the third eye with the pineal gland — a small endocrine gland between the two eyes. He calls it not blind, just closed; latent. He claims the gland actively absorbs attention: focus on it and your eyes lock in spite of you. Modern science has since confirmed the pineal regulates melatonin and circadian rhythm; whether it has the deeper functions Tantra assigns to it remains outside the scope of physiology.

Either way, the practice is the same: it is the act of focusing attention there that matters, not the metaphysics of why.

Why do practitioners of psychic techniques often have tense, fearful eyes?

Chapter 22, "Unblocking the Third Eye"

Osho's answer, in essence: hypnotists, mesmerists and forcing-the-third-eye practitioners use the eyes as a tool of will. The eye becomes hardened. In genuine practice the eye softens, the gaze becomes inward, and tension gives way to a kind of liquid attention. If your eyes feel tense or scared after this technique, you're forcing it. Stop. Return to Technique 1 for a week. Come back gentler.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Where attention goes, energy follows. The technique uses three layers stacked: a precise location (third eye), a quality of awareness (witnessing thought rather than being inside it), and a guided imagination (essence rising and showering as light). Each amplifies the next.

A modern analogy: Like sunlight through a magnifying glass — focused attention concentrates a diffuse field. Add visualisation and you direct that concentration. The "light shower" is your energy field reorganising under directed attention.

If this feels too advanced — the simpler version: Just Step 1 alone. Sit, close eyes, look softly at the spot between the eyebrows. Stay there for 10 minutes. Skip the visualisation. Build the focus first; the rest comes later.

Caveat: The book treats this as the technique that needs the most groundwork. Pythagoras did 40 days of fasting and breath training before he was allowed to practice it. Don't start here. End up here.

EssenceWhere attention goes, energy follows. Where energy gathers, light is felt. The third eye is not a place — it is what you see with when the two eyes stop fighting for the picture.

Technique 6 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: daily life · integration · awareness · beginner
When in worldly activity, keep attention between two breaths, and so practising, in a few days be born anew.

In Plain English

Take Technique 1 off the cushion. While doing ordinary things — washing dishes, walking, waiting in line — keep a small thread of attention on the gap between your breaths. The sutra promises that a few days of this is enough to start a kind of inner rebirth.

How to Practice

  1. Pick one routine activity to start with: brushing teeth, walking to work, washing dishes.
  2. While doing it, keep a soft, background awareness on the gap after the in-breath.
  3. Don't let it interrupt the activity. The activity continues; the awareness just rides alongside.
  4. Each time you forget, return — no self-blame, no restart. The next gap is one breath away.
  5. Over a few weeks, expand to more activities.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Most "spiritual practice" stays on the cushion and dies the moment life resumes. This technique closes that gap. It says: the gap between two breaths is portable. Take it with you.

A modern analogy: Like keeping a song playing softly in your head while you talk. The song doesn't stop the talking; the talking doesn't stop the song. Two layers, simultaneously.

Why this might be the most important breath technique for modern people: Daily life is now denser than at any point in history. Most of us cannot find a real 30-minute silence. This sutra says: you don't need to. You need 30 micro-pauses spread through the day. The arithmetic still works.

EssenceMeditation isn't where you sit. It's what stays awake while you live.

Technique 7 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: sleep · dream · death · advanced
With intangible breath in centre of forehead, as this reaches heart at the moment of sleep, have direction over dreams and over death itself.

In Plain English

A pre-sleep practice. Lying in bed, place attention at the forehead. Imagine a subtle thread of breath descending from there to the heart. If you can stay aware as you fall asleep, the sutra promises lucid dreaming and, eventually, conscious passage through death.

How to Practice

  1. Lie in bed at the actual moment of going to sleep — not some other time.
  2. Eyes closed. Body settled.
  3. Place attention at the centre of the forehead.
  4. Imagine an "intangible breath" — a thread of fine awareness — descending slowly from forehead to heart.
  5. Stay with the thread. Let it deepen, slow, and reach the heart at the very moment sleep takes you.
  6. If you wake, that's fine. Notice. Try again the next night.

Questions From The Book

What other factors help one stay conscious while dreaming?

Chapter 6, "Devices to Transcend Dreaming"

Osho's answer, in essence: three things support lucid dreaming. (1) Falling asleep with awareness — this technique. (2) Eating light at dinner — a heavy stomach pulls awareness into digestion, not consciousness. (3) Sleeping in a particular way: facing east, head supported, body relaxed. Most importantly: drop the ambition to "achieve" lucidity. Awareness can ride into sleep; ambition cannot.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The moment of falling asleep is a real event with a real signature in the brain — a transition through hypnagogia, the gateway between waking and dream. The sutra is asking you to keep one thread of awareness alive across that transition.

The death claim: Tantra views the falling-asleep moment and the dying moment as structurally identical — both are an unwinding of the mind from the body. Practise the small one nightly, the sutra implies, and the big one will be familiar.

Why neuroscience cares: Modern lucid dreaming research (Stephen LaBerge, Voss et al.) confirms that pre-sleep attention to a chosen object substantially raises the probability of dream lucidity. The forehead-to-heart visualisation here is essentially a focused MILD-style induction with an added somatic element.

EssenceThe moment between waking and sleep is identical to the moment of death. Catch one nightly — you have rehearsed for the other.

Technique 8 of 112
Category: Breath · Tags: devotion · junctions · bhakti · intermediate
With utmost devotion, centre on the two junctions of breath, and know the knower.

In Plain English

Same two turning points as Technique 2 — but with one ingredient added: devotion. Whatever you most love or revere — the divine, your teacher, the universe, life itself — let that love be the colour of your attention. Devotion makes attention effortless. Then, in those two turning points, look for the one who is looking.

How to Practice

  1. Sit. Settle. Breath natural.
  2. Bring to mind whatever moves you most — a beloved figure, the open sky, the felt sense of life itself. Let the heart soften.
  3. Now, with that softness, watch the two turns of breath (in→out and out→in).
  4. At each turn, gently ask: who is noticing this turn?
  5. Don't answer in words. Let the question pull attention back to its source.
  6. 20–30 minutes.

The sutra hijacks that mechanism: take the natural focus that love already produces, and aim it at the breath's turning points, with the question "who is noticing?" riding on top.

A modern analogy: Falling in love makes you naturally attentive without trying. This technique uses that same engine, deliberately.

Why this is paired with Technique 2 in the book: Technique 2 is the same anatomy without the fuel. For some practitioners, dry attention is enough. For others, devotion is the carrier wave. The book offers both because people are different.

EssenceDevotion is concentrated love. Concentrated love is attention without effort.
