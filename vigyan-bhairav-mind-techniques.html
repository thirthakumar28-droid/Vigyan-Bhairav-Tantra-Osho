Technique 62 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: wandering · present · acceptance · beginner
Wherever your mind is wandering, internally or externally, at this very place, this.

In Plain English

This is possibly the most compassionate sutra in the entire 112. It says: wherever you find your mind right now — lost in fantasy, worrying about tomorrow, reliving last week — THAT is your meditation spot. Don't fight the wandering. Use it. The place the mind has wandered to IS the technique. Just notice "here I am" — and that noticing is the awakening.

How to Practice

  1. Sit or walk. Don't try to concentrate on anything.
  2. Let the mind wander freely for a few moments.
  3. Then gently ask: where is my mind right now? (A memory? A plan? A fantasy? A worry?)
  4. Don't pull the mind back. Instead, be fully present where the mind already is.
  5. If it's in a memory: be fully in that memory, with awareness. If it's planning: be fully aware that planning is happening.
  6. "This" — the word at the end of the sutra — means: here. Whatever here is right now. That's enough.
  7. Repeat throughout the day, as often as you remember.

Technique 88 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: knower · known · unity · consciousness · non-duality
Each thing is perceived through knowing. The self shines in space through knowing. Perceive one being as knower and known.

In Plain English

Normally we divide experience into three: the knower (subject), the known (object), and the knowing (process). This sutra says: dissolve that trinity into one. The tree you see — is it separate from your seeing? Can you find where “seeing” ends and “tree” begins? Can you find where “you” end and “seeing” begins? In meditation: discover that knower, knowing, and known are one undivided event. The self shines not behind knowing but AS knowing. There is no separate knower — only knowing happening.

How to Practice

  1. Start with an object: Place a flower, a candle, or any simple object before you. Look at it. Normally you experience: “I” (the knower) am looking at “the flower” (the known) through “seeing” (the knowing). Three things. This technique dissolves them into one.
  2. Search for the boundary: Look at the flower. Now ask: where exactly does “seeing” end and “flower” begin? You experience colour, shape, beauty — but is the colour in the flower or in your seeing? Can you separate the two? Try. You will find: you cannot. The flower-as-experienced is inseparable from the seeing of it.
  3. Search for the knower: Now turn attention inward. Where is the “I” that sees? Can you find a separate entity behind the seeing? Look for the seer. All you find is: more seeing, more knowing. There is no little person BEHIND the experience watching it. There is just experiencing, seamlessly unfolding.
  4. The trinity collapses: When you cannot find a boundary between seer and seen, and you cannot find a separate seer behind the seeing, the three collapse into one: knowing. Just knowing. Not “someone knowing something” but knowing happening. One event, not three. This is non-duality.
  5. Apply to sound: Close your eyes. Listen to any sound. Ask: is the sound “out there” or “in here”? Where does hearing happen? In the ear? In the brain? In the sound itself? You cannot locate hearing in any single place because hearing is not a “thing” — it is an undivided event that includes sound, ear, brain, and awareness as one.
  6. Apply to thought: Watch a thought arise. Now ask: who is watching? Is the watcher different from the thought? Or is “watching the thought” itself another thought? And who watches THAT? Every attempt to find a separate knower leads to infinite regress. At some point you must give up the search and recognise: there is only knowing — luminous, self-aware knowing with no separate knower behind it.
  7. The self shines AS knowing: The sutra says “the self shines in space through knowing.” This means: your true self is not hiding behind experience, watching from a distance. Your self IS the knowing itself — the luminous awareness that constitutes every experience. You don’t HAVE awareness; you ARE awareness. Awareness doesn’t belong to you; you belong to it.
  8. Walking practice: Walk with open eyes. See the world. Feel: there is one seamless knowing happening — the visual field, the body sensations, the sounds — all one knowing, not divided into “me” and “world.” One flow of experiencing. Rest in this flow without trying to locate yourself within it.
  9. Formal sitting (20 min): Sit with eyes closed. Let experience flow: thoughts, sensations, sounds, silence. Notice: all of it is KNOWN. There is a knowing quality pervading everything. Now rest AS that knowing quality rather than as the one who “has” it. You are not experiencing knowing; you ARE knowing experiencing itself.
  10. The paradox: This technique leads to a beautiful paradox: the one you were searching for (the knower) was looking through your eyes the whole time. You cannot find it as an object because it is the subject of all seeking. You ARE what you seek. Recognise this, and the search ends in laughter.

Technique 89 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: beloved · moment · mind · knowing
Beloved, at this moment let, mind, knowing, breath, form, be included.

In Plain English

Most meditation says “I am not the body, not the breath, not the mind” and excludes everything to find the witness. This sutra goes the opposite way: include. Sitting still, refuse to divide yourself. Body, breath, thought, knowing — let everything in. Don’t set up a centre apart from them. The centre disappears, the ego cannot survive on inclusiveness, and what is left is consciousness like an open sky covering everything.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably with eyes closed.
  2. Notice that your habit is to divide. The mind wants to say “I am the awareness, not the body,” or “I am the witness, not the thoughts.” For this technique, suspend the habit. Don’t exclude anything.
  3. Let the body in. Whatever sensations are there — pressure of the seat, warmth, slight aches, the soft weight of the limbs — include them in “I am.”
  4. Let the breath in. The in-breath is you. The out-breath is you. Don’t stand back and watch breath; be the breath as it comes and goes.
  5. Let thought in. Whatever thoughts are passing — planning, remembering, drifting — include them. Don’t fight them, don’t try to be the witness behind them. Just let thinking be one of the things that is happening as you.
  6. Let knowing in — the awareness itself. Even that is not a separate centre apart from breath and thought; include the knower along with the known.
  7. Don’t centre yourself anywhere. Osho is specific: “Don’t get yourself centred anywhere — be uncentred.” You are not in the head, not in the heart, not behind the eyes. Drop all locations.
  8. Notice the body’s form changing. Sometimes you will feel the body as enormous, filling the room; sometimes very small, atomic. As you include more, it grows; as you would-exclude, it shrinks. Don’t fix the size. Let it expand.
  9. Now extend the inclusion outward. Keeping the eyes closed, take in the room around you; let the room be inside you, not you inside the room. Then take in the building, the street, the sky. Whatever you can sense, take it inside.
  10. Try the daylight version too. Sit under a tree, look at it, then close your eyes and feel the tree is within you. Look at the rising sun, then close the eyes and feel the sun is rising within you. Osho says this is not pure imagination — the tree and you grow from the same earth, and the inclusion is recovering an actual fact.
  11. Include the difficult things last. A friend, an annoying neighbour, an enemy. Jesus’s instruction to love the enemy as yourself, Osho says, is exactly this technique — not for ethics but for expansion. If even the enemy is included, nothing is left outside; ego cannot survive that.
  12. Stay until you feel the centre dissolve. Don’t worry if it doesn’t happen on day one. The training of inclusiveness is gradual. End by gently opening the eyes and carrying a small inclusive flavour into the next activity — nothing is shut out.

The appreciation of objects and subjects is the same for an enlightened as for an unenlightened person. The former has one greatness: he remains in the subjective mood, not lost in things.

Technique 101 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: identity · omniscience · expansion · belief · divine
Believe omniscient, omnipotent, pervading.

In Plain English

Osho is emphatic: this is NOT positive thinking. It is not affirmation. It is not “fake it till you make it.” It is the stripping away of false limitations. You ARE already omniscient — you just don’t know it because layers of conditioning have convinced you that you are small, limited, separate. This technique asks you to FEEL the truth rather than believe a lie. You are consciousness itself — and consciousness has no boundaries.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in a comfortable position: Spine erect, eyes closed. Take 5 slow, deep breaths. Let the body settle completely. Don’t begin until the surface agitation has calmed.
  2. Begin with the body boundary: Feel the edges of your body — where skin meets air. Now gently question: “Am I really limited to this body?” Don’t answer intellectually. FEEL the question. Let the boundary soften.
  3. Expand to the room: Feel yourself pervading the room you are in. Not imagining — FEELING. You are the space that contains the furniture, the walls, the air. The body is still there but you are no longer confined to it. You pervade the room like warmth pervades a heated space.
  4. Expand to the building, the street: Let the feeling of pervading grow outward. You include the building, the street, the neighbourhood. Don’t think it — feel the expansion as a loosening, a relief, a dropping of tension.
  5. Expand to the city: You pervade the entire city. Millions of people, thousands of buildings — all within you, not outside you. Feel the vastness. Notice: the bigger you become, the more peaceful you feel.
  6. Expand to the earth: You pervade the planet. Oceans, mountains, forests, deserts — all arising within your awareness. The earth is not beneath you; it is within you.
  7. Expand to the cosmos: Stars, galaxies, infinite space — all within you. You are omnipresent. There is nowhere you are not. Feel the absolute freedom of having no boundary. This is not imagination; this is removing the imagined boundary that never actually existed.
  8. Rest as the pervading: Don’t hold the expansion with effort. Simply rest as what you are when you stop pretending to be small. Omniscient means all-knowing — in this expanded state, you don’t need to seek knowledge because you ARE the space in which all knowledge arises.
  9. Feel omnipotence as relaxation: Omnipotent does not mean forcing things. It means there is nothing opposing you because there is nothing outside you. Feel the absence of opposition. That is true power — the power of wholeness.
  10. Return slowly: After 20–30 minutes, gently bring attention back to the body. But carry the flavour of expansion. Walk through your day knowing the boundary is optional, not mandatory. Practise daily for 21 days.

Sweet hearted one, meditate on knowing and not-knowing, existing and not-existing. Then leave both aside that you may be.

In Plain English

The mind can only think in pairs: knowing/not-knowing, existing/not-existing, yes/no, something/nothing. This sutra says: hold both opposites in awareness simultaneously. Let them cancel. How to Practice

  4. Hold both simultaneously. Don't choose one over the other. Let them coexist.
  5. Now do the same with existence: "I exist" — feel that. "I do not exist" — feel that too.
  6. Hold both. Neither is false; neither is complete.
  7. Now: leave both aside. Don't land on either pole. Simply be — without any statement about what you are.
  8. Rest there. 20–40 minutes.

You call the inner space emptiness, but also call it being. How can something be both empty and full?

Chapter 80, "All and Nothing Mean the Same"

Osho's answer, in essence: "All" and "nothing" are contradictory only in the dictionary, not in existence. If you love all, it is the same as loving no one in particular. If you are everything, you are also nothing specific. These are two ways of pointing at the absolute. One is via negation (empty, void, nothingness), the other via affirmation (fullness, totality, God). Neither is more correct. They meet at the same point — the point where mind's categories break down. The technique takes you there by holding both and then dropping both.

Why did Buddhism get uprooted from India because of emptiness teachings?

Chapter 79, "The Philosophy of Emptiness"

Osho's answer, in essence: India's temperament is devotional, theistic. The idea of total emptiness — no God, no soul, no permanence — felt like nihilism to the Indian heart. People could not understand that Buddha was using emptiness as a method, not a philosophy. He denied God not because God doesn't exist, but because any concept of God becomes a hiding place for the ego. He denied the soul so that no subtle "self" could survive. The technique requires total emptiness as the door — but what you find inside is the fullest fullness. People heard only the door and missed what lay behind it.

Were techniques like "become universal consciousness" meant only for very advanced persons?

Chapter 78, "The Inner Guide"

Osho's answer, in essence: They were meant for very innocent persons, not very advanced ones. An innocent person can hear "you are already that" and simply accept it — and in that acceptance, it becomes actual. We need elaborate techniques only because we cannot trust. We cannot simply hear and be. The simpler you are, the fewer steps you need. The more complex your mind, the more technique is required to exhaust it.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The mind operates in binary code: 1 or 0, yes or no, exists or doesn't exist. This sutra says: what if reality is neither 1 nor 0? What if it is the screen on which both 1 and 0 appear? You cannot think your way to what is beyond thought — but you can exhaust thought by giving it two opposites to hold simultaneously. The mind, unable to resolve the contradiction, eventually surrenders. In that surrender, something else becomes apparent.

A modern analogy: Like a computer encountering an instruction that says "this statement is false." The logical processor halts. In that halt, you become aware of the processor itself — not its output, but its existence. That awareness of the processor (not any calculation it produces) is what the sutra calls "you."

If this feels too advanced — the simpler version: Start with Technique 3 (wherever the mind wanders, this) for a month. Then move to Technique 6 (remain in the subject). Only after those feel natural should you attempt this final dissolution. It is the last door, not the first.

Why this technique is placed near the end of the 112: It requires that you have already become comfortable with paradox, with not-knowing, with the absence of ground. Without that preparation, it feels terrifying. With it, it feels like coming home.

The Techniques for Mind & Thought

These techniques use the mind's own machinery against itself — desire, wandering, doubt, naming, knowing. Instead of fighting thought, they redirect your attention to the gaps, the roots, and the watcher behind the watched. The mind dissolves not by suppression but by being fully seen.

7 techniques · ~12 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These seven techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. Unlike most of the 112, the techniques here don't ask you to fight the mind. They use the mind's own movements — desire, wandering, naming, knowing — as the doorway.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don't shop, don't switch, don't combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn't working — that is the work. Stay.

New to meditation?

Start with Technique 3 — "wherever your mind is wandering, this". It is the easiest sutra in all 112. It cannot fail; every moment of distraction noticed is a moment of practice. Try it for 21 days, then return for what comes next.
