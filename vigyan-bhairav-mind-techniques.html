The Techniques for Mind & Thought

These techniques use the mind's own machinery against itself — desire, wandering, doubt, naming, knowing. Instead of fighting thought, they redirect your attention to the gaps, the roots, and the watcher behind the watched. The mind dissolves not by suppression but by being fully seen.

7 techniques · ~12 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These seven techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. Unlike most of the 112, the techniques here don't ask you to fight the mind. They use the mind's own movements — desire, wandering, naming, knowing — as the doorway.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don't shop, don't switch, don't combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn't working — that is the work. Stay.

New to meditation?

Start with Technique 3 — "wherever your mind is wandering, this". It is the easiest sutra in all 112. It cannot fail; every moment of distraction noticed is a moment of practice. Try it for 21 days, then return for what comes next.

Technique 106 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: desire · storm · witness · intermediate
In moods of extreme desire, be undisturbed.

In Plain English

When a massive wave of desire hits — lust, rage, craving, ambition — do not try to suppress it and do not act on it. Instead, stay still inside it. Be the unmoving centre of the cyclone. The desire will still roar, but you are no longer the desire; you are the one watching the desire.

How to Practice

  1. Wait for a genuine moment of extreme desire (anger counts, lust counts, craving counts). Don't manufacture it.
  2. The moment you notice the wave rising, pause externally. Freeze the body.
  3. Now notice: the desire is happening to you. You are the space it is happening inside of.
  4. Don't push the desire away. Don't feed it either. Just hold still and observe.
  5. Watch where the desire lives in the body — chest tightness, heat in the belly, tension in the jaw.
  6. Stay with it. The wave peaks and then, without your help, recedes.
  7. What remains after the wave — notice that. That is you.

  • You feel the desire as a physical event, not a narrative

  • Over weeks: desires still come, but they no longer "have" you

Common Pitfalls

  • Suppressing: pushing the desire down is NOT this technique. Let it be fully present, just unacted upon.
  • Indulging: acting on it immediately means you missed the window
  • Trying it only with small desires — the sutra specifically says "extreme". Small desires won't provide enough energy for the breakthrough.
  • Intellectualizing: "I am witnessing now" is a thought, not witnessing

Questions From The Book

When a mood against someone arises and I try to remain centered, it feels impossible. What am I doing wrong?

Chapter 16, "Beyond the Sin of Unconsciousness"

Osho's answer, in essence: you are trying to remain centered while simultaneously analyzing the mood. The analysis is a second layer of mind on top of the first. The technique asks only one thing: don't place the mood on the other person. The moment you attribute the anger to the person, you have moved outside yourself. Keep the energy inside — not as suppression, but as raw fuel without a target. Without a target, the energy has nowhere to go except back to the source. That return is the centering.

If a desire or mood becomes exhilarating and I don't express it outwardly, does that energy go back to the source?

Chapter 42, "Alertness through Tantra"

Osho's answer, in essence: Yes, but with a condition. If you suppress it out of morality — "I should not feel this" — the energy does not return to the source; it goes underground into the unconscious and festers. Only when you hold the energy with full awareness, without judgment, without repression, does it naturally recycle inward and refresh you. Awareness is the key ingredient that determines whether non-expression is suppression or transformation.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Every desire has a 90-second neurochemical lifespan. Jill Bolte Taylor's "90-second rule" describes how an emotion — once triggered — floods the body with chemicals that clear within about 90 seconds. Everything after that is re-triggering through thought. The sutra says: be still for the first wave. If you can survive 90 seconds without acting or narrating, the biochemistry recedes and you are left standing in clarity.

A modern analogy: Like standing still in a strong wind instead of running with it. The wind passes. You are still here. You realise you were never the wind.

Why neuroscience cares: The prefrontal cortex (executive function) can override the amygdala (reactive impulse) — but only if you buy time. The "freeze" in this technique is exactly the pause that allows the PFC to come online. Meditators show measurably thicker PFC tissue, and this is likely why.

EssenceThe storm is not you. You are the sky it passes through.

Technique 107 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: illusion · detachment · film · witness · beginner
This so-called universe appears as a juggling, a picture show. To be happy, look upon it so.

In Plain English

Treat everything you see — the drama of your day, relationships, successes, failures — as though you are watching a film. Not your film. A film. This is not nihilism or coldness; it is the recognition that your suffering comes from believing the picture show is the whole of reality. When you see it as a show, you are free to enjoy it without being crushed by it.

How to Practice

  1. Choose any period of your day — commuting, a meeting, cooking dinner.
  2. Mentally step back one layer. Imagine you are watching a film of your own life.
  3. Notice: the "you" in the film is a character. You — the watcher — are in the audience.
  4. Don't detach robotically. Let the emotions play. Just recognise their film-like quality — they appear, peak, and pass.
  5. After 10 minutes, notice what has shifted in your body and mood.
  6. Over days, extend this. Let it become a quiet background attitude.

  • Becoming cold or superior: the technique asks for compassionate watching, not judgment of others

Questions From The Book

If the world is illusion, why bother doing anything at all?

Chapter 37, "Techniques to Witness the Flux-like Film of Life"

Osho's answer, in essence: This is the intellect's misunderstanding. The sutra does not say the universe is unreal. It says it appears as a juggling, a picture show. The word "appears" is important. It is a technique of looking, not a philosophy about reality. You look at it as a picture show so that your suffering about it drops. When suffering drops, you do not stop acting — you start acting freely, for the first time, because compulsion is gone. It is the difference between a slave rowing and a free person sailing.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Cognitive fusion — the psychological term for believing you ARE your thoughts and experiences — is the root of most suffering according to both ancient Tantra and modern ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy). This sutra is a defusion technique: create a small distance between the experiencer and the experience.

A modern analogy: You are watching a VR movie and forgot you put the headset on. The sutra says: remember the headset. You don't need to take it off. Just knowing it's there changes everything.

Why psychology cares: "Cognitive defusion" is a core pillar of third-wave behavioural therapies (ACT, DBT). The mechanism: when you label an experience as "a story my mind is telling" rather than "reality," the amygdala response decreases and the anterior cingulate cortex (error-monitoring, metacognition) activates. You move from reactive to reflective.

EssenceYou are not in the movie. You are in the cinema.

Technique 108 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: wandering · present · acceptance · beginner
Wherever your mind is wandering, internally or externally, at this very place, this.

In Plain English

This is possibly the most compassionate sutra in the entire 112. It says: wherever you find your mind right now — lost in fantasy, worrying about tomorrow, reliving last week — THAT is your meditation spot. Don't fight the wandering. Use it. The place the mind has wandered to IS the technique. Just notice "here I am" — and that noticing is the awakening.

How to Practice

  1. Sit or walk. Don't try to concentrate on anything.
  2. Let the mind wander freely for a few moments.
  3. Then gently ask: where is my mind right now? (A memory? A plan? A fantasy? A worry?)
  4. Don't pull the mind back. Instead, be fully present where the mind already is.
  5. If it's in a memory: be fully in that memory, with awareness. If it's planning: be fully aware that planning is happening.
  6. "This" — the word at the end of the sutra — means: here. Whatever here is right now. That's enough.
  7. Repeat throughout the day, as often as you remember.

  • Feeling like you're "cheating" because it's too easy — it's not a cheat; it's the purest technique
  • Forgetting for hours and then beating yourself up — the next remembering is the practice; there is no lost time

Questions From The Book

I am in the present moment with no thought, but I still don't feel like a buddha. What's missing?

Chapter 40, "Sudden Enlightenment and Its Obstacles"

Osho's answer, in essence: Nothing is missing. You are expecting fireworks. Enlightenment is not an experience added to you — it is the removal of the sense that something is missing. The very expectation "I should feel something special" is the last thought, the last veil. Drop even that. This very ordinary moment, fully seen, IS it. The problem is not that enlightenment is absent; it is that you are looking for it to be something other than what this already is.

How can the practice of witnessing make me quiet without making me serious and lifeless?

Chapter 38, "Toward the Authentic Being"

Osho's answer, in essence: If witnessing makes you serious, it has become a strain, not a witnessing. True witnessing is effortless — like a mirror reflecting. A mirror is not serious; it doesn't strain to reflect. If you become grave and still, you have turned the technique into suppression. Let the witnessing be playful. Watch as a child watches — curious, not heavy. If laughter comes, laugh. If you move, move. The witness is not against life — it is the deepest participation in life, because for the first time nothing is excluded.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Every other meditation gives you a target and says "come back when you wander." This sutra flips that: the wandering itself IS the landing. There is no failure mode. Every moment of distraction, the instant you notice it, is a moment of awakening. This reframes the entire relationship with the wandering mind.

A modern analogy: Most GPS systems say "recalculating" when you miss a turn. This sutra says: wherever you are now IS the destination. There is no wrong turn.

Why psychology cares: Modern mindfulness research (Brewer et al., Yale) shows that self-criticism about mind-wandering creates a secondary stress loop. When participants were told "noticing wandering IS the practice," their cortisol levels dropped. This sutra contains that exact instruction, written 5,000 years before the study.

If every technique in this file confuses you, do only this one. It cannot fail. It requires no special conditions. It takes zero time. And it works.

EssenceYou cannot lose this meditation. Wherever you go, you are already there.

Technique 92 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: self-inquiry · ego · dissolution · advanced
Feel: my thought, I-ness, internal organs — me. Before desire and before knowing, how can I say I am? Consider. Dissolve in the beauty.

In Plain English

You assume "I exist." But examine it: the "I" is made of thoughts (removable), the feeling of I-ness (a sensation, not a thing), and the body (internal organs — also not "you"). Before any desire arose and before any knowledge was formed — what were you? If there was no thought, no desire, no knowledge — where was this "I"? Look for it. When you cannot find it, dissolve into that unfindability. That dissolution is beautiful, not terrifying.

How to Practice

  1. Sit quietly. Eyes closed. Body settled.
  2. Ask: what is this "I"? Notice what arises — thoughts? Sensations? Memories? Images?
  3. Each thing that arises: see that it is an object being watched. It is not the watcher.
  4. Now ask: before my first thought today — was "I" there? Before my first desire in childhood — was "I" there?
  5. Keep looking for the "I" that existed before knowing began. You won't find it as a thing.
  6. In that not-finding, something relaxes. The structure of "me" softens. Let it.
  7. The sutra calls this dissolution "beauty." Allow it to feel like that — an opening, not a loss.

  • Trying to create the absence of I, rather than simply looking and not finding it

Questions From The Book

How can one know that a spiritual search is authentic and not just another ego-trip?

Chapter 56, "Discovering Emptiness"

Osho's answer, in essence: If the search makes you feel special, superior, or gives you a spiritual identity to wear — it is an ego-trip dressed in spiritual clothing. A genuine search always humbles. It takes things away rather than giving things. If meditation is making you "someone" — a spiritual seeker, an awakened person — it has become decoration. When it is genuine, it is making you nobody. The real sign is the disappearance of the need to be seen as spiritual.

When I become more aware, a feeling of "I am present, I exist" remains. How to dissolve even this?

Chapter 54, "The Fire of Awareness"

Osho's answer, in essence: The feeling "I am aware" is the last, most subtle layer of ego. Earlier layers were gross — anger, ambition, desire. This one is refined. But it is still a feeling, still an object. The technique asks: who is watching even this feeling of "I am"? Don't answer. The question itself, if held without an answer, dissolves the layer. The answer would create a new layer. Let the question burn without resolution.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The "self" is not a thing located somewhere — it is a process. Modern neuroscience calls it the "narrative self" or the "default mode network" (DMN). The DMN constructs the story of "me" from memories, future plans, and self-referential thought. When you meditate deeply, the DMN quiets. What remains is awareness without a narrator. This sutra is asking you to look for the narrator and discover it was always just another character.

A modern analogy: A whirlpool looks like a thing. It has a shape, a location, a name. But it is not a thing — it is a pattern of movement in water. Stop the movement, and the whirlpool doesn't "die" — it simply was never a thing to begin with. Your "I" is a whirlpool in consciousness.

If this feels too advanced — the simpler version: Try Technique 3 ("wherever your mind wanders, this") for a few weeks first. Once you are comfortable simply noticing without struggling, this deeper inquiry will feel natural rather than frightening.

EssenceYou are looking for the one who is looking. That search, genuinely pursued, ends in laughter — because the seeker was always the sought.

Technique 109 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: non-judgment · purity · acceptance · intermediate
The purity of other teachings is an impurity to us. In reality, know nothing as pure or impure.

In Plain English

Stop labeling experiences as "good" or "bad," "pure" or "impure," "spiritual" or "worldly." The mind's deepest habit is division — this is clean, that is dirty; this is sacred, that is profane. The sutra says: reality itself makes no such division. Drop the judge. When nothing is impure, nothing is pure either. What remains is simply what IS — undivided, whole, free.

How to Practice

  1. Choose a day (or start with an hour) where you commit to making no internal judgments.
  2. Whatever arises — a beautiful thought, an ugly thought, pleasure, pain, anger, love — simply let it be without the label.
  3. Notice how reflexive labeling is: good/bad appears before you've even finished perceiving something.
  4. Each time you catch a judgment forming, don't fight it. Just don't follow it. Let it dissolve.
  5. Watch how much energy the mind spends on sorting reality into categories. That energy is now freed.
  6. After the hour/day, notice how you feel. Less tired? More open?

  • Confusing non-judgment with indifference — you can still act wisely without the mental habit of sorting everything into bins

Questions From The Book

If nothing is impure, then how can the teachings of others be called impure by this sutra?

Chapter 42, "Alertness through Tantra"

Osho's answer, in essence: The sutra is being deliberately paradoxical to shake you. It says "the purity of other teachings is an impurity TO US" — then immediately says "know nothing as pure or impure." The first half is the provocation; the second half is the technique. The provocation breaks your loyalty to a system of judgment. Then the technique emerges: live without the category system itself. Tantra uses the shock of contradiction to break habitual thinking.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The brain's categorization system — approach/avoid, good/bad — is a survival mechanism that runs constantly. In primitive environments, it saves your life. In modern life, it mostly creates mental noise and suffering. Judgments about food, people, thoughts, your own body, the weather — this ceaseless binary sorting consumes enormous cognitive resources. The sutra asks: what if you stopped sorting for one hour?

A modern analogy: Like removing the spam filter from your email and just reading everything that arrives. Surprisingly, most of what the filter was blocking turns out to be harmless — even interesting.

Why psychology cares: Non-judgmental awareness is the core instruction of Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR, Jon Kabat-Zinn). Research consistently shows that the habit of constant evaluation correlates with higher anxiety, depression, and cortisol levels. This sutra is that entire research programme in one sentence, written millennia earlier.

EssenceThe judge in your head is retired. What arrives when you stop sorting? — Life.

Technique 110 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: subject · witness · attention · intermediate
The appreciation of objects and subjects is the same for an enlightened as for an unenlightened person. The former has one greatness: he remains in the subjective mood, not lost in things.

In Plain English

An awakened person sees the same world you do. Trees, people, food, problems — all the same. The only difference: they never lose track of the one who is seeing. They stay home — in the subject — while looking at objects. You leave home every time an object pulls you. The technique: stay with the seer, not with the seen. Don't change what you look at; change where you look from.

How to Practice

  1. Sit with eyes open. Look at anything — a wall, a tree, your hands.
  2. Now shift attention: instead of focusing on the object, focus on the fact that you are seeing.
  3. The object remains in your visual field. But your priority has shifted to the seer.
  4. Notice: the seer never changes. Objects change constantly. The seer is steady.
  5. Walk through your day with this background awareness: "I am the one who is seeing, hearing, feeling — not the things being seen, heard, felt."
  6. Whenever you "get lost" in a thing — a thought, an argument, a craving — gently return to the seer.

Questions From The Book

In which way can the practice of self-remembering transform the human mind?

Chapter 36, "From Illusion to Reality"

Osho's answer, in essence: Gurdjieff called this technique "self-remembering" — remember yourself while looking at anything. Normally you forget yourself; you become the thing you are watching. A movie absorbs you. An argument absorbs you. Self-remembering means: keep a thread of awareness on yourself while engaging with anything. It transforms the mind because energy that was constantly leaking outward now has a home to return to. You become a centre rather than a scattered field.

If I am always the subject, never lost in objects, won't I become cold and removed from life?

Chapter 73, "Fear of Transformation Goes Deep"

Osho's answer, in essence: The opposite happens. When you are not lost in things, you can actually enjoy them for the first time. A person drowning in water cannot enjoy swimming. But someone standing on solid ground who chooses to enter the water can play in it. The subjective mood is the ground. From there, engagement is a choice, not a compulsion. Love becomes purer when you are not lost in it, because there is no fear of losing yourself.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Attention is always flowing outward — toward screens, problems, people, desires. This creates what psychology calls "continuous partial attention" — you are everywhere and therefore nowhere. The sutra reverses the direction: instead of attention flowing outward toward objects, let attention rest in its source. The objects don't disappear; you just stop being consumed by them.

A modern analogy: Like the difference between a flashlight and a lighthouse. The flashlight moves from object to object, always chasing. The lighthouse stays home and illuminates whatever passes through its beam. Be the lighthouse.

Why neuroscience cares: The distinction between "subject" and "object" maps onto what researchers call metacognition — awareness of awareness. Metacognitive capacity is trainable and correlates with emotional regulation, decision quality, and reduced rumination. This sutra is a metacognition drill.

EssenceYou see the world and the world sees you. One of you never changes. Stay with that one.

Technique 112 of 112
Category: Mind & Thought · Tags: emptiness · transcendence · knowing · advanced
Sweet hearted one, meditate on knowing and not-knowing, existing and not-existing. Then leave both aside that you may be.

In Plain English

The mind can only think in pairs: knowing/not-knowing, existing/not-existing, yes/no, something/nothing. This sutra says: hold both opposites in awareness simultaneously. Let them cancel. Then drop both. What remains cannot be named — but it IS. The sutra calls it "you" — "that you may be." This is not a philosophical exercise; it is a direct pointing to what you are when all categories are abandoned.

How to Practice

  1. Sit in deep stillness. 10 minutes of silent settling first.
  2. Contemplate: "I know things." Feel the state of knowing — the confidence, the structure it gives.
  3. Now contemplate: "I know nothing." Feel the state of not-knowing — the openness, the uncertainty.
  4. Hold both simultaneously. Don't choose one over the other. Let them coexist.
  5. Now do the same with existence: "I exist" — feel that. "I do not exist" — feel that too.
  6. Hold both. Neither is false; neither is complete.
  7. Now: leave both aside. Don't land on either pole. Simply be — without any statement about what you are.
  8. Rest there. 20–40 minutes.

Signs It's Working

  • The mind stops grasping — a feeling like releasing a clenched fist you didn't know was clenched
  • A strange peace that isn't "positive" or "negative" — it just IS
  • The sense that categories like "self/other," "here/there," "then/now" are optional overlays, not reality
  • Moments where you genuinely cannot say whether you exist or not — and it doesn't bother you

Common Pitfalls

  • Intellectualizing: "I get it, it's neither!" — understanding the idea is not doing the technique
  • Choosing one pole: "I'll go with not-knowing, it sounds more spiritual" — the technique requires both, then neither
  • Trying this as your first technique — without prior grounding, it can feel like vertigo rather than freedom
  • Expecting a blinding insight — the result is subtler: a gentle absence of all conclusion

Questions From The Book

You call the inner space emptiness, but also call it being. How can something be both empty and full?

Chapter 80, "All and Nothing Mean the Same"

Osho's answer, in essence: "All" and "nothing" are contradictory only in the dictionary, not in existence. If you love all, it is the same as loving no one in particular. If you are everything, you are also nothing specific. These are two ways of pointing at the absolute. One is via negation (empty, void, nothingness), the other via affirmation (fullness, totality, God). Neither is more correct. They meet at the same point — the point where mind's categories break down. The technique takes you there by holding both and then dropping both.

Why did Buddhism get uprooted from India because of emptiness teachings?

Chapter 79, "The Philosophy of Emptiness"

Osho's answer, in essence: India's temperament is devotional, theistic. The idea of total emptiness — no God, no soul, no permanence — felt like nihilism to the Indian heart. People could not understand that Buddha was using emptiness as a method, not a philosophy. He denied God not because God doesn't exist, but because any concept of God becomes a hiding place for the ego. He denied the soul so that no subtle "self" could survive. The technique requires total emptiness as the door — but what you find inside is the fullest fullness. People heard only the door and missed what lay behind it.

Were techniques like "become universal consciousness" meant only for very advanced persons?

Chapter 78, "The Inner Guide"

Osho's answer, in essence: They were meant for very innocent persons, not very advanced ones. An innocent person can hear "you are already that" and simply accept it — and in that acceptance, it becomes actual. We need elaborate techniques only because we cannot trust. We cannot simply hear and be. The simpler you are, the fewer steps you need. The more complex your mind, the more technique is required to exhaust it.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The mind operates in binary code: 1 or 0, yes or no, exists or doesn't exist. This sutra says: what if reality is neither 1 nor 0? What if it is the screen on which both 1 and 0 appear? You cannot think your way to what is beyond thought — but you can exhaust thought by giving it two opposites to hold simultaneously. The mind, unable to resolve the contradiction, eventually surrenders. In that surrender, something else becomes apparent.

A modern analogy: Like a computer encountering an instruction that says "this statement is false." The logical processor halts. In that halt, you become aware of the processor itself — not its output, but its existence. That awareness of the processor (not any calculation it produces) is what the sutra calls "you."

If this feels too advanced — the simpler version: Start with Technique 3 (wherever the mind wanders, this) for a month. Then move to Technique 6 (remain in the subject). Only after those feel natural should you attempt this final dissolution. It is the last door, not the first.

Why this technique is placed near the end of the 112: It requires that you have already become comfortable with paradox, with not-knowing, with the absence of ground. Without that preparation, it feels terrifying. With it, it feels like coming home.
