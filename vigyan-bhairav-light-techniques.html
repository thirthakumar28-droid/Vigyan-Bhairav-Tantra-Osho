The Techniques for Light & Gazing

Ten techniques that use inner light, outer darkness, fire, sky, and the luminosity of awareness itself as doorways to the divine. Light is consciousness made visible; follow it inward and you become the source.

10 techniques · ~14 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These ten techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The light and gazing sutras form the fourth major category — using inner luminosity, outer darkness, fire, sky-gazing, and the radiance of pure attention to dissolve the boundary between seer and seen.

These techniques span the full spectrum: from visualising light rising through the spine, to staring into a black night, to realising that awareness itself is luminous. Some are gentle beginner practices; others are among the most powerful and potentially overwhelming in the entire tantra.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Critical safety note: Techniques 1, 2, and 5 involve powerful energy work. Never leave these incomplete mid-session. Always bring the energy to the crown and release. Read all cautions before beginning.

New to light meditation?

Start with Technique 4 — the clear sky or Technique 9 — wherever attention alights. Both require nothing special and are safe for all levels. Spend 20–40 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.

The fourth state — beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. Pure awareness that witnesses all three states without being any of them.

An ancient Jewish mystical sect (c. 200 BCE – 100 CE) that, according to Osho, experienced God as absolute darkness — the formless, the void before creation.

Nature or matter in Sankhya philosophy — the manifest world as distinguished from purusha (pure consciousness). The fire technique burns prakriti to reveal purusha.

Technique 33 of 112
Category: Emptiness & Void · Tags: sky · blue · serenity · beyond-clouds · simplicity
Simply by looking into the blue sky beyond clouds, the serenity.

In Plain English

Look at the sky — but specifically BEYOND the clouds, into the pure blue. The clouds are thoughts; the blue is the void. Don’t focus on clouds (thoughts) — see PAST them to the infinite blue emptiness. Applied internally: look past your thoughts to the space they move in. That space is the inner void. It is always serene because nothing disturbs it. Thoughts disturb themselves; the space is untouched.

How to Practice

  1. Find a sky with clouds: Unlike the pure-blue-sky technique (which requires cloudless sky), this one NEEDS clouds. The clouds are essential — they are the analogy for thoughts. You need both clouds AND blue to practise this. A partly cloudy day is ideal.
  2. Lie down or sit comfortably: Position yourself where you can see a large expanse of sky without straining your neck. Lying on your back is ideal. Let the body be completely relaxed and supported.
  3. Notice the clouds: First, see the clouds. Watch them move, change shape, dissolve, and reform. They are constantly shifting — never stable, never permanent. This is exactly like your thoughts: constantly shifting, reforming, dissolving.
  4. Now look BEYOND them: Shift your gaze — and your attention — from the clouds to the blue sky BEHIND them. The blue is always there, whether clouds cover it or not. It doesn’t move, doesn’t change, doesn’t react to the clouds. It simply IS. The clouds pass through it without affecting it.
  5. Feel the analogy: The blue sky is your awareness — the void. The clouds are your thoughts. Thoughts pass through awareness like clouds pass through sky. The sky is never disturbed by clouds, no matter how dark or turbulent. Similarly, the void of awareness is never disturbed by thoughts, no matter how intense.
  6. Apply it inwardly: Close your eyes. Now see your THOUGHTS as clouds. Don’t engage with them — notice them moving across the inner sky. And now look PAST them — look at the space they move in. The inner blue sky. The void. It is always there, always serene, always untouched. Thoughts cannot damage it any more than clouds can damage the sky.
  7. Rest in the blue: Both externally (staring past clouds) and internally (seeing past thoughts), REST your attention in the blue. Let the clouds/thoughts do whatever they do — they are not your concern. Your home is the sky, not the clouds. The void, not the thoughts.
  8. The serenity: The sutra says “the serenity” — note the article “the.” Not “a” serenity (one among many) but THE serenity — the absolute, the only, the ultimate. The void is by nature serene. Nothing can disturb it because it has no form to be disturbed. When you rest there, you share its nature. You BECOME serene.
  9. Duration: Start with 20 minutes and extend naturally. Many practitioners find this so enjoyable that they naturally stay 40 minutes or more. There is no upper limit — the sky doesn’t run out of blue, and the void doesn’t run out of serenity.
  10. Daily reminder: Throughout the day, whenever you notice thoughts becoming turbulent (anxiety, anger, worry), remember: you are the sky, not the clouds. Look past the thoughts to the space they move in. Even a 3-second recognition of the inner sky brings instant relief. The void is always accessible — it is closer than your next thought.

Signs It’s Working

  • A felt shift from “watching clouds” to “resting in blue” — attention moves from foreground to background
  • Thoughts become less personal, less urgent — they are just clouds passing
  • A spontaneous serenity that requires no effort to maintain
  • The inner sky feels vast, spacious, utterly peaceful
  • Emotions (even strong ones) begin to feel like weather — passing through, not defining you
  • A growing unshakeability in daily life — disturbances still come but don’t penetrate to the core

Common Pitfalls

  • Engaging with the clouds/thoughts instead of seeing past them — getting caught in content rather than resting in context
  • Trying to push thoughts away or make the sky cloudless — the technique does NOT require thought-free states; it requires seeing PAST thoughts
  • Intellectualising the analogy without actually FEELING the spaciousness — the understanding must be bodily, not merely conceptual
  • Choosing a completely cloudless day (then there is no contrast, no “beyond”) or a completely overcast day (then the blue is not visible)
  • Making it effortful — this is the most effortless technique in the entire tantra; it works by relaxation, not by trying

Questions From The Book

Why does Osho say this is one of the most important techniques?

Chapter 61, "The Serenity of Blue"

Osho explains: because it is completely safe, completely natural, and directly experiential. There is no energy to mishandle, no complex visualisation, no danger of any kind. AND it gives the mind a perfect, liveable analogy for the relationship between awareness and thought. Once you truly understand “I am the sky, not the clouds,” 90% of psychological suffering ends instantly. Most suffering comes from identification WITH thoughts (clouds). This technique breaks that identification permanently.

What if I live in a place without blue sky?

Chapter 61

Osho says: the outer sky is only the beginning. Once the analogy is understood, you can practise entirely with the INNER sky. Close your eyes and look past thoughts to the space they inhabit. That inner space is always blue (metaphorically) — always clear, always serene, always available. The outer sky is training wheels; the inner sky is the real practice. You can do this on a bus, in a meeting, in bed at night. The void is not weather-dependent.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is a perfect metaphor for metacognition — the awareness OF awareness. Normally we are lost IN our thoughts (staring at clouds). This technique shifts attention to the SPACE that contains thoughts (the sky). In psychology, this shift is called “cognitive defusion” — unhooking from thought content to recognise thoughts as passing mental events rather than reality.

ACT therapy parallel: Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) uses exactly this metaphor: “You are the sky; thoughts and feelings are the weather.” Research shows this single shift — from identification with mental content to identification with awareness itself — dramatically reduces anxiety, depression, and psychological rigidity. This 5,000-year-old technique anticipated modern psychotherapy by millennia.

One-line essenceClouds cannot damage the sky. Thoughts cannot damage the void. Look past your thoughts to the blue serenity that was always there — and rest there. You ARE there.
Technique 34 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: listen · while · ultimate · mystical
Listen while the ultimate mystical teaching is imparted. Eyes still, without blinking, at once become absolutely free.

In Plain English

This is the classical Tantric transmission technique — the moment a master whispers his secret into the disciple’s ear. The disciple’s job is total receptivity: eyes open and unblinking, the body still, the mind a vacant ear. Osho is firm that this cannot just be “done” from a book; it is essentially a long waiting. The technique has two halves: the patient ripening, and the instant of total stillness when the words land in a no-mind.

How to Practice

  1. Be honest about what this sutra is. Osho calls it “a secret method.” It is meant for the moment a living master imparts his secret — sometimes a single word whispered in the ear — to a disciple who is ready. If you do not have such a master, this is preparation only: you are building the receptivity in advance.
  2. The first half is the preparation, and that is what most people actually have to do. Become someone who can wait without asking. Osho: “He is not to ask, because asking means desire. He is not to expect, because expectation means conditions. He is just to wait.”
  3. Practise the receptive posture daily. Sit before something you trust as teaching — a passage of a master’s words, a recording, even a photograph — or simply sit, as Lin Chi did on his master’s verandah, with no agenda at all.
  4. Keep the eyes open and as still as possible. No winking. Osho is specific: even a slight movement of the eyes shows inner disturbance. Whenever the eyes flicker, the inside has shifted; let them be stones again.
  5. At the same time, become the ear. Osho’s exact image: “Just become an empty ear with no movement within.” Don’t listen for something — that becomes thinking. Just be open.
  6. Drop expectation altogether. If you are waiting for a particular word, a particular insight, that is the mind. Osho insists: “just waiting — not waiting for something, because then it will become thinking.” Pure waiting, without object.
  7. Don’t strain. The eyes can blink when they have to; tension defeats the technique. The stillness is from the inside — when the mind stops, the eyes stop on their own.
  8. This may take a long time. Lin Chi waited six years on the verandah, in silence, no reading, no talking. The old nun waited until the bamboo of her shoulder-pole broke and the water-pots fell. The technique is not the sitting; the technique is the inner ripening that the sitting allows.
  9. Notice that anything can become the trigger when you are ready — a master’s laughter, the sound of a gong, a pail of water breaking on a moonlit path. Don’t fix on what the moment must look like. The Tantric point: readiness is everything; the trigger is incidental.
  10. If you do receive teaching from a teacher you trust — even reading a sentence that strikes deep — bring this technique to it: stop the eyes, stop the mind, become the ear, let the words fall into vacancy. Don’t analyse afterwards. The seed is sown into emptiness.
  11. If no master is in your life, do not invent one and do not despair. Practise the inner posture daily. Osho says even Shiva’s own words bounce off if the soil is wrong; reverse the rule — if you become the soil, even a small thing will work.
  12. Conclude each sitting without judgement. The success of this technique can never be measured by the sitting itself; it shows in what you become.

Technique 35 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: edge · deep · well · look
At the edge of a deep well look steadily into its depths until – the wondrousness.

In Plain English

Find a real deep well. Stand at the edge, lean over, and look down into its depth without thinking. Osho takes this sutra literally. The well’s depth is reflected in you; if you keep looking long enough, with no thought, you discover the same depth inside. The instruction ends with a single word — the wondrousness. You go on looking until wonder fills you.

How to Practice

  1. Find an actual deep well. Osho is not metaphorical here. A village well, an old stone well, anything with real darkness at the bottom. Failing that, anything with a similar quality of depth — a deep gorge, a quarry, a still dark pond, a chasm — can be used, but a well is best.
  2. Make sure the place is safe. Stand or kneel at the edge so you can lean over and look straight down. Don’t lean further than is steady. The technique requires settled attention, not vertigo.
  3. Look down into the depth. Eyes open. Don’t scan, don’t analyse, don’t identify the shapes you see. Osho: “Look! Do not think. I need not repeat it. Always remember that look means: look, do not think.”
  4. Stay there. Feel the depth pulling your sight further and further down. The well becomes a kind of mirror — the outer depth begins to suggest an inner depth.
  5. Don’t hurry the result. Osho: “Go on looking, go on looking, go on looking, day after day, month after month.” Make it a daily practice if you can. A long, patient gaze is the whole instruction.
  6. Whenever a thought slips in, drop it; return the gaze to the depth. Don’t fight the thought; just don’t feed it. The depth is the stronger object.
  7. Notice the relationship reversing. Like Chuang Tzu watching the river from the bridge until the river stopped and the bridge flowed, with long enough looking the fixed division between “outside well” and “inside me” loosens. The depth is the same depth.
  8. The sutra promises one thing only: the wondrousness. Osho explains this is not a vision, an experience, or a sensation. It is wonder — the simple sense of mystery suddenly all around and within you. Mind disappears; mystery descends.
  9. Don’t stop before that wonder comes. Most people give up after a few minutes because nothing seems to be happening. The technique is built for sustained gazing — if you only do it for ten minutes once, almost certainly nothing happens.
  10. If a real well is not accessible at all, Osho permits a substitute: any object with the quality of unfathomable depth that you can return to daily — a deep pool of clear water, a star-filled night sky used the same way (gazing into rather than at). But the well is preferred because it is dark, vertical, and silent.
  11. Keep silence around the practice. No conversation just before or just after. Walk away gently when you finish.
  12. The day the wondrousness arrives, you will not have to ask whether it has arrived. Until then: look, only look.

Technique 36 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: look · object · slowly · withdraw
Look upon some object, then slowly withdraw your sight from it, then slowly withdraw your thought from it. Then.

In Plain English

Look at any object — a flower, a candle, a temple statue — until thinking has stopped and only the object remains. Then slowly turn your eyes away. The object disappears outwardly but the mental image of it stays. Now drop the inner image too. When the object has gone and the picture of the object has gone, you are left in the bare aloneness Shiva calls Then. That aloneness is the centre.

How to Practice

  1. Choose a single object and stay with it for the whole training. Osho’s example is a roseflower; a candle flame, a temple statue (the original purpose of the image, he says), or any single still object will work. Use the same object every day so the inner image is built up cleanly.
  2. Sit comfortably. Place the object at a comfortable distance, eye-level. Light it well enough to see clearly.
  3. Begin with looking, not thinking. Osho is firm: “Look means: look, do not think. If you think, it is not a look; then you have contaminated everything.” Just take in the object visually. Don’t name it, classify it, comment on it.
  4. Continue looking until thoughts subside on their own and the field of awareness has just one thing in it: this object. There is no hurry — this part may take many minutes the first few times.
  5. When you can feel that “simply the flower is there in your mind, nothing else,” begin the first withdrawal. Slowly slide your gaze a little away from the object — off centre, then further, until the object is no longer in focus. Don’t close the eyes yet.
  6. The outer object is now out of focus, but the mental image will be vivid in your inner mirror. Stay with that image, with the eyes still open and the gaze loose.
  7. Now close the eyes. The image of the object is the only thing in awareness. Don’t try to make it sharper; don’t cling to it. Just let it be there.
  8. Begin the second withdrawal. Don’t fight the image, don’t push it away. Osho’s precise instruction: become indifferent to it. Feel as though you have moved away from the image, as you moved your eyes away from the object — but inwardly. The image fades on its own when you stop facing it.
  9. Wait. The image will dim. Then it will dissolve. Don’t reach for it again when it goes.
  10. What remains is what Shiva calls simply Then. No object, no image, only you, alone. Don’t add anything. Don’t look for an experience. This bare aloneness is the realisation.
  11. If thoughts re-enter, repeat the technique. The skill is in the careful sequence: outer first, inner second. Trying to drop both at once normally fails — that is why the sutra splits the work in two.
  12. Practise daily with the same object. Osho says the second withdrawal becomes much easier once the first is stable. After some weeks the whole sequence can be done in a few minutes, on demand.

Technique 70 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: spine · chakras · light · energy · visualization
Consider your essence as light rays rising from centre to centre up the vertebrae, and so rises livingness in you.

In Plain English

Visualise — no, feel — light rising through your spine from the sex centre at the base to the crown at the top. As the light ascends through each energy centre, feel aliveness intensifying. You are not imagining something false; physics confirms you are made of light particles. This technique asks you to experience that truth directly.

How to Practice

  1. Preparation: Choose a time when you will not be disturbed for at least 45 minutes. Lock the door. Turn off your phone. This is non-negotiable — interruption mid-practice can leave energy stranded in uncomfortable places.
  2. Settle the body: Sit comfortably with spine erect, or lie down flat. Take 5–10 slow breaths. Let the body become heavy, grounded, still. Don’t begin the main practice until the surface mind has quieted.
  3. Develop feeling, not thinking: This is the critical distinction Osho emphasises. Do NOT think “light is rising.” Instead, FEEL warmth, tingling, aliveness. Start by feeling the warmth of your own body — the heat at your sex centre. That heat IS light slowed down. Let yourself feel it as luminous.
  4. First centre (Muladhara/sex centre): Feel a pool of warm golden light at the base of your spine. Don’t rush. Spend several minutes here until the warmth feels real. Let the warmth intensify naturally. It is not imagination — blood flows here, heat is real.
  5. Rising to the navel: When the base feels alive, allow the light to rise — like a slow river of warm honey — up through the belly to the navel centre. Feel the navel glow. Warmth spreads through the abdomen. The belly softens. Stay until it feels alive.
  6. Rising to the heart: Let the river of light continue upward to the heart. Here, something changes: breathing deepens spontaneously, the heartbeat may slow or become more noticeable. A feeling of openness, vulnerability, love may arise. Stay as long as the heart needs. Don’t push feelings away.
  7. Rising to the throat: The light ascends to the throat. You may feel a lump, a desire to speak or sing or cry. Let the throat open. The light purifies whatever is held here. Stay until it feels clear.
  8. Rising to the third eye: The light moves to the space between the eyebrows. Pressure may build here — this is normal. The inner visual field may shift: colours, patterns, or a sense of vast space. Stay until the energy feels ready to move further.
  9. Rising to the crown (sahasrar): Finally, feel the light push through the top of the head. Imagine — feel — the crown opening like a thousand-petalled lotus. Light streams upward into infinite space. You are not contained by the body any longer. Stay here as long as feels natural.
  10. Release into cosmos: Let the light dissolve upward into the sky. You are empty, transparent, clean. The “livingness” the sutra mentions is this: you feel more alive than you have ever felt, because the life-force is no longer blocked at any point.
  11. CRITICAL — Never leave incomplete: If you must stop early, ALWAYS bring the energy to the crown and release it upward before opening your eyes. Energy left at the sex centre or navel creates agitation; at the heart, emotional overwhelm; at the third eye, headaches. Complete the circuit.
  12. Return: When ready, feel the body again. Wiggle toes and fingers. Open eyes slowly. Sit for 2–3 minutes before standing. Drink water.

Technique 71 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: lightning · jump · chakras · energy · spark
Or in the spaces between, feel this as lightning.

In Plain English

This is a variation of the previous technique. Instead of light rising gradually like a river, feel it JUMPING between centres like lightning bolts — instantaneous, electric, sharp. The spaces between the chakras are bridged in a flash. For minds that are naturally jumpy, restless, or male-energy-dominant, this abrupt approach may work better than the slow, gradual river of the previous sutra.

How to Practice

  1. Same preparation as Technique 1: Total privacy. 40–60 minutes of uninterrupted time. Spine erect or lying flat. 5–10 breaths to settle.
  2. Feel the base centre: Become aware of warmth and energy at the base of the spine. Let it gather intensity for 2–3 minutes — like a storm cloud building charge.
  3. FLASH to navel: In one instant — not gradually — feel the energy JUMP from base to navel. Like a lightning bolt. Like a spark leaping a gap. The sensation should be sudden, electric, vivid.
  4. Let the navel absorb: Pause at the navel for 1–2 minutes. Let the energy settle and intensify again. Build the charge.
  5. FLASH to heart: Another instantaneous jump. Base-to-navel was the first bolt; navel-to-heart is the second. Feel the electric shock of arrival at the heart.
  6. Continue upward: Heart to throat — flash. Throat to third eye — flash. Third eye to crown — the final bolt.
  7. At the crown: Feel all the lightning energy collecting and then bursting upward like a thunderbolt into the sky. Complete release.
  8. Rest in the aftermath: After the final release, sit in the profound silence that follows a storm. The body may feel electric, buzzing, incredibly alive.
  9. If too much heat arises: Osho warns that this technique can generate intense heat. If you feel burning, agitation, or overwhelm, SWITCH immediately to the gradual method (Technique 1). Move the energy slowly and gently to the crown. Not everyone is suited to the lightning approach.
  10. Experiment with both: Try the gradual method for a week. Then try the lightning method for a week. Your body will tell you which one is yours. Some people are rivers; some people are lightning. Neither is superior.

What is the difference between this darkness and depression?

Technique 78 of 112
Category: Light & Gazing · Tags: attention · presence · immediacy · this-point · awareness
Wherever your attention alights, at this very point, experience.

In Plain English

This is perhaps the simplest and most profound technique in the entire light category — and it is disguised as nothing special. Wherever your attention goes — to a sound, a colour, a face, a sensation, a thought — at THAT very point, be completely present. Don’t move on. Don’t interpret. Don’t compare. Just EXPERIENCE the raw aliveness of whatever attention touches. The point of attention becomes luminous when you are fully there. The “light” in this technique is the light of awareness itself.

How to Practice

  1. No special conditions needed: This can be done anywhere, anytime, in any posture, with eyes open or closed. It requires nothing except total presence.
  2. Notice where your attention goes: Right now, something is attracting your attention — these words, a background sound, a body sensation, a thought. Whatever it is, DON’T interfere with it. Let attention go where it naturally goes.
  3. At THAT point, be totally present: The key word is “at this very point.” Don’t move to the next thing. Don’t label it. Don’t think about it. Just BE with it. Experience it raw, naked, without the layer of interpretation.
  4. The point becomes luminous: When you are fully present with anything — a sound, a colour, a texture, even a thought — it begins to glow with aliveness. This is not hallucination; it is what happens when attention is undivided. Normally attention is scattered; when collected at one point, it creates a quality of brilliance at that point.
  5. Don’t hold or grasp: When attention naturally moves to something else, let it. But at the NEW point, be fully there again. The practice is not concentration (forcing attention to stay) but presence (being total wherever attention happens to be).
  6. Formal practice (20 min): Sit quietly. Let attention wander freely. But wherever it alights — on a breath, a sound, an itch, a memory — be completely there for as long as it stays. When it moves, follow it with the same totality. Every point of attention becomes a meditation object.
  7. Informal practice (all day): Throughout the day, periodically notice: where is my attention right now? At that point, be fully present for a moment. Then continue with your activity. This takes zero time out of your day but transforms the quality of every moment.
  8. The insight: You will discover that what we call “light” or “consciousness” or “awareness” is simply what happens when attention is total. There is no separate mystical light to seek — the light IS attention itself, fully present. Every moment is already luminous; you just haven’t been fully there to notice.
  9. Don’t make it complicated: This technique is so simple that the mind refuses to believe it can work. The mind wants something complex, esoteric, special. But the sutra is clear: wherever attention alights, at THIS VERY POINT, experience. That’s all. Nothing more is needed.
  10. Deepening: Over time, the gaps between moments of presence shrink. You spend more and more of the day actually HERE rather than lost in commentary about here. This is the gradual illumination of life itself — not through special states but through ordinary attention made total.

How can something so simple be called a “light technique”?
