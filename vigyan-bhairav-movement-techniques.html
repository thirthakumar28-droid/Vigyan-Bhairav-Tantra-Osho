Technique 99 of 112
Category: Self-Inquiry · Tags: pervading · directions · expansion · limitless · everywhere
Feel yourself as pervading all directions, far, near.

In Plain English

Close your eyes and feel yourself expanding in all directions simultaneously — north, south, east, west, up, down. Not the body expanding but AWARENESS expanding. You pervade far and near at the same time. There is no distance that is “away” from you because you ARE all directions. Continue expanding until there is no centre and no periphery — just infinite pervading awareness with no location and no limit.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably with eyes closed: Take 5–7 deep breaths to settle. Let the body become completely still. Bring awareness to the centre of your being — wherever that feels like it is. Start from a point.
  2. Feel awareness expanding forward: From the centre, feel awareness moving forward, through the space in front of you, through the wall, into the distance. Not thinking about distance — FEELING expansion in that direction.
  3. Add backward expansion: Simultaneously feel awareness expanding behind you. Forward and backward at the same time. You are not going somewhere — you already ARE in both directions.
  4. Add left and right: Now feel yourself pervading to the left and to the right simultaneously. Four directions at once. The sense of being located in one spot begins to dissolve.
  5. Add up and down: Feel awareness expanding upward through the ceiling, into the sky, into space. Simultaneously downward through the floor, into the earth, into the depths. Six directions at once.
  6. Let the directions merge: Now stop holding them as separate directions. Let the expansion become spherical — ALL directions at once, simultaneously. You are not expanding in six directions; you are expanding in infinite directions. The sphere of your awareness grows without limit.
  7. Far and near become the same: The sutra says “far, near.” Feel that far and near are equally within you. The wall across the room is as much within your awareness as the sensation of your breath. Both are equally HERE because you pervade all distances.
  8. Dissolve the centre: As expansion continues, the centre from which you started also dissolves. There is no longer a “here” from which you expand — there is only everywhere. No centre, no periphery. Just awareness pervading all directions without location.
  9. Rest as the infinite: In this state of limitless pervading, rest. Don’t try to hold it with effort. Simply allow the natural state of awareness — which has no boundary — to be as it is. You are not creating this; you are releasing the contraction that prevented you from knowing it.
  10. Return gently: After 20–25 minutes, allow attention to softly contract back toward the body. Feel the breath. Feel the body’s weight. Open eyes slowly. But carry the flavour: you are not limited to this point. You pervade. The contraction is voluntary, not necessary.

  • Getting dizzy or ungrounded — if this happens, bring attention to the lower body and earth below before ending
  • Making it a goal or achievement — expansion is not something you accomplish; it is something you allow

Technique 106 of 112
Category: Movement · Tags: empathy · becoming-others · consciousness · compassion · dissolving-self
Feel the consciousness of each person as your own consciousness. So, leaving aside concern for self, become each being.

In Plain English

Look at any person and FEEL their consciousness as your own. Not “what are they thinking” (that’s projection) but feel: the same awareness that looks through my eyes looks through theirs. The same “I am” lives in them. When you leave aside concern for YOUR self entirely and become each being, the isolated self dissolves into the universal consciousness that was always shared.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your setting: Sit in a café, a park, a train station — anywhere people are present and you can observe without being disturbed. Alternatively, begin with a single person: a friend, a family member, even a stranger on a bench. You need at least 20 minutes of uninterrupted observation.
  2. Settle into your own awareness first: Before attempting to feel another’s consciousness, become firmly aware of your OWN consciousness. Close your eyes briefly. Feel: I am aware. I exist. There is a “looking” happening from behind these eyes. This is your starting point — know what consciousness feels like from the inside.
  3. Look at a person: Open your eyes and look at one specific person. Not their clothes, not their face, not their story. Look at the ALIVENESS behind their eyes. The fact that they, too, are looking out from a centre. They, too, feel “I am.”
  4. Feel, don’t think: Do NOT think “they have consciousness too.” That is a philosophy, not an experience. Instead, FEEL: the same awareness that is here, looking through my eyes, is THERE, looking through theirs. It is literally the same awareness wearing a different mask. Feel it as you feel warmth or hunger — directly, not as a concept.
  5. Leave aside concern for self: This is the key instruction. As long as you are concerned with YOUR self (your appearance, your comfort, your thoughts about them), you cannot truly feel another’s consciousness. Drop all self-referencing. Forget yourself entirely. BECOME the other person’s awareness.
  6. Expand to multiple people: Once you can feel one person’s consciousness as your own, expand. Feel two people simultaneously. Then five. Then the entire café. Each one of them: the same awareness, the same “I am,” looking out from different locations. You are all of them.
  7. Include “enemies” and strangers: The real test is with people you dislike, fear, or feel superior/inferior to. Can you feel THEIR consciousness as yours? Can you feel the same awareness looking through the eyes of someone you find repulsive? If yes, the technique is working deeply.
  8. Include non-human beings: Extend the practice to animals — a dog, a bird, an insect. Feel: consciousness is there too, looking out, experiencing. Even plants: feel the aliveness. This is not anthropomorphism — it is the recognition that awareness pervades all living forms.
  9. The dissolving: At some point, the sense of “my” consciousness versus “their” consciousness collapses. There is just one consciousness — everywhere, in everything. The isolated self was a fiction. This dissolving is the goal of the technique.
  10. Carry it into daily life: Once practised formally for 21 days, begin carrying this perception throughout daily interactions. When talking to someone, FEEL their awareness as your own. Conflict dissolves. Judgment dissolves. Compassion arises naturally — not as a moral duty but as the inevitable result of experiencing unity.

  • Intellectualising: thinking ABOUT consciousness rather than FEELING it — keep it experiential
  • Skipping the self-awareness step — you must know what consciousness feels like in yourself before recognising it in others
  • Using this as people-reading or empathic manipulation — the point is dissolution of self, not gaining power over others
  • Becoming overwhelmed in crowds — start with one person, build gradually

How is this different from ordinary empathy or projection?

Chapter 67, "Become Each Being"

Osho is emphatic: ordinary empathy projects YOUR feelings onto others (“I imagine how THEY feel”). This technique does the opposite: you recognise that the SAME awareness lives in both. You are not imagining their experience — you are recognising that the consciousness looking through their eyes is the very same consciousness that looks through yours. Not similar — identical. One ocean with many waves. This is not empathy but identity — at the level of awareness, not personality.

Why does Osho say “leaving aside concern for self” is essential?

Chapter 67

As long as you maintain concern for your separate self — how you look, what they think of you, what you might gain — you remain isolated in your own bubble. The bubble of self-concern prevents you from truly FEELING another’s consciousness. It’s like trying to listen while talking: impossible. You must stop the inner monologue of self-concern to actually FEEL the awareness that lives in another.

Technique 107 of 112
Category: Movement · Tags: one-consciousness · nothing-else · monism · unity · seeing
This consciousness exists as each being, and nothing else exists.

In Plain English

This is the culmination of the previous technique. See that consciousness is the ONLY thing that exists — appearing as different forms (person, tree, rock, sky) but always the same awareness. Nothing else exists except this one consciousness playing all roles. The multiplicity is appearance; the unity is reality. When walking in a crowd, see one consciousness wearing different masks. When in nature, see one consciousness being trees, birds, river, sky.

How to Practice

  1. Master Technique 98 first: This technique builds on the previous one. You must already be able to feel another person’s consciousness as your own. If you cannot, return to Technique 98 and practise for at least 21 days before attempting this one.
  2. Sit in a rich environment: A park, a busy street, a natural landscape — anywhere where MANY forms are present: people, animals, plants, buildings, sky. You need multiplicity around you to practise seeing through it to the unity beneath.
  3. Begin with people: Look at the crowd and feel: one consciousness looking through all these eyes. Not many consciousnesses — ONE. The same awareness playing many characters. A theater with one actor in countless costumes.
  4. Extend to animals: See a bird. Feel: the same consciousness that looks through my eyes looks through those tiny black eyes. The same “I am” flies in that body. Not a different consciousness — the same one. Consciousness does not have boundaries — it fills whatever form it inhabits completely.
  5. Extend to plants: See a tree. Feel: consciousness is there too, but slower, quieter, more patient. The same awareness that rushes through the human mind stands still and tall in the tree for a hundred years. Same consciousness, different tempo.
  6. Extend to “inanimate” matter: This is the radical step. See a rock. Feel: even here, consciousness rests. In deep sleep, as it were. Not dead matter but consciousness so deeply at rest it appears inert. Modern physics confirms: matter is energy, energy is vibration, and vibration requires... a field in which to vibrate. That field is consciousness.
  7. The key insight — “nothing else exists”: Now see it all at once. Everything you look at — EVERYTHING — is consciousness in different costumes. There is literally nothing else. Not consciousness PLUS matter, not consciousness INSIDE matter, but consciousness AS everything. The chair, the sky, your hand, the sound of traffic — all consciousness appearing in different forms.
  8. Let multiplicity dissolve: Hold this perception steadily. The apparent diversity of the world — so many separate things! — begins to shimmer and reveal its underlying unity. Like looking at a movie screen: at first you see characters, plot, scenery. Then you step back and see: it’s all just light on a screen. One light, infinite images.
  9. Rest in the seeing: When the perception stabilises even for a moment, rest in it. Don’t grasp. Don’t try to hold it. Just rest. One consciousness, everywhere, being everything. Nothing else exists. You are That. Everything is That.
  10. Return gently: When ready, allow the ordinary multiplicity to return. But notice: it never quite looks the same again. Even when you see separate things, you remember what you saw. The unity becomes a background knowing that informs all perception.

Questions From The Book

What does Osho mean by “nothing else exists”?

Chapter 67, "Nothing Else Exists"

Osho explains: this is the most radical statement in tantra. Not that consciousness exists alongside matter (dualism), not that consciousness is one of many things (pluralism), but that consciousness is the ONLY thing that exists (monism). What we call matter, energy, space, time — all are forms of consciousness. When you see a stone, you are seeing consciousness in its most condensed, sleeping form. When you see a human being, you are seeing consciousness in its most awake form. But the substance is always the same. One ocean, many waves — but the waves ARE the ocean.

How does this relate to modern physics?

Chapter 67

Osho notes that physics has already dissolved matter into energy (E=mc²), and energy into probability waves that only “collapse” into definite states when observed. The observer — consciousness — turns out to be inseparable from the observed. The tantra arrived at this conclusion 5,000 years ago through inner observation: everything is consciousness appearing as form. Physics approaches the same truth from the outside.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is the quintessential non-dual (advaita) teaching: there is only one “thing” — awareness — and it appears as the entire manifest world. The technique asks you to shift from the default mode of seeing many separate things to seeing one consciousness expressing itself in infinite forms. This is not a metaphysical claim to adopt but a perceptual shift to undergo.

Phenomenological parallel: When you dream at night, the entire dream world — characters, landscape, events — is made of one substance: your dreaming mind. This technique suggests that waking reality has the same structure: one consciousness dreaming the world into being. The forms are real (as appearances), but the substance is always and only consciousness.

Technique 108 of 112
Category: Movement & Body · Tags: consciousness · spirit · guidance · each
This consciousness is the spirit of guidance of each one. Be this one.

In Plain English

The same consciousness that runs the body without consulting the mind — that beats the heart, balances the inner ear, knows when you are about to fall — is also a guide for living. Osho calls it the inner guide. It does not argue, it does not explain; it simply rises from below the navel as a clear pull when the head has been quieted. The technique is to stop using the head where the inner guide is the right tool, and to let it move you.

How to Practice

  1. This is a daily-life technique, not only a sit. But begin with a sit, because the head must be still enough for the inner guide to be heard. Sit until thinking has become a soft hum rather than a roar.
  2. Learn the felt difference between mind and inner guide. Osho describes it precisely: when something comes from the inner guide, it rises upward — you feel a flow, a warmth, beginning around the navel and moving up. Mind, by contrast, sits at the surface of the head and pushes downward. Sit and watch your decisions for a few days; you will start to feel which one is which.
  3. Choose a small situation to test it. Not the wedding, not the job change — small things first. Where to sit in a restaurant. Which book to pick up. Whether to call a friend now or later. Don’t reason; pause, feel, and act on the upward rise.
  4. Don’t expect explanations. Reason wants proofs; the inner guide does not provide them. Osho: it “simply bubbles up.” Don’t demand evidence and don’t back-rationalise.
  5. Trust the small successes. When the inner guide turns out right, notice. The mind will dismiss it as luck; don’t let it. Each confirmation builds the courage to use it the next time.
  6. Use it in genuinely puzzled moments — the moments when no map applies. Osho gives the example of being lost in a forest with no path and nobody to ask. Sit under a tree. Let the chatter subside. When a moment of non-thinking arrives, stand up and start walking. Let the body move where it moves; you stay a witness. Don’t interfere. Practise this kind of surrender on small “forests”: a strange neighbourhood, a stuck creative project, a tangled relationship.
  7. Don’t skip the use of reason where reason is the right tool. Madame Curie’s insight came in a dream — after she had exhausted the head with weeks of work. The inner guide does not replace clear thinking; it supplements it where thinking has run out. Use both, in order.
  8. Practise in fast, no-time situations. Driving on a sudden hazard. A child about to fall. Words needed in a moment of conflict. There is no time to think; whoever does not have the inner guide available is too late. The samurai sword image Osho uses is precise: when there is no time, only the inner guide can act.
  9. Don’t outsource. The outer teacher, says Osho, can only point you to your inner guide; once found, the outer teacher is no longer needed. Don’t replace your inner guide with someone else’s.
  10. Be ready for the inner guide to lead you into difficulty. It is not a comfort-machine. If it pulls you towards a hard decision, follow. Osho is direct: even if it leads into danger, go — that danger is your path of growth.
  11. Notice the second half of the sutra: “Be this one.” The aim is not just to consult the inner guide occasionally, but to be the consciousness that guides every being. Don’t identify only with the head and ask the heart for advice; gradually move your sense of “I” downward, to where the guidance is.
  12. Over months, the inner guide stops being a separate voice and becomes simply how you live — an unhurried, accurate, quiet movement that does not require the chatter that used to precede every action.

The Techniques for Movement

Five techniques that use the movement of consciousness — not physical movement but the shift of awareness from the isolated self into the universal, from the bounded body into infinite space. These are techniques of expansion, dissolving, and the ultimate freedom of realising you are everywhere.

5 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These five techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The movement sutras form the ninth major category — but “movement” here does not mean physical motion. It means the movement of consciousness itself: expanding beyond the body, dissolving the isolated self, entering the space of pure awareness.

These techniques range from empathic identification with others (feeling another’s consciousness as your own) to the absolute stillness of entering supportless, eternal space. They represent a progression: from recognising consciousness in others, to seeing it as the only thing that exists, to releasing the body, to releasing thought, and finally to entering the groundless ground of being itself.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Note on “movement”: In this category, movement refers to the movement of consciousness, not necessarily physical movement. These are techniques of inner expansion — consciousness moving beyond its habitual boundaries into its true unlimited nature.

New to movement meditation?

Start with Technique 1 — feel the consciousness of each person or Technique 3 — toss attachment for body aside. Both are accessible for beginners and produce tangible results within days. Spend 20–30 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.
