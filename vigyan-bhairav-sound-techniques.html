The Techniques for Sound & Mantra

Eight techniques that use sound — spoken, heard, imagined, or fading — as a bridge from the noisy mind to the silent centre. Sound is the subtlest form of matter; follow it inward and it dissolves into pure awareness.

8 techniques · ~10 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These eight techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The sound and mantra sutras form the third major category — using vibration, resonance, and the dying echo of a tone to carry the mind from word to wordlessness.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don't shop, don't switch, don't combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn't working — that is the work. Stay.

New to sound meditation?

Start with Technique 2 — bathe in the centre of sound. It requires nothing but ears and a willingness to listen without labelling. Spend 20 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for what comes next.

Technique 37 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: letters · feeling · dissolution · freedom
Devi, imagine the Sanskrit letters in these honey-filled foci of awareness, first as letters, then more subtly as sounds, then as most subtle feeling. Then, leaving them aside, be free.

In Plain English

Take any word or syllable. First see it as written form. Then hear it as pure sound, dropping the meaning. Then feel it as vibration in the body. Then drop even that — and what remains is freedom, the awareness prior to all form.

How to Practice

  1. Choose a word — a Sanskrit syllable like AUM, or any word that resonates.
  2. Visualise it as written letters. See the shape in your mind's eye.
  3. Now drop the visual — hear only the sound. Repeat it mentally or aloud.
  4. Drop the meaning of the sound. Let it become pure vibration, a humming in the body.
  5. Drop even the vibration. Leave everything aside.
  6. Rest in what remains — the silence after all layers have been removed.

Questions From The Book

Why Sanskrit specifically? Can any language work?

Chapter 25, "From Words to Pure Sounds to Being"

Osho clarifies: the sutra mentions Sanskrit because it was the original language of the text. But any language works — because the technique moves away from language entirely. The point is to go from the gross (written form) through the subtle (sound) to the most subtle (feeling) and then beyond. The language is the starting material, not the destination.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Language is layered: symbol (visual) → phoneme (auditory) → meaning (cognitive) → felt-sense (somatic). This technique deconstructs that stack, peeling each layer off. What remains is awareness itself — prior to representation.

Linguistic parallel: Semantic satiation (repeating a word until it loses meaning) is the accidental, trivial version of this. The sutra makes it intentional and takes it further — past meaning-loss into body-vibration, then past even that into formlessness.

One-line essenceWords veil the silence. Peel them back — form, sound, feeling, nothing — and the silence was always already here.
Technique 38 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: listening · waterfall · omnipresence · immersion
Bathe in the centre of sound, as in the continuous sound of a waterfall. Or, by putting the fingers in the ears, hear the sound of sounds.

In Plain English

Immerse yourself in ambient sound — traffic, rain, a fan, a waterfall — until you are surrounded by it from all directions. Then find the centre of that sound-sphere: that centre is you, the listener. Or plug your ears and listen to the inner sound of your own body.

How to Practice

  1. Sit anywhere sound is continuous — near traffic, running water, a fan, rain.
  2. Close your eyes. Let the sound arrive from all directions simultaneously.
  3. Don't label individual sounds. Let them merge into one ocean of vibration.
  4. Feel yourself at the centre of this sphere of sound. You are the hub; sound is the wheel.
  5. Rest at the centre. The sounds come to you; you don't go to them.
  6. Alternative: press thumbs gently into ears. Listen to the rushing inner sound (blood, neural activity). Stay with it for 10–20 minutes.

Questions From The Book

How does listening to noise lead to silence?

Chapter 25, "Bathe in Sound"

Osho explains: the technique doesn't fight noise — it uses it. When you merge with the totality of sound without preference, the labelling mind stops. The mind needs to distinguish, categorise, and name. When sound is allowed as one mass, the mind has nothing to do — and falls silent naturally. The noise continues; you find silence at its centre.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This is open-monitoring meditation applied specifically to sound. Instead of focusing on one sound (concentrative), you receive all sounds equally. The brain's auditory cortex, overwhelmed by non-discriminative input, eventually quiets the categorisation circuits. What remains is pure listening — awareness without object.

Modern parallel: Sound baths (singing bowls, gongs) work on this principle — saturate the auditory field so the mind gives up trying to track individual sounds. This ancient sutra teaches you to do it with whatever sounds already exist around you.

One-line essenceYou cannot find silence by fighting noise. Drown in the noise — and at its very centre, silence waits.
Technique 39 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: AUM · chanting · merging · vibration
Intone a sound, as AUM, slowly. As sound enters soundfulness, so do you.

In Plain English

Chant AUM (or any single tone) slowly and with total attention. As the sound fills you completely — until there is no gap between you and the sound — you enter the state Osho calls "soundfulness": sound without a separate listener.

How to Practice

  1. Sit upright. Take three slow breaths to settle.
  2. Inhale fully. On the exhale, chant "AUM" slowly — let each syllable (A-U-M) take several seconds.
  3. Feel the vibration in the belly (A), chest (U), and skull (M).
  4. Don't just hear the sound — become it. Let there be no listener separate from the chanting.
  5. As the echo fades at the end of each AUM, ride the silence for a moment before the next.
  6. Continue for 20–30 minutes. Let the chant become slower and slower over time.

Questions From The Book

What is "soundfulness" and how is it different from just hearing?

Chapter 27, "Soundlessness, Soundfulness"

Osho says: hearing implies a hearer and a heard — two things. Soundfulness is non-dual: the sound has filled you so completely that the listener has dissolved. It's like a sponge soaked in water — you can no longer find the sponge separate from the water. You become the vibration. That is the door — because if the listener has dissolved, what remains is pure consciousness.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Chanting AUM stimulates the vagus nerve through slow exhalation and laryngeal vibration. fMRI studies show AUM chanting deactivates the amygdala and default mode network more effectively than quiet rest. The "merging" experience correlates with reduced self-referential processing.

Overtone science: The three syllables A-U-M create distinct resonance chambers in the body. The transition between them produces overtones that the inner ear registers even when the conscious mind doesn't. These overtones may explain the "filling" quality.

One-line essenceChant slowly enough that you dissolve into the chant. When only sound remains, that is the doorway.
Technique 40 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: beginning · awakening · subtle · alertness
In the beginning and gradual refinement of the sound of any letter, awake.

In Plain English

When you speak or chant any letter or syllable, bring total awareness to the very first instant the sound is born — the spark before it becomes full voice. That initial impulse, caught with alertness, is a moment of pure awakening.

How to Practice

  1. Choose any simple sound — "Ah", "Oh", "Mm", or a single letter.
  2. Before you voice it, notice the impulse — the micro-moment when intention becomes vibration.
  3. Slowly begin the sound. Catch the very first edge where silence becomes audible.
  4. As the sound develops, notice how it refines — becomes clearer, fuller.
  5. Stay alert through the entire arc: birth, growth, peak, decay, silence.
  6. Repeat. Each time, try to catch the beginning even earlier — the pre-sound impulse.

Questions From The Book

Why focus on the beginning of a sound rather than the sound itself?

Chapter 27, "The Beginning of Sound"

Osho explains: the beginning is where creation happens. At the beginning of any phenomenon, energy is fresh, undivided, pure. Once the sound is established, the mind relaxes and goes unconscious again. But at the beginning, you must be totally present — because beginnings demand alertness. The technique exploits this natural demand.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Neuroscience calls this "event-related potential" — the brain's response to the onset of a stimulus is always stronger than to its continuation. This sutra aligns with that: catch the onset, because that's when attention naturally peaks. By making onset-catching deliberate, you train sustained alertness.

Mindfulness parallel: Noting practice in Vipassana ("arising, arising") is similar — catching phenomena at their birth point. This sutra focuses it specifically on sound, making it more tangible.

One-line essenceEvery sound has a birthday — a spark from silence into vibration. Catch that spark, and you are awake.
Technique 41 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: music · strings · composite · omnipresence
While listening to stringed instruments, hear their composite central sound; thus omnipresence.

In Plain English

When listening to a sitar, guitar, or any stringed instrument, don't follow individual notes. Instead, listen for the one unified sound beneath all the notes — the "hum" that holds the music together. That central sound is omnipresent, and finding it makes you omnipresent too.

How to Practice

  1. Play or listen to any stringed instrument — sitar, tanpura, guitar, cello, harp.
  2. Close your eyes. Don't follow the melody or individual notes.
  3. Listen for the one continuous sound underneath — the drone, the resonance, the overtone that persists even as notes change.
  4. Let individual notes fade into background. Only the central hum matters.
  5. Merge with that hum. Feel it as the sound of existence itself.
  6. Stay in this listening for the duration of the music, and then in the silence after.

Questions From The Book

How can a stringed instrument create omnipresence?

Chapter 27, "The Composite Central Sound"

Osho explains: every stringed instrument produces overtones — sounds above and below the played note. When you stop tracking individual notes and listen to the totality, you hear something that has no single source. It seems to come from everywhere. That "everywhere" quality is what the sutra means by omnipresence. You merge with a sound that has no boundary — and therefore you, the listener, become boundless.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Psychoacoustics shows that our brain localises sounds by their transients (attacks). Sustained, resonant tones with rich overtones lose localisation — they seem to come from "everywhere." The sutra exploits this: by attending to the sustained resonance rather than the note-attacks, you shift from focused to diffuse auditory processing — and with it, your sense of self diffuses.

Nada yoga: This is the basis of the Indian tradition of Nada Yoga — using sound (particularly the tanpura drone) as a meditation vehicle. The drone has no melody, no rhythm — only pure resonance. It is this technique in instrument form.

One-line essenceBeneath every melody is one sound. Find it, and you find the sound of existence itself — which is everywhere, and so are you.
Technique 42 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: fading · feeling · heart · passage
Intone a sound audibly, then less and less audibly as feeling deepens into this silent harmony.

In Plain English

Chant any sound aloud. Gradually make it quieter — softer and softer — until only the feeling of the sound remains, without any audible vibration. The sound was the vehicle; the feeling is the destination.

How to Practice

  1. Choose a sound — AUM, a loved one's name, a favourite mantra, or any word.
  2. Begin chanting it at normal volume. Full voice.
  3. After a few minutes, reduce the volume — still audible, but softer.
  4. Continue reducing. Whisper. Then move to lip-only movement with no sound.
  5. Then drop the lips. Only the mental sound remains.
  6. Then drop the mental sound. Only the feeling — the vibration, the emotion, the quality — remains.
  7. Rest in that feeling. It is the silent harmony the sutra speaks of.

Questions From The Book

What is the "feeling" that remains when sound disappears?

Chapter 29, "Sound as Passage toward Feeling"

Osho says: the sound always carries a feeling — like a boat carries a passenger. When the boat reaches the shore (silence), the passenger (feeling) steps off. The feeling is not an emotion specifically — it is the vibratory quality of the sound experienced in the heart rather than the ears. A-U-M has a feeling-tone of vastness and completion. Your name has a feeling-tone of identity. A lover's name has a feeling-tone of devotion. The technique uses sound to reach that tone, then discards the carrier.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Cognitive science distinguishes between "cold" processing (conceptual) and "hot" processing (felt-sense). Sound starts as auditory cortex activity (cold) but gradually recruits limbic areas (hot) through repetition and emotional association. By progressively fading the auditory input while maintaining attention, you shift entirely into felt-sense mode — embodied, non-verbal awareness.

Japa tradition: This is the classical progression of japa (mantra repetition): vaikhari (spoken) → upamshu (whispered) → manasik (mental) → ajapa (happening by itself, only feeling). This sutra makes that entire journey explicit in one sitting.

One-line essenceSound is the boat. Feeling is the shore. Let the boat dissolve, and you arrive where you always were.
Technique 43 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: mouth · slightly · open · keep
With mouth slightly open, keep mind in the middle of the tongue. Or, as breath comes silently in, feel the sound “hh.”

In Plain English

Move the focus of mind out of the head and into the centre of the tongue. Open the mouth slightly — not for speaking, but as if you are about to speak — and place attention exactly in the middle of the tongue. Thinking, says Osho, is talking-within; the silent vibration of thought rises from a centre in the middle of the tongue. Hold awareness there, and the inner talking stops.

How to Practice

  1. Sit upright in any comfortable posture. Eyes closed.
  2. Open the mouth very slightly — just enough that the lips part. Osho is precise: “as if you are going to speak, not like when you are speaking, but like when you are just going to speak.”
  3. Don’t move the tongue. Let it rest naturally inside the open mouth.
  4. Now bring the whole of your attention — what Osho calls “the focus of the mind” — out of the head and down to the exact middle of the tongue. Feel as if you are not thinking from the head any longer; you are located in the centre of the tongue.
  5. This will feel strange. That is the point. Osho says you will discover a control-centre for thought right there in the middle of the tongue. Stay on it.
  6. Notice that thought is talking-within. Watch the tongue while you think a sentence: it actually vibrates very slightly. The vibrations come from the middle of the tongue outward. Confirm this for yourself; it is not a metaphor.
  7. When attention is fully placed at that centre, the inner talking begins to stop on its own. Don’t try to stop it — just be at the middle of the tongue. Thinking falls because its source is being held still by attention.
  8. Stay with this until you feel a quality Osho describes as solidity — an inner stillness, almost like becoming immovable. Thought is the inner motion; without it, you are momentarily out of motion.
  9. Now move on to the second half of the sutra (the “Or” option, which Osho says combines naturally with the first). Keep the mouth slightly open. As the breath comes in, listen for the soft sound “hh” — the natural hush of inhalation passing across the open mouth and tongue.
  10. Do not create the sound. Osho is firm: “If you create it, you have missed the point.” Listen only. The sound is barely audible — you must become very alert to hear it at all.
  11. Listen to “hh” only on the in-breath, not the out-breath. The out-breath carries you outward; you want the inward direction. Let each in-breath sound the soft “hh.”
  12. As you continue, you will notice the sound is not only on the tongue. It begins to be heard in the throat, very faintly. Then deeper still, in the heart. Osho: “When it reaches the heart, you have gone beyond mind.” Stay with whichever level of the “hh” you can hear, and the next level will open on its own.

Technique 44 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: center · sound
Center on the sound “aum” without any “a” or “m.”

In Plain English

Aum is not one sound; it is three sounds — a, u, m — fused together. Intone Aum and learn to hear the three separately inside it. Then drop the “a” and the “m,” leaving only the middle “u.” The technique is not really about Aum — it is a way of forcing the ear to become so sensitive that the mind cannot keep working alongside it. Osho calls it a method for those with a sensitive ear: musicians, poets, anyone whose hearing is alive.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably in a quiet place where you can hear small sounds. The technique depends on auditory delicacy.
  2. Begin to chant Aum aloud, slowly. Pronounce it fully: a-u-m. Don’t rush. Each chant should last as long as a comfortable out-breath.
  3. While chanting, listen. Try to hear the three sounds inside the single Aum. They are infused together but, Osho says, distinct — very close, but separate.
  4. Don’t pronounce them as three: don’t say “a-u-m” in pieces. The chant remains one continuous sound. The work is in the hearing, not in the speaking.
  5. Continue chanting day after day. This may take time. Osho is honest: if your ear has not been trained, this is difficult. Train it — sit by a river or in the wind and listen for the changing nuances of natural sound. Sensitivity grows by use.
  6. One day the three sounds will separate clearly inside the chant. You will hear: a… u… m, woven into one breath.
  7. Now begin to soften the “a” at the start. Let it become smaller, less voiced, until it drops away.
  8. Then soften the “m” at the end the same way. The closing hum becomes less and less, until it too drops.
  9. What remains is only the middle “u” — pure, sustained, with no beginning “a” and no closing “m.” This sound has no name in ordinary speech; you can no longer call it Aum because the boundary-sounds are gone.
  10. Centre on this sound. Osho explains the secret: by now your ear is so finely engaged and your attention so absorbed that the mind has nothing left to do. The mantra is incidental — the real work was the sensitivity it forced.
  11. Don’t expect a particular experience. Osho recounts the boy who was given the Zen koan “hear the sound of one hand,” and after long sincere effort returned and said: “I have heard, but it is soundlessness.” The same may happen to you. The pure middle sound dissolves into a silent, attentive, alive emptiness.
  12. If you find this technique too subtle, Osho gives an honest verdict: it is not for everyone. Try the tongue-centre technique above (#43) or the “hh” instead. The 112 sutras are a buffet; pick what your ear can actually do.

Technique 45 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: AH · exhale · release · death · spontaneity
Silently intone a word ending in "AH." Then in the "HH," effortlessly, the spontaneity.

In Plain English

Choose any word that ends with the sound "AH" (like "Allah," "Buddha," "mama," or simply "ahhh"). Intone it silently, emphasising the final "AH." That "AH" is an exhale, a release, a letting-go. In the effortless silence after the "H" sound — spontaneity, the uncaused, arises.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably. Eyes closed.
  2. Choose a word ending in "AH" — or simply use the sound "Ahhh" alone.
  3. Silently (mentally) intone the word. Feel the "AH" as a complete exhale — everything leaving.
  4. At the end of the "AH," notice the trailing breath: "HH..." — a whisper of silence.
  5. In that "HH" — don't do anything. Don't intend, don't seek. Let whatever arises arise by itself.
  6. That is spontaneity — action without a doer, experience without an experiencer.
  7. Repeat for 20 minutes.

Questions From The Book

Why specifically "AH"? What is special about this sound?

Chapter 31, "From Sound to Inner Silence"

Osho explains: "AH" is the sound of complete exhalation — you cannot say "AH" while inhaling. It is the sound of letting go, the sound of death (the last breath is always an out-breath). By emphasising "AH," you practise a miniature death — a total release of holding. And in that release, life springs back fresh, unconditioned, spontaneous. It is renewal through surrender.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The "AH" sound lengthens the exhale, which directly stimulates the vagus nerve and shifts the nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-release). The "spontaneity" that follows is the body's natural response when the grip of conscious control loosens — what somatic therapists call "the unwind."

Somatic parallel: Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing uses spontaneous micro-movements after release as signs of trauma discharge. This sutra does the same thing: create a total exhale (release), then let whatever needs to happen, happen — without directing it.

One-line essence"AH" is the sound of letting go. In the silence after, life moves by itself — and that movement is grace.
Technique 46 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: stopping · ears · pressing · rectum
Stopping ears by pressing and the rectum by contracting, enter the sound.

In Plain English

Press your fingers into your ears so all outer sound is shut out, and at the same time pull the muscles of the anus upward and contract them. Two doors close at once. With both shut, an inner sound — a kind of pillar of soundlessness — appears inside the body. Enter it. Without the rectum contracted, Osho says, this very subtle sound leaks out and you cannot find it.

How to Practice

  1. Sit upright in a comfortable posture — cross-legged on the floor, or on a firm chair with feet flat on the ground. Spine straight.
  2. Use your thumbs or index fingers to press firmly into both ears, sealing the ear canals. Press hard enough that outer sound is genuinely shut out. The other fingers can rest on the head or face.
  3. At the same time, pull the rectum (anal sphincter and pelvic-floor muscles) upward and contract. This is the same muscle action used to stop urination mid-flow, but with the anal area too — a firm, lifting squeeze. Hold the contraction.
  4. So you have closed two openings simultaneously: the ears above, the rectum below. The body is now sealed at both ends along its central channel.
  5. Close your eyes. Don’t do anything else. Just be inside the closed body.
  6. Listen. With outer sound shut out, an inner sound is audible — usually first as a low hum or hiss, sometimes as a high tone. Osho calls it “a pillar of sound” rising in you. Many people normally don’t hear it because, he says, the rectum is the opening through which this very subtle sound escapes; once you contract, the sound is held inside and becomes vivid.
  7. Don’t name what you hear, don’t classify it. Just let it be loud in you. The instruction is “enter the sound” — not analyse it, not chase it, but move into it as into a room.
  8. Let the sound become bigger than you. The body becomes a vessel filled with this inner pillar. Osho says it produces a deep feeling of fulfilment, as if the whole world has stopped and time has stopped.
  9. If the rectum contraction tires, release briefly and re-contract. Don’t strain. The contraction must be firm but not painful. The same with the ear-pressure: firm seal, not crushing.
  10. Stay 10–20 minutes the first times. As the inner sound becomes available you can sit longer.
  11. Release slowly. First release the rectum. Then take the fingers out of the ears one at a time. Sit a moment with eyes closed before opening them. Outer sound will seem unusually crude for a few minutes — this is normal.
  12. This is a body technique. Osho is clear that most modern people are insensitive to the body and so miss it; if at first nothing happens, treat your body as a sensitive instrument that needs a few sessions to wake up. Practise daily.

Osho’s Key Insight

Tantra is not a philosophy. Rather, it is a science with one difference: science is objective, Tantra is subjective. But still, it is a science and not a philosophy. Philosophy thinks about the truth, the unknown, the ultimate; science tries to discover what is. Science enters the immediate; philosophy thinks of the ultimate. Philosophy is always looking toward the sky; science is more down to ...

Technique 47 of 112
Category: Sound & Mantra · Tags: name · identity · mantra · dissolution
Enter the sound of your name and, through this sound, all sounds.

In Plain English

Repeat your own name as a mantra. Not to reinforce identity, but to dissolve it — because when a name is repeated enough, it loses meaning and becomes pure sound. Through your name, enter sound itself. Through sound itself, enter silence.

How to Practice

  1. Sit quietly. Close your eyes.
  2. Begin repeating your own name — aloud at first, then softer, then mentally.
  3. Don't think about who the name belongs to. Let it be just a sound.
  4. As meaning drops away, the name becomes a vibration — like any other mantra.
  5. Follow that vibration inward. It leads to the same silence as any other sound.
  6. From that silence, all sounds arise — including your name. Rest in the source.

Won't repeating my name make me more egotistic?

After enough repetition, you stand behind the name — and for the first time, you know you are not it.
