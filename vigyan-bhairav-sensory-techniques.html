The Techniques for the Senses

Eight techniques that transform the senses from sources of distraction into doorways of awakening. Osho insists: the senses are not the enemy — they are the bridge. Use them totally, and they dissolve into awareness itself.

8 techniques · ~12 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These eight techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The sensory sutras form the eighth major category — using taste, touch, sight, the shock of natural gaps, and the freshness of beginner’s mind to bypass the thinking layer and arrive directly at presence.

Tantra’s fundamental insight: the senses are NOT obstacles to meditation. They are the most immediate doorways to it. Every other spiritual path says “close the senses, withdraw.” Tantra says “USE the senses so totally that only awareness remains.” These eight techniques demonstrate exactly how.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: The senses are doorways, NOT distractions. Osho emphasises this repeatedly. Every tradition that tells you to suppress the senses creates internal war. These techniques create internal peace by USING the senses as bridges to awareness.

New to sensory meditation?

Start with Technique 64 — become the taste or Technique 69 — touch eyeballs as feathers. Both are gentle, require nothing special, and produce results within days. Spend 21 days with one before exploring further.

Technique 63 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: sneeze · fright · gaps · shock · continuous-awareness
At the start of sneezing, during fright, in anxiety, above a chasm, flying in battle, in extreme curiosity, at the beginning of hunger, at the end of hunger, be uninterruptedly aware.

In Plain English

Nature gives you free meditation moments every day — instants when the mind stops involuntarily. The moment before a sneeze. A sudden fright. Looking over a cliff edge. Extreme curiosity. The first pang of hunger and the satisfaction when it ends. In all these moments, thought stops naturally. The technique: instead of missing these gaps, USE them. Stay alert THROUGH them. They are doorways that nature opens for you — walk through.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the principle first: Between any two thoughts there is a gap. Normally this gap is so tiny you miss it. But certain situations FORCE the gap to widen: sneezing, shock, fright, vertigo, extreme curiosity, hunger pangs. In these moments, the thinking machinery halts involuntarily. The technique is to be AWARE during these natural halts.
  2. Make a list of YOUR gaps: Osho suggests cataloguing the moments in your life when the mind naturally stops. For some it’s sneezing. For others it’s the moment of jumping into cold water. For others it’s the instant of unexpected news. Identify YOUR natural gap-moments.
  3. The sneeze practice: When you feel a sneeze building, become extremely alert. Don’t suppress the sneeze — let it come — but stay AWARE through the entire arc: the building, the explosion, the aftermath. In the instant of the sneeze itself, thinking stops completely. Catch that gap. What is there when thought is absent? Pure awareness.
  4. The fright practice: You cannot manufacture real fright, but you can use surprises. Ask a friend to startle you at random moments. Or notice natural frights: a car horn, a door slamming, someone appearing unexpectedly. In that first instant of fright — BEFORE the reaction — there is a gap of pure awareness. Catch it.
  5. The vertigo practice: Stand at the edge of a safe height (balcony with railing, cliff edge with barrier). Look down. The mind reels and stops. In that moment of vertigo, thinking ceases. Stay aware. Don’t grab the railing out of panic — you are safe. Simply notice the gap.
  6. The curiosity practice: When something creates extreme curiosity — an unopened letter, a mystery about to be revealed, a door about to open — notice: in the instant BEFORE the revelation, the mind is completely still, completely attentive. That stillness IS the meditation.
  7. The hunger practice: When hunger first arises as a pang, catch that instant. The body sends a signal; the mind has not yet reacted. In that first flash of hunger there is pure sensation without interpretation. Similarly: the first bite of food when very hungry — the satisfaction is so total that the mind stops. Be aware in THAT moment.
  8. Make it continuous: The sutra says “uninterruptedly aware.” This means: don’t just catch one gap per day. Make it a 24-hour practice. Whenever ANY gap naturally occurs — be there. Over time, you develop a background awareness that is always watching for these openings.
  9. Evening review: Each night before sleep, review: how many gaps did I catch today? What happened in them? The review itself sharpens the sensitivity for the next day. Within a week, you will begin catching gaps you never noticed before.
  10. The integration: After weeks of practice, something extraordinary happens: you realise the gaps are not separate from the flow of life. The awareness that catches the gaps begins to pervade the non-gap moments too. The background awareness becomes foreground. This is the flowering of the technique.

  • Confusing the gap with thinking ABOUT the gap — the gap is pre-thought; if you’re narrating it, you’ve already left it
  • Practising only one type of gap and ignoring others — expand your repertoire

Questions From The Book

Why does Osho call these “free meditations” that nature provides?

Chapter 53, "Gaps in the Natural Flow"

Osho explains: most meditation techniques require effort — you must DO something. But nature already provides moments when the mind stops on its own. A sneeze, a shock, a moment of vertigo — these are gifts. The mind stops WITHOUT your effort. All you need to do is be alert enough to notice. It’s like finding a door already open — you don’t need to push. Just walk through. The tragedy is that millions of gaps occur in your life and you sleep through every one.

How is the gap during fright different from unconscious panic?

Chapter 53

Osho distinguishes: in fright there are two moments. The FIRST is the gap — pure awareness, no-thought, just alertness. The SECOND is the reaction — fear, panic, running. Most people immediately jump to the second and never notice the first. The technique is to catch that first instant — BEFORE the fear reaction kicks in. That first instant is pure consciousness without content. It is the same consciousness a Buddha lives in permanently.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The startle reflex creates a measurable 200–500ms window where the default mode network (self-referential thinking) goes offline. This technique uses that neurological fact as a meditation doorway. The “gap” is not mystical — it’s the brief period when the brain’s narrative machinery shuts down in response to novel input.

Neuroscience parallel: Orienting response research shows that novel or startling stimuli produce a momentary suspension of ongoing cognitive activity. The brain enters a state of heightened receptivity with minimal conceptual processing. This is exactly what meditators spend years cultivating — and the sneeze gives it to you for free, if you can catch it.

One-line essenceNature gives you free meditation moments every day — the sneeze, the shock, the hunger pang. Stop sleeping through them.
Technique 64 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: taste · eating · food · filling · sensory-absorption
When eating or drinking, become the taste of the food or drink, and be filled.

In Plain English

When eating, BECOME the taste. Not “I am tasting food” but dissolve entirely into the tasting itself. Close your eyes with the first bite. Let the flavour fill your entire being — not just the tongue but the whole body. Forget the eater and the eaten. Only tasting remains. This transforms eating from mechanical fueling into meditation. Every meal becomes a door.

How to Practice

  1. Choose one meal per day: Don’t try to make every meal a meditation at first. Pick one — ideally when you’re alone, without screens, without conversation. Breakfast or a quiet lunch works best.
  2. Prepare the space: Turn off all devices. No TV, no phone, no book, no music. Just you and the food. If possible, eat in silence. Let the table be clean and uncluttered. Treat this meal as sacred — not religiously but attentively.
  3. Before the first bite: Look at the food for 30 seconds. See its colours, textures, the steam rising. Smell it. Let anticipation build. This awakens the senses — saliva flows, the tongue prepares, the body becomes receptive.
  4. The first bite — close your eyes: Take the first bite and immediately close your eyes. This is the most important moment. With eyes closed, 100% of awareness goes to taste. Feel the texture on the tongue. Feel the temperature. Let the flavour explode into your whole mouth.
  5. BECOME the taste: This is the critical instruction. Don’t observe the taste from a distance (“oh, this is sweet”). MERGE with it. Let there be no separation between you and the flavour. You are not the one tasting — you ARE the tasting. The subject (eater) and object (food) dissolve into the verb (tasting).
  6. Let it fill the whole body: The sutra says “be filled.” Don’t keep the taste confined to the mouth. Feel it spreading through the throat, the chest, the belly. Like a wave of flavour washing through the entire body. The whole body tastes, not just the tongue.
  7. Chew slowly: Thirty to fifty chews per bite. Not as a discipline but because each chew releases new layers of flavour. What seems like one taste reveals itself as a symphony of ten tastes when chewed slowly enough.
  8. Notice the moment of swallowing: There is a distinct moment when the body decides to swallow. Notice that moment. Feel the food travelling down the throat. Feel the belly receiving it. The journey from mouth to stomach is a meditation in itself.
  9. Between bites: Pause between bites. Put the fork or spoon down. Feel the aftertaste. Let the previous bite complete its journey before beginning the next. This pause is where most of the awareness deepens.
  10. The meal ends when fullness arrives: With this level of attention, you will notice the EXACT moment when the body says “enough.” It comes much earlier than you expect — because you are actually receiving nourishment (not just calories but the full energetic content) when you eat with total presence.

  • Using it only with “special” food — apply it equally to plain rice and gourmet cuisine; awareness is the seasoning
  • Getting frustrated when the mind wanders during the meal — gently return to the taste each time
  • Confusing analysis (“I detect notes of citrus”) with merging — wine-tasting vocabulary is the mind; becoming the taste is beyond words

Why does Osho say eating can be a complete meditation?

Chapter 54, "Eating as Meditation"

Osho says: anything done with total awareness becomes meditation. Eating is particularly powerful because taste is the most primitive, most body-centred sense. It bypasses the intellect entirely. You cannot intellectualise a flavour — you can only experience it. This makes eating an ideal doorway for people who live too much in their heads. The tongue brings you back to the body instantly.

What does “be filled” mean in this context?

Chapter 54

Osho explains that “be filled” has two meanings simultaneously. First, let the taste fill your entire being — not just the tongue but every cell. Second, allow yourself to feel FULFILLED — satisfied, complete, needing nothing more. When you truly become the taste, a deep contentment arises that no amount of mechanical eating can produce. You eat less but are nourished more.

Technique 65 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: wandering · here · immediacy · catching · presence
Wherever your mind is wandering, internally or externally, at this very place, this.

In Plain English

The mind wanders — to the past, to fantasy, to worry. Normally you try to STOP it. This technique says: don’t stop it. Instead, wherever the mind has wandered TO — be fully present THERE. If the mind goes to a memory — be fully in that memory. If it goes to a fantasy — be fully in that fantasy. Full presence transforms any content. The problem was never the wandering but the half-heartedness. Be total wherever you are.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the revolution: Every other meditation technique says “bring the mind back.” This one says “GO WITH IT.” Wherever the mind goes, follow it there completely. This is radical acceptance of the mind’s nature. The mind is a wanderer — instead of fighting its nature, USE it.
  2. Sit comfortably and close your eyes: No need for any special posture. Just close your eyes and let the mind do whatever it does. Don’t try to meditate. Don’t try to be still. Let the mind wander freely.
  3. Notice where it goes: A memory arises — your childhood home, a conversation yesterday, a worry about tomorrow, a fantasy about a vacation. Simply notice where the mind has gone. Don’t judge it. Don’t pull it back.
  4. BE TOTALLY THERE: This is the practice. Wherever the mind is — be 100% there. If it’s in a memory of your mother’s kitchen, be FULLY in that kitchen. Smell the food. See the colours. Hear the sounds. Don’t be half in the memory and half watching yourself remember. Be COMPLETE in it.
  5. The transformation happens through totality: When you are 100% in any content, something miraculous occurs: the content dissolves. Total presence in a memory makes the memory transparent. You see through it to the awareness that is witnessing it. Half-hearted presence sustains content; total presence dissolves it.
  6. Apply throughout the day: This is not only a sitting practice. Walking down the street, if the mind goes to a worry — be TOTALLY in the worry. Feel it fully. Inhabit it completely. You will find that total attention to worry dissolves it far faster than fighting it.
  7. The paradox: By not fighting the mind’s wandering, you discover something astonishing: the mind stops wandering. A river that is allowed to flow freely finds its own stillness. A river that is dammed creates turbulence. Let the mind flow, and it finds peace.
  8. “At this very place, this”: The sutra’s final words are cryptic but powerful. “This” means: the divine, the truth, awareness. At whatever place the mind wanders to, THIS (truth, awareness, God) is already there. There is no place the mind can go where consciousness is absent. So wherever it goes, you can find the divine there.
  9. Practice for 30 minutes: Sit for 30 minutes daily. Let the mind wander. Follow it everywhere it goes with total presence. After 30 minutes, notice: the mind is quieter than when you started. Not because you forced it but because totality naturally produces silence.
  10. No failure is possible: This is the most forgiving technique in the entire tantra. There is no wrong way to do it. The mind wandered? Good — be there. It wandered again? Good — be there too. Every moment of wandering is an opportunity, not a failure.

  • Forgetting the “total” part — casual attention to wandering is just normal mind; TOTAL attention is the technique

Isn’t this just allowing the mind to do whatever it wants?

Chapter 54, "Wherever the Mind Wanders"

Osho clarifies the crucial difference: normally the mind wanders AND you are unconscious. It takes you for a ride. In this technique, the mind wanders BUT you remain aware. You go WITH it consciously. You are the rider, not the horse. The content is the same but the quality is different. The mind wanders in sleep (unconscious) or in meditation (conscious). Same wandering, utterly different outcomes.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This anticipates Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) by millennia. ACT’s core principle: don’t fight internal experiences; instead, bring open, curious attention to them. What you resist persists; what you fully attend to naturally transforms. This technique applies that insight to the meditation cushion.

Default Mode Network: Research shows the DMN (the “wandering mind” network) is not silenced by force but by engagement. When you fully attend to ANY content, the DMN deactivates naturally. This technique uses the mind’s own wandering as the engagement that quiets it — an elegant neurological judo.

One-line essenceStop fighting the wandering mind. Follow it with total presence wherever it goes, and discover: there is nowhere awareness cannot reach.
Technique 66 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: desire · quitting · sudden-stop · gap · freedom
When some desire comes, consider it. Then suddenly, quit it.

In Plain English

A desire arises — to eat, to speak, to move, to check your phone. Consider it fully — feel it, acknowledge it, let it build. Then SUDDENLY quit it. Drop it completely. In the instant of dropping, a gap opens. The desire was momentum; the sudden stop creates a void-moment. In that void: freedom. The technique is not suppression (which is gradual) but sudden abandonment (which is total).

How to Practice

  1. Understand the difference from suppression: Suppression says “don’t have this desire.” This technique says “HAVE the desire fully, then drop it suddenly.” The first creates conflict; the second creates freedom. You need the desire present and felt — then the sudden dropping creates the gap.
  2. Start with small desires: Don’t begin with your biggest addiction. Start tiny: the desire to scratch an itch, to shift position, to check your phone, to take another bite. These micro-desires are perfect training ground.
  3. Phase 1 — CONSIDER: When a desire arises, turn your full attention to it. Feel it in the body. Where does it live? The belly? The throat? The hands? What sensation does it create? Urgency? Pulling? Heat? Don’t resist it. Let it build to its peak intensity. CONSIDER it — feel it completely.
  4. Phase 2 — SUDDENLY QUIT: At the peak of the desire — when it is strongest and most demanding — drop it INSTANTANEOUSLY. Not gradually. Not by talking yourself out of it. Not by replacing it with something else. Simply DROP. Like releasing a held breath. Like opening a clenched fist. One moment desire; next moment nothing.
  5. Phase 3 — NOTICE THE GAP: In the instant of dropping, something extraordinary happens: a vacuum, a void, a moment of absolute freedom. The energy that was going toward the desire has nowhere to go. It pools in pure awareness. THAT moment is the meditation. That gap is the door.
  6. Practice with movement: Start walking across the room with intention. Build momentum. Then STOP. Freeze mid-stride. The body stops, and in the sudden stop, the mind stops too. Feel the gap between the momentum and the stillness.
  7. Practice with speech: You want to say something. Feel the words forming, the desire to speak building. Then — close your mouth. Don’t say it. Not because it’s wrong to say it, but for the experiment. Feel the gap when the impulse to speak is suddenly abandoned.
  8. Practice with reaching: Reach for your phone, your coffee, a book. Let the hand extend. Then STOP before contact. Pull the hand back. Feel the sudden emptiness where the grasping was. That emptiness is awareness.
  9. Evening practice (10 desires): In the evening, sit quietly and recall 10 desires that arose during the day. With each one, mentally re-create the desire, feel it build, then suddenly drop it. Feel the gap each time. Ten gaps per evening, consciously created.
  10. The mastery: Over weeks, you develop the ability to drop ANY desire at ANY moment. Not from suppression but from the understanding that desire is just energy — and when it is dropped, that energy becomes available for awareness. This is not anti-life; it is profoundly pro-freedom.

  • Dropping gradually instead of suddenly — the power is in the ABRUPTNESS; gradual dropping is just losing interest

  • Applying this to survival needs inappropriately — don’t drop the desire for water when dehydrated; use non-essential desires
  • Making it a moral exercise (“I should not desire”) — this is consciousness work, not ethics

Questions From The Book

How is suddenly quitting different from will-power?

Chapter 55, "Consider and Quit"

Osho is emphatic: this is NOT will-power. Will-power fights desire with a counter-force — creating tension, conflict, and eventual explosion. Sudden quitting is more like relaxing a clenched fist. You don’t fight the fist open; you simply stop clenching. The desire was being held by attention; when attention is suddenly withdrawn, the desire simply collapses — not because of force but because of the absence of fuel. No fight, no conflict — just sudden cessation.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: This maps to the neuroscience of “response inhibition” — the prefrontal cortex’s ability to halt a motor or cognitive programme already in progress. But the technique adds a contemplative dimension: the gap created by inhibition is not just a pause but a window into non-compulsive awareness. It trains the brain that desire is not destiny.

Addiction research parallel: “Urge surfing” in addiction therapy teaches people to feel cravings without acting on them. This technique is similar but more radical: it says feel the craving fully, then DROP it suddenly. The resulting neurological reset — the sudden release of dopaminergic anticipation — creates a distinct moment of clarity that weakens the habit loop.

Technique 67 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: single-object · absence · realization · stripping · awareness
Feel an object before you. Feel the absence of all other objects but this one. Then, leaving aside the object-feeling and the absence-feeling, realize.

In Plain English

Look at one object — a cup, a stone, a candle. Feel it fully. Then feel the ABSENCE of everything else — only this object exists. The rest of the world is gone. Then drop even this object. Now neither the object nor other objects exist. What remains? Pure awareness — the “realize” the sutra speaks of. Three steps: object → absence of others → absence of everything → awareness.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your object: Select any single object. It need not be special — a cup, a stone, a pen, a flower, a candle. Place it before you at arm’s length. The simpler and more ordinary the object, the better — a beautiful object distracts with its beauty.
  2. Step 1 — Feel the object (5 min): Give your entire attention to this object. See its shape, colour, texture, weight (imagined from sight). Feel its presence. Not just visually but with your whole being — feel it occupying space, feel its thereness. Let it become the most real thing in existence.
  3. Step 2 — Feel the absence of everything else (5 min): Now feel that everything else has disappeared. The room is gone. The city is gone. The world is gone. Other people — gone. Your body (except the seeing) — gone. Only THIS object and your awareness of it remain. The universe has contracted to just this cup and the consciousness beholding it.
  4. Step 3 — The first stripping: Notice what happens when everything else is absent. The object becomes extraordinarily vivid, almost numinous. Without context, without comparison, without the background of other objects, this single thing becomes infinite in its detail. A cup becomes the entire universe.
  5. Step 4 — Leave aside even the object (5 min): Now the radical step. Drop the object too. It disappears. Not into other things (since they are already absent) but into nothingness. The universe is now completely empty. No objects exist anywhere. Nothing to perceive.
  6. Step 5 — Leave aside the absence-feeling: Now even the FEELING of absence must go. Don’t hold onto emptiness. Don’t cling to nothingness as an experience. Let even the absence disappear. What is left when not even nothing remains?
  7. Step 6 — REALIZE: What remains is pure awareness itself — awareness without an object. Not awareness OF something but awareness itself, luminous, self-knowing, without content. This is what you are. This is what the sutra points to with the single word “realize.”
  8. The entire sequence takes 20 minutes: 5 minutes with the object, 5 minutes with absence, 5 minutes dropping both, 5 minutes resting in what remains. Do this once daily in a quiet space.
  9. Variation with eyes closed: After doing it visually several times, try it with eyes closed. Feel an imagined object before you. Then strip away everything as before. The inner version can be even more powerful because there are fewer sensory distractions.
  10. The philosophical depth: This technique systematically deconstructs the subject-object structure of consciousness. Normally awareness is always awareness OF something. Remove all objects and discover: awareness does not need objects to exist. It is self-luminous.

Technique 68 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: beyond-purity · non-judgement · tantra · acceptance · freedom
The purity of other teachings is an impurity to us. In reality, know nothing as pure or impure.

In Plain English

Drop ALL categories of pure/impure, good/bad, sacred/profane. Tantra says: these categories are the chains. Nothing is impure and nothing is pure — these are mental overlays on reality. When you drop judging (this food is “bad,” this act is “impure”), a tremendous freedom arises. The energy that was consumed by constant moral categorisation becomes available for awareness.

How to Practice

  1. Understand the context: This is the most radical sutra in the tantra. Every other spiritual tradition divides the world into sacred/profane, pure/impure, allowed/forbidden. Tantra says: THAT division is the real impurity. It splits reality and splits you. This technique heals that split.
  2. One-day experiment: Choose one full day. From morning to night, make ZERO moral judgments. Not about food (“this is healthy/unhealthy”). Not about people (“she is good/he is bad”). Not about yourself (“I was wrong/right”). Not about actions (“this is proper/improper”). Simply observe without the overlays of judgement.
  3. Notice the judge: You will be astonished how CONSTANT the judging is. The mind categorises everything: this is acceptable, that is not. This is clean, that is dirty. This person is worthy, that one is not. The judge runs 24/7 and you have never noticed because it feels like “reality.”
  4. Catch each judgement as it arises: When the mind says “impure” or “wrong” or “bad” — catch it. Don’t fight it. Just notice: “Ah, the judge is speaking again.” And let the judgement go. Return to raw perception without the overlay.
  5. See what remains without judgement: When you look at something “dirty” without the label “dirty” — what do you actually see? Colour, texture, shape. That’s all. The “dirtiness” was added by the mind. Remove it, and reality is simply what it is — neither pure nor impure.
  6. Notice the energy freed: Judging consumes enormous mental energy. Cataloguing, comparing, condemning, approving — it never stops. When it stops (even for an hour), you will feel a surge of free energy, lightness, spaciousness. This is the freedom tantra promises.
  7. Apply to the body: Notice how you judge your own body — this part is beautiful, that part is ugly. This function is “dignified,” that function is “shameful.” Drop all of it. The body is simply the body. No part is impure.
  8. Apply to emotions: Stop labelling emotions as positive or negative. Anger arises — it is not “bad.” Sadness arises — it is not “wrong.” Joy arises — it is not “better.” They are all simply energies moving through you. Without judgement, they flow freely and resolve naturally.
  9. The paradox of morality: This technique does NOT make you immoral. Osho is clear: when judgement drops, a natural morality arises that is far more authentic than the imposed kind. Without the judge, compassion flows naturally because you are no longer separated from others by categories of better/worse.
  10. Extend to one week: After your one-day experiment, extend to a full week. Notice how profoundly your relationship to reality changes when you stop dividing it into acceptable and unacceptable. Reality becomes seamless, whole, sacred in its totality.

Technique 69 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: eyes · touch · feather · subtle · heart-opening
Touch eyeballs as feathers, subtlety within. The heart opens.

In Plain English

Close your eyes. Place your palms very gently over your closed eyelids — as light as feathers. Don’t press at all. Just the softest possible contact. Feel the eyeballs receiving this gentleness. The eyes hold tremendous tension (from constant looking, focusing, judging). When touched with absolute gentleness, the eyes relax — and with them the entire nervous system softens. The heart opens because the eyes and heart are connected through the vagus nerve.

How to Practice

  1. Prepare your hands: Rub your palms together vigorously for 10–15 seconds until they feel warm and alive with energy. The friction generates both heat and a subtle electromagnetic charge. Your palms become healing instruments.
  2. Close your eyes: Sit comfortably or lie down. Close your eyes and let them settle for a moment. Don’t force them shut — let the eyelids drop naturally, like curtains falling.
  3. Place palms over eyes: Cup your warm palms gently over your closed eyes. The heels of the palms rest on the cheekbones. The fingers rest on the forehead. The cups of the palms hover over the eyeballs. Almost no pressure — just the lightest possible contact.
  4. The key instruction — FEATHER touch: Osho’s specific word is “feathers.” Imagine your palms ARE feathers. The lightest substance. Barely there. If you are pressing even slightly, you are pressing too much. Less and less pressure until it is almost nothing — just the warmth and the presence of the hands.
  5. Feel the eyes receiving: Notice what happens to your eyeballs under this gentleness. They begin to relax. The muscles behind the eyes (which are constantly tense from focusing) begin to soften. The eyes may feel like they are floating, sinking backward into the skull, becoming liquid.
  6. Notice the subtlety within: As the eyes relax, subtler perceptions arise. You may see colours (violet, gold, blue). You may feel a tingling between the eyebrows. You may feel the eyes “opening inward” rather than outward. This is the “subtlety within” the sutra mentions.
  7. The heart connection: After 3–5 minutes of palming, bring your attention to the heart. You will notice: the heart has softened too. There may be warmth in the chest, a sense of openness or tenderness. This is not imagination — the vagus nerve physically connects the eyes and heart. When the eyes relax, the signal travels directly to the heart.
  8. Stay for 5–10 minutes: Keep the palms over the eyes for the full duration. The relaxation deepens progressively. What begins as eye-relaxation becomes whole-body softening. The nervous system shifts from sympathetic (fight-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-digest-love).
  9. Remove hands slowly: When ready, remove your palms very slowly. Keep eyes closed for another minute. Notice how different the inner space feels — softer, darker, more spacious. When you finally open your eyes, the world may look clearer, more vivid, as if a film has been cleaned away.
  10. Practice before sleep: This technique is particularly powerful at bedtime. The eye-relaxation cascades into whole-body relaxation, and sleep comes quickly and deeply. Many practitioners report this as the single most effective technique for insomnia.

Technique 70 of 112
Category: Senses · Tags: fresh-seeing · beginner-mind · wonder · ordinary · beauty
See as if for the first time a beauteous person or an ordinary object.

In Plain English

Look at anything — your partner, a cup, a tree, your own hand — as if you have NEVER seen it before. Drop all familiarity, all memory, all labels. See it with the freshness of a child’s first encounter. When you look at your spouse as if for the first time, love rushes back. When you look at a leaf as if for the first time, wonder arises. Familiarity is the death of perception. This technique kills familiarity and resurrects wonder.

How to Practice

  1. Choose something familiar: The more familiar the better. Your own hand. Your coffee cup. The view from your window. Your partner’s face. The wall of your room. Choose something so familiar that you have stopped SEEING it entirely — it has become invisible through repetition.
  2. Forget its name: The first step is to drop the label. Don’t call it a “hand” or a “cup” or a “tree.” Names create the illusion of knowing. You say “tree” and think you know what you’re looking at. You don’t. You know a word. Drop the word and see the THING.
  3. Forget its function: Don’t think about what it’s FOR. A cup is not “something to drink from” — that’s function, not reality. A hand is not “something to grasp with.” Drop all utility. See it as a being, not a tool.
  4. Forget your history with it: Drop all memories associated with this object or person. Your partner is not “the person I married in 2015” or “the person who annoyed me yesterday.” All history creates a screen between you and the reality. See them fresh, NOW, without the weight of the past.
  5. See with a child’s eyes: Imagine you are a baby seeing this for the very first time. You have no concepts, no language, no categories. Just pure visual (and tactile, and olfactory) perception. Raw sensation without interpretation. What do you ACTUALLY see? Colours. Shapes. Textures. Light and shadow. Movement or stillness. That’s all. And it is miraculous.
  6. Stay with the seeing for 5 minutes: Don’t glance and move on. STAY with the object. Keep seeing it fresh. The mind will try to reassert labels (“it’s just a cup”). Each time, drop the label again and return to raw seeing. Five minutes of truly fresh seeing can transform your relationship with reality.
  7. Apply to your partner or loved ones: This is where the technique becomes transformative for relationships. Look at your partner — right now — as if you have never seen them. See their face as a landscape you are exploring for the first time. The curve of an eyebrow. The texture of skin. The mystery of eyes looking back at you. Love returns instantly when familiarity dies.
  8. Apply to your own body: Look at your hand as if seeing a hand for the first time ever. Notice: five fingers, each different, each articulated, each capable of independent movement. Skin that stretches and folds. Nails growing from within. Veins carrying life beneath the surface. Your hand is an absolute miracle — you just stopped noticing.
  9. Walk through your home as a stranger: Walk through your living space as if you have never been here before. A visitor from another planet seeing human habitation for the first time. Every object becomes fascinating. The familiar becomes extraordinary. THIS is how a child experiences the world — and why children are so full of wonder.
  10. Make it daily: Choose one thing each day to see freshly. Rotate: Monday, your hand. Tuesday, the sky. Wednesday, your partner’s face. Thursday, your food. Friday, a tree. Over time, the fresh-seeing becomes a permanent quality — the eyes lose their deadness and reality regains its magic.
