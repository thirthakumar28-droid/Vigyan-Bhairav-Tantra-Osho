The Techniques for Sleep & Dream

Five techniques that use the sleep transition, rhythmic movement, intense sensation, and bodily dissolution as doorways to conscious sleep. The threshold between waking and sleeping is a portal — cross it with awareness intact and you discover the immortal witness.

5 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These five techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The sleep and dream sutras form the tenth major category — using the sleep transition, death-like stillness, rhythmic movement, and dissolution to maintain awareness through the threshold where most people lose consciousness.

These techniques approach awakening through the nightly miracle of sleep. Every night you cross from waking to sleeping — from somebody to nobody, from self to no-self. Usually this happens unconsciously. These techniques teach you to cross that threshold WITH awareness. When you can sleep consciously, death holds no mystery — death is simply the final sleep.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These sleep techniques work at the TRANSITION point — the hypnagogic threshold. The moment between waking and sleeping is the most vulnerable, most open, most magical moment of the day. These techniques teach you to STAY AWARE through it.

New to sleep meditation?

Start with Technique 1 — lie down as dead or Technique 5 — body dissolving into elements. Both are done lying in bed, require no special preparation, and produce tangible results quickly. Practise every night for 21 nights.

Technique 103 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: death-posture · stillness · shavasana
Lie down as dead. Enraged in wrath, stay so. Or stare without moving an eyelash. Or suck something and become the sucking.

In Plain English

Lie like a corpse — no movement. Feel as if dead. When the body is totally still and “dead,” consciousness separates from it. You discover you are not the body. The sutra offers four variations — death posture, frozen rage, unblinking stare, or total absorption in sucking — all creating such intense stillness or single-pointed focus that the self separates from the body and witnesses it from beyond.

How to Practice

  1. Prepare for bed as normal: Complete your evening routine. Brush teeth, dim lights, put away screens. When you lie down in bed, this is your practice. No special setup required — the technique IS the transition from waking to sleep.
  2. Lie flat on your back: Arms at your sides, palms up or down — whichever is natural. Legs slightly apart. This is shavasana, the corpse pose. Feel the bed supporting your full weight. Let gravity take everything.
  3. Decide to be dead: This is the crucial inner shift. You are not “relaxing” — you are DEAD. A dead body cannot move a finger, cannot scratch an itch, cannot adjust position. Make a firm inner commitment: “I will not move. I am dead.” This commitment must be total.
  4. Surrender all muscular control: Starting from the feet, consciously release every muscle group. Feet — dead. Calves — dead. Thighs — dead. Pelvis, belly, chest — dead. Arms, hands, fingers — dead. Neck, jaw, face, eyes — dead. Even the tongue — let it fall back, lifeless. Feel the entire body as a corpse lying on the bed.
  5. Do not respond to the body: The body will test you. An itch will arise. An urge to swallow. A desire to adjust position. A restless leg. DO NOT RESPOND. A dead body does not scratch. Let every sensation arise, peak, and pass WITHOUT ANY RESPONSE. This is the death posture — total non-responsiveness.
  6. Feel the separation: After 10–15 minutes of absolute stillness, something remarkable happens: consciousness begins to SEPARATE from the body. You feel aware, alert, present — but the body feels distant, foreign, not-you. You are witnessing the body rather than being it. This is the discovery: you are not the body.
  7. Maintain awareness through the sleep transition: As the body sinks deeper into its death-stillness, sleep will approach. The hypnagogic imagery will begin — flashes of light, random images, a feeling of floating. STAY AWARE through this transition. Don’t engage with the imagery, don’t follow it — just witness.
  8. Enter sleep consciously: If you can maintain awareness through the hypnagogic transition, you will enter sleep KNOWING you are entering sleep. The body sleeps; you remain. This is yoga nidra — conscious sleep. Even if you only achieve this for moments before unconsciousness takes over, those moments are transformative.
  9. The morning insight: Upon waking, notice: something was aware even during sleep. You may not remember it clearly, but there is a sense of continuity — of something that was present through the night even when “you” were gone. That something is the witness — what you truly are.
  10. Practise every night: Make this your nightly ritual. Over 21 days, the periods of conscious sleep will extend. Some nights you will succeed beautifully; other nights sleep will take you immediately. Both are fine. The practice accumulates. Eventually, conscious sleep becomes your natural state.

Questions From The Book

Why does the sutra combine death posture with rage, staring, and sucking?

Chapter 66, "Lie Down As Dead"

Osho explains: all four variations share one principle — TOTALITY. Lie as dead TOTALLY. Be in rage TOTALLY (without acting on it). Stare TOTALLY without blinking. Suck TOTALLY until you become the sucking. The common thread is total absorption that leaves no energy for the ego. When absorption is total, the self drops. The death posture is the most accessible version for sleep practice — but any total absorption creates the same gap.

Is this the same as yoga nidra?

Chapter 66

Osho says: yoga nidra as taught today is a systematized version of this ancient sutra. The principle is identical — maintain awareness while the body enters sleep. Traditional yoga nidra uses body scanning and rotation of consciousness; this sutra uses the more direct method of simply being dead. Both arrive at the same place: conscious sleep, where the witness remains while the body-mind complex rests.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Sleep paralysis research shows that the body has a natural mechanism for disconnecting motor control during sleep (atonia). This technique deliberately invokes that mechanism through voluntary stillness. By refusing to move, you trigger the body’s sleep-entry protocols while maintaining cortical alertness. The result is a dissociation between body-state (sleeping) and awareness-state (alert) — proving experientially that consciousness is not dependent on bodily activity.

Lucid dreaming research: The Wake-Initiated Lucid Dream (WILD) technique in lucid dreaming research uses exactly this method: maintain awareness while the body falls asleep. Studies show that practitioners can reliably enter REM sleep with metacognitive awareness intact. This 5,000-year-old sutra is the original WILD technique.

One-line essenceLie as dead. Do not move. When the body is truly still, consciousness separates — and you discover you were never the body at all.
Technique 104 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: sleep-entry · balance · centering
Without support for feet or hands, sit only on the buttocks. Suddenly, the centering.

In Plain English

Use the balance-centering as a pre-sleep technique — the mind-gap it creates transitions smoothly into conscious sleep. When you sit balanced on buttocks alone with nothing supporting hands or feet, the mind becomes completely absorbed in maintaining equilibrium. The moment balance is found, mind STOPS. That gap — that sudden centering — is identical to the gap at sleep-entry. Use it as a doorway.

How to Practice

  1. Practise in bed before lying down: Sit on your mattress or on the floor beside your bed. This is a pre-sleep technique — do it in the last minutes before sleep. Dim the lights. The environment should already say “sleep.”
  2. Lift feet and hands: Lean back slightly and lift both feet off the surface. Arms extended or held up — touching nothing. You are balanced entirely on your sit-bones. The body must find equilibrium without any external support.
  3. Let the mind absorb into balance: As the body wobbles and searches for stability, notice: the mind has become completely silent. All attention is consumed by the physical challenge. There is no room for thoughts, worries, plans for tomorrow. The balance demand has emptied the mind.
  4. Find the still point: When balance is achieved — even for a few seconds — the mind enters a GAP. No wobbling, no thinking, no effort. Just stillness. This gap is neurologically identical to the hypnagogic moment — it is a doorway between states.
  5. Repeat 5–7 times: Hold balance for 10–30 seconds, then release. Rest. Repeat. Each repetition deepens the mind-gap. After 5–7 rounds, the mind is thoroughly emptied and centred — primed for conscious sleep entry.
  6. Immediately lie down: After the final balance hold, lie down immediately in corpse pose. The mind is empty, centred, quiet. The body is tired from the core engagement. This combination — empty mind + tired body — is IDEAL for conscious sleep entry.
  7. Carry the gap into sleep: As you lie down, maintain the QUALITY of that balance-gap. The same empty, centred awareness. Don’t think. Don’t plan. Just rest in the gap. Let sleep come TO you while you remain in that centred stillness.
  8. The centering persists: Even if you fall asleep quickly, the centering you created through balance will persist into sleep. Many practitioners report that their sleep is deeper, more restful, and more aware after this pre-sleep centering. The quality of sleep changes.
  9. Morning variation: This technique can also be used upon waking — sit up immediately and balance before the mind fills with the day’s agenda. The morning mind-gap is equally valuable. Bookend your sleep with centering.
  10. Progressive difficulty: As your core strengthens, make the balance harder — close eyes, extend legs higher, hold longer. The harder the balance, the deeper the mind-gap. Eventually you can maintain the gap even after releasing the posture — the centered awareness becomes your ground state.

  • Thinking about the technique instead of simply doing it — analysis activates the mind; just balance and feel
  • Doing it only occasionally rather than nightly — the cumulative effect over 21 days is what transforms sleep

How does physical balance relate to sleep?

Chapter 60, "Sudden Centering"

Osho explains: the mind must be empty for sleep to come. Normally the mind chatters on, keeping you awake. Balance creates forced emptiness — the mind HAS to be silent during balance because all attention is consumed by the physical demand. Once emptied through balance, the mind doesn’t fill up again easily. That emptiness is the same emptiness through which sleep arrives. You have simply created the CONDITION for sleep rather than waiting for it to happen accidentally.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Sleep onset requires deactivation of the default mode network (the brain’s “thinking about yourself” system). Balance practice forces attention into the vestibular-cerebellar system, effectively shutting down DMN activity. This is why people who exercise report better sleep — physical engagement silences the rumination network. This technique concentrates that effect into 5 minutes of precise, targeted neurological resetting.

Sleep hygiene parallel: Modern sleep science recommends “cognitive shuffling” and body-scan relaxation before bed. Both work by occupying the thinking mind with non-threatening tasks. Balance practice achieves the same result more powerfully — it doesn’t just distract the mind, it EMPTIES it. The mind-gap produced by balance is deeper than anything achievable through mental tricks alone.

Technique 105 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: rhythm · sleep · rocking
In a moving vehicle, by rhythmically swaying, experience.

In Plain English

Use rhythmic movement (rocking, swaying) to induce the hypnagogic state consciously. Like being rocked to sleep as a baby but maintaining awareness through the transition. The repetitive rhythm lulls the thinking mind while the rhythmic body-sense keeps a thread of awareness alive. You enter the sleep-state through the body’s ancient memory of being rocked.

How to Practice

  1. Choose your rhythm: Sit cross-legged on your bed or cushion before sleep. Begin to sway gently — side to side, or forward and back. Find a rhythm that feels natural, hypnotic, like being rocked in a cradle. Not too fast (stimulating), not too slow (no momentum). About the speed of a slow pendulum.
  2. Close your eyes: Let the swaying happen with eyes closed. The visual world disappears; only the rhythm remains. Feel the body as a pendulum — weightless, effortless, moving of its own accord. Let the rhythm become automatic — you are not doing it; it is happening.
  3. Let the rhythm take over: After 3–5 minutes, the swaying should feel autonomous — as if the body is being rocked by an invisible hand. Your job is to NOT interfere. Don’t direct it, don’t control it, don’t even participate consciously. Just let it happen while you watch.
  4. Feel the lullaby effect: The rhythmic movement activates the same neurological pathways that put infants to sleep. You will feel drowsy, dreamy, soft. The edges of consciousness begin to blur. Thoughts slow down. The boundary between waking and sleeping thins. This is the hypnagogic state — the portal.
  5. Maintain the thread of awareness: As drowsiness deepens, keep ONE thread of awareness alive — the felt sense of the swaying itself. You don’t need to think, analyse, or remain fully alert. Just the FEELING of movement. This thread carries you through the transition consciously.
  6. Let the swaying diminish naturally: As sleep approaches, the swaying will naturally slow, diminish, and eventually stop. Let this happen organically — don’t force it to continue and don’t force it to stop. When the body wants to be still, let it be still. When it stops, you may be at the exact threshold.
  7. Lie down in the stillness: When the swaying has stopped and profound drowsiness fills you, gently lie down. The transition from rhythmic movement to stillness is like a train slowing into a station. The momentum of awareness carries forward even as the body becomes still.
  8. Enter sleep with the rhythm’s echo: Even after lying still, you can feel the echo of the swaying — a ghost-rhythm in the body. This echo is your awareness-thread. Follow it into sleep. It becomes the bridge between waking awareness and sleep awareness.
  9. Vehicle variation: The original sutra mentions “a moving vehicle” — bus, train, car (as passenger). If you travel, use the vehicle’s motion as your rocking. Close your eyes, feel the sway and vibration, and let it carry you into the hypnagogic state. Many people already fall asleep in vehicles — now do it CONSCIOUSLY.
  10. Rocking chair variation: A rocking chair is perhaps the ideal modern implementation of this technique. The rhythmic movement is effortless, sustainable, and perfectly calibrated for inducing conscious drowsiness. Rock yourself toward sleep, then transfer to bed at the threshold.

  • Expecting dramatic experiences — this technique works subtly; its gift is effortless, natural, conscious sleep entry

Why does rhythm induce altered states?

Chapter 67, "Rhythmic Experience"

Osho explains: the thinking mind operates through irregularity — one thought leads unpredictably to another. Rhythm introduces REGULARITY, which the thinking mind cannot process or engage with (there is nothing new to think about). So the thinking mind withdraws, bored, and what remains is pure awareness without thought. This is why drums, chanting, rocking, and repetitive movement have been used in every culture to induce trance states. Rhythm bypasses the intellect entirely.

The experience cannot be named in advance — it is the experience of pure being without the filter of the thinking mind. When rhythm has silenced thought, what remains is raw existence — unmediated, uninterpreted, direct. THAT is “the experience.” It cannot be described because description requires the thinking mind, which has been bypassed.

The body has an ancient, pre-verbal response to rhythmic motion that this technique deliberately exploits.

This technique returns the adult to that primal state of being rocked by existence itself. The conscious addition is simply maintaining awareness through the transition that infants make unconsciously.

One-line essenceLet rhythm rock you toward sleep as it did when you were an infant — but this time, stay awake inside the rocking. Cross the threshold with eyes open on the inside.
Technique 106 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: sensation · focus · sleep-like-depth
Pierce some point of your body with a pin, and gently enter the piercing.

In Plain English

Focus on a strong body sensation (not self-harm — use an ice cube or strong pressure) to create such intense single-pointed focus that the mind drops into a state resembling sleep with maintained awareness. The sensation becomes so absorbing that all other mental activity ceases — and in that cessation, you enter a depth normally only available in sleep, but with full consciousness.

How to Practice

  1. Important note — modern adaptation: This technique does NOT require actual piercing or self-harm. Use intense but safe sensations: an ice cube held against the skin, strong finger-pressure on an acupressure point, a clothespin on the earlobe, or simply pressing a fingernail into the palm. The principle is INTENSE FOCUSED SENSATION, not pain or damage.
  2. Choose your sensation source: An ice cube on the inner wrist is ideal — intensely cold, impossible to ignore, completely safe. Alternatively: strong thumb pressure on the point between thumb and forefinger (the “hegu” acupressure point), or pressing a fingernail firmly into the opposite palm.
  3. Create the sensation: Apply the ice cube or pressure. The sensation should be strong enough to DEMAND attention — impossible to ignore — but not so extreme that it triggers panic or the fight-or-flight response. You want intensity without distress. Alert discomfort, not suffering.
  4. Enter the sensation: Here is the key instruction: “gently ENTER the piercing.” Don’t resist the sensation. Don’t endure it. ENTER it. Move your awareness INTO the point of sensation. Feel it from the inside. Become the sensation rather than the one experiencing it. Dissolve into the feeling.
  5. Let it absorb you completely: As you enter the sensation more and more deeply, all other mental activity stops. Thoughts cannot compete with this intense, immediate, present-moment experience. The mind becomes single-pointed — focused entirely on this one spot, this one sensation. Everything else falls away.
  6. Notice the depth: After 5–10 minutes of total absorption in the sensation, something shifts. The quality of consciousness changes — it becomes deeper, quieter, more interior. This depth is the same depth you normally only experience in deep sleep — but here you are aware. The sensation has pulled you DOWN, below the surface mind, into the silent depths.
  7. Release the sensation: After reaching this depth, gently remove the ice or release the pressure. The sensation fades — but the DEPTH remains. You are now in a sleep-like depth of consciousness without being asleep. Rest here. This is the space between waking and sleeping, accessed through sensation rather than through the sleep transition.
  8. Use as pre-sleep practice: This technique is especially powerful when done in bed just before sleep. The depth of consciousness it produces slides naturally into sleep — but because you ARRIVED there consciously (through the sensation), you maintain awareness through the transition. Conscious sleep follows easily from this depth.
  9. The principle of absorption: ANY total absorption produces sleep-like depth with maintained awareness. Sensation is simply one of the most reliable doorways because the body CANNOT ignore intense sensation. Unlike thought-based techniques (which the mind can evade), sensation-based techniques bypass all mental resistance. The body is the doorway.
  10. Safety and gentleness: The sutra says “gently enter.” Gentleness is key. This is not an endurance test or pain practice. It is a SENSITIVITY practice — using intensity to develop such exquisite attention that consciousness penetrates to depths normally hidden. Be gentle with yourself. The goal is depth, not damage.

Why does Shiva suggest piercing? Isn’t that violent?

Chapter 67, "Enter The Piercing"

Osho explains: in Shiva’s time, this was literal — a thin needle through a fold of skin, like ear piercing. The pain was brief and manageable. But the PRINCIPLE is what matters: intense sensation creates absolute present-moment focus. Modern practitioners can achieve the same result with ice, pressure, or any strong sensation. When the insular cortex is maximally activated, the default mode network (responsible for mind-wandering and self-referential thought) is suppressed. This creates a state of pure presence without narrative — exactly the state needed for conscious sleep entry. The body becomes the doorway to transcendence through the simple mechanism of attentional competition.

Cold exposure research: Studies on cold water immersion show that intense cold produces immediate mindfulness — subjects report complete cessation of rumination and a feeling of profound presence. Ice-cube meditation (a modern adaptation of this technique) has been used successfully in trauma therapy to ground dissociated patients in present-moment body awareness. The principle is ancient; the science confirms it.

Technique 107 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: dissolution · cosmic · sleep · letting-go
Think of your body as being made of cosmic essences and leave it to be consumed.

In Plain English

As you fall asleep, feel the body dissolving back into its elements — earth, water, fire, air, space. Let it be consumed by the cosmos. In that dissolving, consciousness remains while the body “dies” into sleep. The body returns to the universe each night; only awareness persists. This is a nightly rehearsal for the ultimate dissolution — death — done gently, naturally, beautifully.

How to Practice

  1. Lie in bed, ready for sleep: This is an in-bed, eyes-closed, falling-asleep technique. All preparation is done. Lights off. Body in corpse pose or your natural sleep position. This is the last thing you do before sleep takes you.
  2. Feel the body as elements: Begin by recognising what the body is made of. The EARTH element: bones, muscles, flesh — everything solid. The WATER element: blood, saliva, lymph — everything fluid. The FIRE element: body heat, digestion, metabolism — everything warm. The AIR element: breath, gases, the lightness in the lungs. The SPACE element: the hollowness within — the spaces inside chest, belly, skull.
  3. Begin with earth dissolving: Feel the solid parts of the body growing heavy, sinking into the mattress, merging with the earth below. The bones dissolve downward. The muscles melt. The flesh returns to earth. Feel the solidity leaving your body — you become less solid, more transparent. The earth element is being consumed by the cosmos.
  4. Water dissolving: Now feel the fluid element evaporating, being absorbed. The blood becomes thinner, lighter. Saliva dries. The body’s water returns to the cosmic ocean. You feel drier, lighter, less embodied. Another layer of physicality dissolves.
  5. Fire dissolving: The warmth begins to leave the body. Not cold — but the fire-element returns to the cosmic fire, the sun. The body’s heat radiates outward, returning to the universe. Digestion stops. Metabolism quiets. The inner flame dims to an ember, then returns to its source.
  6. Air dissolving: The breath becomes thinner, lighter, barely perceptible. The air in the lungs returns to the atmosphere. Breathing becomes so subtle it almost stops. The air element leaves — you are no longer breathing; the cosmos breathes through what remains of you.
  7. Space dissolving: Finally, even the space within the body dissolves into the infinite space of the cosmos. The boundary between inner space and outer space disappears. There is no longer a “body” with space inside it — there is just space. Infinite, unbounded, cosmic space. And awareness floating in it.
  8. What remains: When all five elements have been consumed — when the body has been completely returned to the cosmos — what remains? Only consciousness. Pure awareness without a body. This is what persists through sleep. This is what persists through death. This is what you ARE. Let this bodiless awareness enter sleep. Let it rest in the cosmic space it has always been part of.
  9. Fall asleep in dissolution: Most people will fall asleep during this process — often during the earth or water phase. This is perfect. The dissolution continues unconsciously, and the quality of sleep is profoundly different — deeper, more restful, more cosmic. You wake feeling like you dissolved and were reassembled fresh.
  10. Morning reassembly: Upon waking, notice: the elements reassemble. Space condenses into body-space. Air fills the lungs with a deep breath. Fire returns as warmth floods the body. Water returns as you feel fluids moving. Earth returns as solidity and weight. You are reconstructed from the cosmos each morning. Every day is a rebirth.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating it as a rigid sequence rather than a natural letting-go — the elements can dissolve in any order; follow what feels true

Questions From The Book

Why does the sutra say “leave it to be consumed”?

Chapter 68, "Cosmic Essences"

Osho explains: “leave it” means you are NOT the body. You can leave it because it is not you — it is something you HAVE, like clothing. And “consumed” means returned to source — the elements don’t disappear; they return home. Earth to earth, water to water, fire to fire, air to air, space to space. Nothing is destroyed — everything simply returns to where it came from. The only thing that has no source to return to is consciousness itself — because it never came FROM anywhere. It simply IS. That is the discovery.

Is this a death meditation?

Chapter 68

Osho says: yes and no. It is a rehearsal for death — done every night, gently, safely, reversibly. By dissolving the body into elements each night and discovering that awareness persists, you prove to yourself experientially that death is not the end. The body dies (elements return to source), but YOU do not die because you are not the body. Every night becomes a small death, and every morning a small birth. When the final death comes, it will feel familiar — just another dissolution, just another night.
