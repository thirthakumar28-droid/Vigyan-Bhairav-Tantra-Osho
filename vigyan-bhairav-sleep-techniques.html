Technique 9 of 112
Category: Sleep & Dream · Tags: death-posture · stillness · shavasana
Lie down as dead. Enraged in wrath, stay so. Or stare without moving an eyelash. Or suck something and become the sucking.

In Plain English

Lie like a corpse — no movement. Feel as if dead. When the body is totally still and “dead,” consciousness separates from it. You discover you are not the body. The sutra offers four variations — death posture, frozen rage, unblinking stare, or total absorption in sucking — all creating such intense stillness or single-pointed focus that the self separates from the body and witnesses it from beyond.

How to Practice

  1. Prepare for bed as normal: Complete your evening routine. Brush teeth, dim lights, put away screens. When you lie down in bed, this is your practice. No special setup required — the technique IS the transition from waking to sleep.
  2. Lie flat on your back: Arms at your sides, palms up or down — whichever is natural. Legs slightly apart. This is shavasana, the corpse pose. Feel the bed supporting your full weight. Let gravity take everything.
  3. Decide to be dead: This is the crucial inner shift. You are not “relaxing” — you are DEAD. A dead body cannot move a finger, cannot scratch an itch, cannot adjust position. Make a firm inner commitment: “I will not move. I am dead.” This commitment must be total.
  4. Surrender all muscular control: Starting from the feet, consciously release every muscle group. Feet — dead. Calves — dead. Thighs — dead. Pelvis, belly, chest — dead. Arms, hands, fingers — dead. Neck, jaw, face, eyes — dead. Even the tongue — let it fall back, lifeless. Feel the entire body as a corpse lying on the bed.
  5. Do not respond to the body: The body will test you. An itch will arise. An urge to swallow. A desire to adjust position. A restless leg. DO NOT RESPOND. A dead body does not scratch. Let every sensation arise, peak, and pass WITHOUT ANY RESPONSE. This is the death posture — total non-responsiveness.
  6. Feel the separation: After 10–15 minutes of absolute stillness, something remarkable happens: consciousness begins to SEPARATE from the body. You feel aware, alert, present — but the body feels distant, foreign, not-you. You are witnessing the body rather than being it. This is the discovery: you are not the body.
  7. Maintain awareness through the sleep transition: As the body sinks deeper into its death-stillness, sleep will approach. The hypnagogic imagery will begin — flashes of light, random images, a feeling of floating. STAY AWARE through this transition. Don’t engage with the imagery, don’t follow it — just witness.
  8. Enter sleep consciously: If you can maintain awareness through the hypnagogic transition, you will enter sleep KNOWING you are entering sleep. The body sleeps; you remain. This is yoga nidra — conscious sleep. Even if you only achieve this for moments before unconsciousness takes over, those moments are transformative.
  9. The morning insight: Upon waking, notice: something was aware even during sleep. You may not remember it clearly, but there is a sense of continuity — of something that was present through the night even when “you” were gone. That something is the witness — what you truly are.
  10. Practise every night: Make this your nightly ritual. Over 21 days, the periods of conscious sleep will extend. Some nights you will succeed beautifully; other nights sleep will take you immediately. Both are fine. The practice accumulates. Eventually, conscious sleep becomes your natural state.

Signs It’s Working

  • The body feels increasingly distant, heavy, or numb after 10–15 minutes of stillness
  • A clear sense of being separate from the body — witnessing it rather than being it
  • Hypnagogic imagery arising with you AWARE of it rather than lost in it
  • Moments of knowing you are falling asleep while remaining conscious
  • Waking with a sense that awareness persisted through the night
  • Reduced fear of death — death feels like a familiar transition, not an unknown terror

Common Pitfalls

  • Moving even slightly — any movement resets the process; the commitment to stillness must be absolute
  • Trying too hard to stay awake — the goal is not insomnia but CONSCIOUS sleep; let sleep come, just maintain witness
  • Getting excited when the separation begins and thereby losing it — stay neutral, observing
  • Giving up after 5 minutes because “nothing is happening” — 15–20 minutes of total stillness is the minimum
  • Confusing physical relaxation with the death posture — relaxation still implies a living body; this technique requires feeling genuinely DEAD

Questions From The Book

Why does the sutra combine death posture with rage, staring, and sucking?

Chapter 66, "Lie Down As Dead"

Osho explains: all four variations share one principle — TOTALITY. Lie as dead TOTALLY. Be in rage TOTALLY (without acting on it). Stare TOTALLY without blinking. Suck TOTALLY until you become the sucking. The common thread is total absorption that leaves no energy for the ego. When absorption is total, the self drops. The death posture is the most accessible version for sleep practice — but any total absorption creates the same gap.

Is this the same as yoga nidra?

Chapter 66

Osho says: yoga nidra as taught today is a systematized version of this ancient sutra. The principle is identical — maintain awareness while the body enters sleep. Traditional yoga nidra uses body scanning and rotation of consciousness; this sutra uses the more direct method of simply being dead. Both arrive at the same place: conscious sleep, where the witness remains while the body-mind complex rests.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: Sleep paralysis research shows that the body has a natural mechanism for disconnecting motor control during sleep (atonia). This technique deliberately invokes that mechanism through voluntary stillness. By refusing to move, you trigger the body’s sleep-entry protocols while maintaining cortical alertness. The result is a dissociation between body-state (sleeping) and awareness-state (alert) — proving experientially that consciousness is not dependent on bodily activity.

Lucid dreaming research: The Wake-Initiated Lucid Dream (WILD) technique in lucid dreaming research uses exactly this method: maintain awareness while the body falls asleep. Studies show that practitioners can reliably enter REM sleep with metacognitive awareness intact. This 5,000-year-old sutra is the original WILD technique.

One-line essenceLie as dead. Do not move. When the body is truly still, consciousness separates — and you discover you were never the body at all.
Technique 55 of 112
Category: Witnessing · Tags: sleep · threshold · hypnagogia · gap · being
At the point of sleep, when the sleep has not yet come and the external wakefulness vanishes, at this point being is revealed.

In Plain English

Every night as you fall asleep, there is one micro-moment: wakefulness has gone but sleep has not yet arrived. You are neither awake nor asleep. This gap — usually less than a second — is the doorway to your being. Most people miss it because they are already unconscious by then. This technique trains you to catch that exact moment and rest in it. In that gap, you meet yourself directly — without the mediation of waking mind or sleeping unconsciousness.

How to Practice

  1. Timing is everything: This practice happens every night as you fall asleep. There are no special conditions needed — just your bed, darkness, and the willingness to stay alert as consciousness transitions from waking to sleep.
  2. Preparation: Lie in your usual sleeping position. Complete your bedtime routine. Let the body relax completely. There should be no physical tension to distract you.
  3. Set the intention: Before closing your eyes, set a clear intention: “Tonight I will catch the moment between waking and sleep.” This intention acts as an alarm that goes off at the right moment — it plants a seed of alertness that persists even as the waking mind fades.
  4. Watch the dissolution: As you lie still, watch the process of falling asleep. External sounds become distant. The body loses its clear boundaries. Thoughts become fragmented, irrational, dream-like. Images may float through without logic. You are on the threshold.
  5. The critical moment: There is a PRECISE instant where: the external world has completely vanished (you no longer hear the room, feel the bed distinctly) BUT sleep has not yet taken you (you are still dimly aware). This gap between the two is what the sutra points to. It lasts a fraction of a second at first.
  6. Catch it: When you catch this moment, DON’T try to hold onto it (that will snap you back to full wakefulness). Don’t try to analyse it (that’s also waking). Just BE in it. Rest in it. Like floating in warm water — no effort, no holding, just presence.
  7. You will fail many times: For the first weeks, you will either fall asleep and miss the gap entirely, or you will try too hard to stay alert and prevent sleep from coming. Both are normal. The practice is a razor’s edge between trying and not-trying. Keep the intention soft, not forceful.
  8. Use the hypnagogic imagery: As you approach sleep, strange images often appear — faces, landscapes, abstract shapes. These are hypnagogic hallucinations, and they are a SIGN that you are close to the gap. When these images appear, you are on the doorstep. Stay alert but relaxed.
  9. What you find in the gap: When you catch this moment, what is revealed is pure being — consciousness without content. No thoughts, no body-sense, no world — but awareness remains. It is like a vast, peaceful, luminous emptiness. This is what you ARE when you are not being anything in particular.
  10. Expansion over months: Initially the gap lasts a fraction of a second. With practice over weeks and months, it EXTENDS. What was a split-second becomes a few seconds, then minutes. Advanced practitioners report being able to REST in this gap for extended periods while the body sleeps normally.
  11. Morning counterpart: The same gap exists in reverse on waking. There is a moment where sleep has gone but full wakefulness has not yet arrived. Catch this too. The morning gap is often easier because awareness is increasing rather than decreasing.
  12. Connection to turiya: This gap between waking and sleeping is a doorway to turiya — the fourth state beyond the three ordinary states. Each time you rest in the gap, you strengthen your access to turiya. Eventually, turiya becomes available not just at sleep transitions but throughout the day.

  • Giving up because “I just fall asleep” — of course you do. Everyone does. The practice is simply to fall asleep WITH the intention, again and again, until one night you catch it.

Questions From The Book

What is revealed in this gap between waking and sleep?

Chapter 36, "The Point of Sleep"

Osho says: what is revealed is your BEING — not your mind, not your body, not your identity, but the pure existence that underlies all of these. In waking, you know yourself as a person with a name and a story. In sleep, you know nothing. In the gap between the two: you know yourself as EXISTENCE ITSELF — nameless, storyless, bodiless, but absolutely present. This is what the Upanishads call the atman — the self that exists before birth and after death, the self that is never affected by waking or sleeping because it witnesses both.

Why is this gap normally missed?

Chapter 36

Osho explains: the gap is missed because awareness is a spectrum, not an on/off switch. As wakefulness fades, awareness dims gradually. By the time external perception has fully vanished, awareness has usually dimmed below the threshold of self-recognition. You pass through the gap but are too dim to notice yourself passing through it. The technique trains you to maintain JUST ENOUGH alertness to recognise the gap while allowing ENOUGH relaxation for the waking mind to actually release. This is the razor’s edge.

Modern Commentary

AI-distilled, not from Osho

What this is really pointing at: The hypnagogic state (N1 sleep transition) is now well-studied in sleep science. Research by Thomas Edison, Salvador Dalí, and modern creativity researchers shows that this threshold state produces unique cognitive states: the rigid logic of waking dissolves but awareness hasn’t yet disappeared. This is the same state yogis access in yoga nidra (“yogic sleep”) — deep rest with maintained awareness.

Sleep onset research: EEG studies show that the transition from alpha waves (relaxed wakefulness) to theta waves (light sleep) involves a brief period of mixed alpha-theta activity. Meditators trained in this technique show prolonged alpha-theta states at sleep onset — maintaining awareness through the transition that non-meditators pass through unconsciously. The gap is neurologically real and trainably extendable.

One-line essenceEvery night you pass through a door between waking and sleep. Learn to pause in that doorway, and you find what you are when you are neither — pure being, unmasked.

The Techniques for Sleep & Dream

Five techniques that use the sleep transition, rhythmic movement, intense sensation, and bodily dissolution as doorways to conscious sleep. The threshold between waking and sleeping is a portal — cross it with awareness intact and you discover the immortal witness.

5 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These five techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The sleep and dream sutras form the tenth major category — using the sleep transition, death-like stillness, rhythmic movement, and dissolution to maintain awareness through the threshold where most people lose consciousness.

These techniques approach awakening through the nightly miracle of sleep. Every night you cross from waking to sleeping — from somebody to nobody, from self to no-self. Usually this happens unconsciously. These techniques teach you to cross that threshold WITH awareness. When you can sleep consciously, death holds no mystery — death is simply the final sleep.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These sleep techniques work at the TRANSITION point — the hypnagogic threshold. The moment between waking and sleeping is the most vulnerable, most open, most magical moment of the day. These techniques teach you to STAY AWARE through it.

New to sleep meditation?

Start with Technique 1 — lie down as dead or Technique 5 — body dissolving into elements. Both are done lying in bed, require no special preparation, and produce tangible results quickly. Practise every night for 21 nights.

Glossary — unfamiliar terms?

Vigyan Bhairav Tantra
A ~5,000-year-old Sanskrit text presented as a dialogue between Shiva and Devi, listing 112 meditation techniques.
hypnagogic
The transitional state between wakefulness and sleep — characterised by dream-like imagery, body numbness, and reduced critical thinking. The key portal these techniques exploit.
shavasana
Literally “corpse pose” in yoga — lying flat on the back, completely still, like a dead body. The foundation of Technique 1.
conscious sleep
Maintaining awareness while the body and mind enter sleep. The witness remains — you know you are sleeping while you sleep. Also called yoga nidra.
yoga nidra
“Yogic sleep” — a state of conscious deep sleep where awareness persists without content. The deepest rest possible while remaining aware.
dissolution
The feeling of the body’s boundaries dissolving — melting back into the elements (earth, water, fire, air, space) from which it arose.
witness
The pure awareness that observes all states — waking, dreaming, sleeping — without being affected by any of them. What you truly are.
hypnagogic imagery
Spontaneous visual patterns, faces, landscapes that appear at the threshold of sleep. Signs that the transition is happening.
death posture
Lying completely still with no muscular engagement — mimicking a corpse to trigger the body’s sleep mechanisms while maintaining mental alertness.

Practise on your own discretion — these techniques are best done at bedtime and are inherently safe. If sleep disturbance occurs, reduce intensity and return to normal sleep hygiene for a few days before resuming. The goal is not insomnia but CONSCIOUS sleep.
