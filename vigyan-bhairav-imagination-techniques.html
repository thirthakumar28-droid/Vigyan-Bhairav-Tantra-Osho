Technique 81 of 112

As subjectively, letters flow into words and words into sentences, and as, objectively, circles flow into worlds and worlds into principles, find at last these converging in your being.

Inside, letters become words become sentences. Outside, atoms become worlds become great principles. Both rivers are flowing in the same direction — toward you. Open the doors of the body and let the universe pour through, until everything that is happening is happening through you. The ego, which lives by keeping the world out, cannot survive this kind of openness.

How to Practice

  1. This is a technique of vulnerability. Begin by accepting Osho’s starting point: life is insecure by its nature, and any attempt to seal yourself off from it kills the life as well as the danger. The ego is the citadel of security; this technique is meant to make a few cracks in the wall.
  2. Sit under a tree where there is some breeze. Close your eyes. Listen to the rustling of the leaves.
  3. Feel the wind move toward the tree, through the tree, around your body. Now make a small inner shift: instead of letting the wind pass by you, let it pass through you. Become like the tree — porous. The wind enters from one side and leaves from the other.

  6. Try the same with sound. In a temple, beside a bell, beside a waterfall — listen with the whole body, not the ears. Every cell drinks the sound. The sound enters from one side, passes through, leaves from the other. Osho says round temple shapes were built so that bell-sound converges on the listener from all directions, exactly to support this practice.

From every direction, the universe is moving inward. Don’t resist any of it. The second half of the sutra (the objective river — circles into worlds, worlds into principles) is converging on this point you call yourself.

  11. Practise saying inner Yes. Osho calls a real theist someone who has said yes to the whole of existence and has no inner “no.” Each yes opens one more pore. The technique cannot proceed where there is even one held no.
  12. Sit daily under sky, tree, or sun. The training of openness is gradual. Each session, the universe converges a little more freely. The day you discover there is no longer anyone here for it to converge upon — that is the realisation the sutra promises.

Technique 86 of 112
Category: Imagination · Tags: suppose · contemplate · something · beyond
Suppose you contemplate something beyond perception, beyond grasping, beyond not being. – you.

In Plain English

Try to imagine what cannot be imagined — something that cannot be perceived, cannot be grasped, and yet is not nothing. The mind cannot do this; that is the point. Every image it offers, you discard. When the mind has been emptied of all its images and the inner screen is bare, the seer suddenly happens to itself. Shiva ends the sutra with one word — “you.” That is the answer to the impossible contemplation.

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably, eyes closed.
  2. Begin to contemplate something that cannot be perceived. Try to imagine it. The mind will immediately offer pictures — light, space, a void, a blue, a sphere, a god, a face. Watch them rise.
  3. Discard each one. Osho gives the formula: neti, neti — not this, not this. Whatever the mind shows you can be perceived; therefore, that is not it. Drop it.
  4. The next image will appear immediately. Drop that too. And the next, and the next. Be ready: many will come. New combinations will be invented — symbols, colours, sensations. None of them are the thing the sutra is asking for. All can still be perceived. Drop, drop, drop.
  5. Don’t fight the mind. Just keep saying inwardly “not this” and let each image fall. Osho calls this the “throwing the furniture out” phase. The room has to be empty.
  6. Don’t expect to succeed at the contemplation. The contemplation is impossible by design. The point is not to find the unimaginable; the point is the cleaning that the trying produces.
  7. Persist. The mind’s supply of images is large but finite for one sitting. Eventually the screen begins to thin out. The gaps between images grow longer.
  8. One moment the screen is empty. There is no object, no symbol, no dream, no picture — only bare attention with nothing in front of it.
  9. In that moment, Osho says, “the whole attention changes.” With nothing left to perceive, awareness reflects back upon itself. You become aware of the perceiver. The sutra closes with: “… — you.”
  10. Don’t try to grab this. The instant you treat the seer as another object, it has slipped away. Just remain in the empty awareness; let the recognition happen by itself.
  11. Don’t mistake the empty screen for nothing. Osho warns specifically: the mind will say “this is nothing, this doesn’t exist.” Don’t believe it. The sutra calls this “beyond not-being” — emphatically existing, just not perceivable.
  12. Repeat daily. The technique is arduous because the habit of clinging to objects is deep. Osho compares it to Svetketu being sent to live with cows for years until verbal mind dropped. Don’t expect quick results; expect a long, patient subtraction.
  13. End each sitting without judgement. Whether the screen emptied today or not, the practice was the discarding. That itself is the work.

Technique 92 of 112
Category: Imagination · Tags: mind · stuff · inexpressible · fineness
Put mind-stuff in such inexpressible fineness above, below and in your heart.

In Plain English

The Sanskrit word here is chitta — not the busy thinking mind, but the very background on which thoughts float, like the sky on which clouds move. Refine the chitta until it has no thoughts in it at all — until it is so subtle, so empty of content, that nothing can be said about it. Carry that finest, thoughtless awareness above the heart, below the heart, into the heart itself. When pure consciousness reaches the heart, the centre of you shifts back to where it was in childhood, and the world looks completely different.

How to Practice

  1. Begin by understanding the difference between thoughts and mind-stuff. Thoughts are the clouds. Mind-stuff is the sky on which they pass. Today you live in the head, where the sky is full of clouds. The technique is to clear the sky.
  2. Sit upright, eyes closed. Let the body settle.
  3. Don’t fight thoughts. Just stop feeding them. Whatever appears, let it pass without engaging. The cloud moves on if you don’t hold it.
  4. Aim for the precise balance Osho describes. Two states are easy to recognise: ordinary thinking, and dreamless deep sleep. Both miss this technique. You want a third: the thoughtlessness of deep sleep plus the alertness of waking. Patanjali’s samadhi, says Osho, is “like sushupti (deep sleep) with one difference — you are not asleep.”
  5. If the mind goes blank but you fall asleep, you have lost alertness. If you stay alert but thoughts crowd in, you have lost the thoughtlessness. Both must coexist. This is delicate; expect to drift one way or the other for a long time before the balance comes.
  6. When the balance arrives, awareness becomes “inexpressibly fine” — nothing about it can be said, because there is no thing in it. You are aware, but there is no content of awareness.
  7. Now place this fine awareness above the head. Don’t use words; just feel pure consciousness extending upward beyond the crown.
  8. Then below — below the feet, into the ground, into the depths beneath the body. Pure consciousness, no thoughts, extending downward.
  9. Then in the heart. Bring the fine awareness fully into the chest, into the heart-centre. Stay there. The heart cannot hold thoughts; if you arrived with thoughts, you would still be in the head. Only thoughtless consciousness can settle in the heart.
  10. The moment thoughtless awareness rests in the heart, the “centre” of you shifts. Don’t do anything to make this happen — it happens by itself. Osho: “You are thrown back. You are thrown to the heart.”
  11. Don’t fight your ego or try to dissolve it directly. This sutra never mentions ego. Osho is explicit: by simply moving from head to heart, the ego (which is a head-projection) disappears on its own. Fighting it gives it food.
  12. When the sitting ends, return to the head as needed for daily life — but you now know the passage. From this day, you can drop back to the heart any time you need. Each return makes the passage clearer.

Technique 96 of 112
Category: Imagination · Tags: vast-space · openness · no-landmarks · mind-pressure-ends
Abide in some place infinitely spacious, clear of trees, of hills, of habitations. Thence comes the end of mind pressures.

In Plain English

Go to (or imagine) a vast, empty landscape — desert, ocean, salt flat, open sky. No landmarks, no boundaries, no reference points. When the mind has nothing to land on, its pressure dissolves. Without objects to categorise, the mind simply stops.

How to Practice

  1. Find or imagine the space: If possible, physically go to a vast open space — a desert, a beach at low tide, an open plain, a hilltop with panoramic views, a frozen lake. If you cannot go physically, sit with eyes closed and IMAGINE such a space with full sensory detail. The key is: no landmarks, no boundaries, no objects for the mind to grasp.
  2. Open to the vastness: If physically present, stand or sit and look in all directions. Let the enormity of the space sink in. There is nothing to focus on, nothing to name, nothing to categorise. Just space extending in every direction without end. If imagining, build this vastness in your mind’s eye until it feels immersive and real.
  3. Feel the mind searching: Notice what the mind does in vast emptiness: it SEARCHES for objects. It wants something to land on — a tree, a building, a rock, a person. Without landmarks, the mind becomes restless. It scans, seeking something to label. FEEL this search happening. The mind is a categorising machine and here there is nothing to categorise.
  4. Let the search exhaust itself: Don’t fight the restless searching. Let the mind look. Let it scan. Let it try to find something to grasp. But there IS nothing. Gradually — like a bird that cannot find a perch — the mind exhausts its searching and simply STOPS. When there is truly nothing to land on, the bird of mind must rest in mid-air. That resting is meditation.
  5. Feel the pressure dissolve: “Mind pressures” — the sutra’s phrase — refers to the constant mental activity of categorising, labelling, comparing, judging. In the absence of objects, these pressures have nothing to press against. They dissolve. Feel this dissolution physically: the forehead relaxes, the jaw unclenches, the eyes soften, the shoulders drop. Mind pressure is also body pressure.
  6. Become the space: As the mind quiets, your sense of self begins to merge with the space. The boundary between “you” (small, located, bounded) and the space (vast, everywhere, boundless) thins and may disappear entirely. You don’t feel like a person IN space — you feel like space itself, aware. This is the end of mind pressures because there is no longer a compressed “someone” to be pressured.
  7. No reference points: The key principle is: the mind needs REFERENCE POINTS to maintain its activity. Without reference points (no trees, no hills, no habitations), the mind cannot orient, cannot measure, cannot compare. And a mind that cannot compare STOPS. A mind without comparison is a mind at peace. Spaciousness is not just visual — it is the absence of the mental activity that objects provoke.
  8. Stay as long as possible: Whether physically in a vast space or imagining one, stay for at least 20 minutes. The longer you remain in objectless space, the deeper the dissolution goes. Some practitioners find that after 40–60 minutes, the mind reaches a profound silence that can persist for hours afterward.
  9. Indoor variation: If going to vast spaces is impractical, create a minimal environment. Face a white wall. Remove all objects from your visual field. Or: close your eyes and imagine infinite white space in all directions — no floor, no ceiling, no horizon, no objects. Just white openness. The mind will respond the same way: search, fail, and stop.
  10. Daily micro-practice: Even in a cluttered city, you can briefly imagine infinite space around you. In a stressful meeting, in traffic, in an argument — close your eyes for 3 seconds and feel infinite space extending in all directions. The mind pressures immediately reduce. The space is always available in imagination, even when not in reality.

Questions From The Book

Why does the sutra specifically say “clear of trees, of hills, of habitations”?

Chapter 74, "Spaciousness and Mind"

Osho explains: each item represents a category of mental object. Trees represent natural objects (the mind says “ah, a tree”). Hills represent large-scale landmarks (the mind orients itself relative to them). Habitations represent human structures (the mind thinks of people, social roles, purpose). By removing ALL three categories — natural objects, landmarks, and human structures — the sutra eliminates every possible thing the mind could grasp. Without ANY category of object, the mind is truly stranded in emptiness. And that is precisely where liberation lives.

Can imagination of space be as effective as physically being there?

Chapter 74

Osho says: yes, if the imagination is TOTAL. The nervous system responds to vividly imagined experience almost identically to actual experience. If you can truly FEEL infinite space — if your body responds with relaxation, expansion, and openness — then the imagination is as effective as reality. The key is totality of involvement. A half-hearted imagination of space will not work. But a vivid, whole-body, whole-mind immersion in imagined vastness produces the same dissolution of mind pressure as standing in the Sahara.

In Plain English

Plenum means the whole open space — the endless sky. Sitting in some vast natural place, feel that the whole sky is your own innermost “bliss body” (the deepest, second-to-last layer Tantra speaks of, just around the soul).   1. This technique pairs with sutra #96 (“Abide in some place endlessly spacious, clear of trees, hills, habitations”). Use the same setting: a hilltop, an open beach, a desert, a roof under open sky, anywhere with endless space and no human beings around.
  2. Sit alone, eyes open at first. The aloneness is essential. Osho explains why: the moment you see a human being you start reacting in old patterns; alone, you can forget for a while that you are even “a human being.”

  4. Begin with silence. Let the eyes rest on the open sky. Feel the silence around you — not as the absence of sound, but as a quality. The wind, the bird, the rustle of leaves, all of these can be silence too if they are harmonious; in nature even noises are silent.

  7. Once that glimpse is there, the second half of the technique becomes possible. Now imagine that this very quality — the bliss you have just tasted — is filling the whole sky. Not filling it from outside, but the sky is bliss.
  8. Then take Shiva’s exact step: the bliss body is yours. The whole plenum is your own body of bliss. The sky becomes the inside of you, the largest layer of yourself. You are not under the sky; the sky is your innermost robe.
  9. Stay there. Don’t move, don’t analyse. The body underneath, sitting on the hilltop, becomes a small visible point inside the much larger bliss-body that is the sky.
  10. Don’t fight thoughts. They will arise less when this magnitude is held. If they do come, return to silence first, then to bliss; never skip the order.
  11. If silence and bliss feel too abstract, follow Buddha’s suggestion that Osho recommends in this exact passage: substitute compassion. Everyone has tasted love at least a little. Feel that the whole sky is filled with your compassion. Then bliss will rise from compassion.
  12. End the sitting slowly. Bring the bliss-body back to the size of your physical body. Sit a few minutes before standing. The flavour of the practice tends to last for hours afterwards as a mild quiet sweetness.

  • A feeling of kinship with everything — trees, people, sky all felt as part of your own being

Common Pitfalls

  • Imagining expansion intellectually rather than FEELING it — the body must participate; expansion is felt, not thought
  • Losing the “within” while expanding to “around” — the sutra says SIMULTANEOUSLY; maintain both
  • Creating a grandiose ego (“I am the universe!”) rather than recognising that the “I” dissolves in the expansion
  • Forcing expansion beyond what feels natural — let it unfold gradually over many sessions
  • Confusing dissociation (feeling nothing in the body) with expansion (feeling EVERYTHING including the body)

The Techniques for Imagination

Six techniques that use imagination — not fantasy, but the creative faculty that stretches the mind beyond its limits — as doorways to the formless. Imagination exhausts itself trying to grasp the ungraspable, and in that failure, truth reveals itself.

6 techniques · ~8 min read · works fully offline

About these techniques

These six techniques are drawn from the Vigyan Bhairav Tantra, a Sanskrit text dated to roughly 5,000 years ago, in which Shiva tells his consort Devi 112 ways to recognise the divine within ordinary life.

Osho (1931–1990), an Indian mystic, gave a series of talks unpacking each one; that material was published as The Book of Secrets. The imagination sutras form the fourteenth major category — using the creative faculty of the mind not to build fantasy but to exhaust itself against the formless, the paradoxical, and the unnameable.

These techniques approach awakening through imagination’s OWN failure. Imagination cannot grasp the formless, cannot hold paradox, cannot survive semantic satiation. In each technique, imagination is asked to do the impossible — and in its failure, the door opens to that which lies beyond the mind’s reach.

How to use this page

Pick ONE technique. Practise it for 21 days. Don’t shop, don’t switch, don’t combine. Twenty-one days is the threshold the book itself recommends — long enough for resistance to surface, peak, and pass.

If on day 7 you think it isn’t working — that is the work. Stay.

Key principle: These imagination techniques work by stretching the mind to its breaking point. You are not creating images for their own sake — you are using the ATTEMPT to imagine the unimaginable as the doorway. The failure of imagination IS the success of meditation.

New to imagination meditation?

Start with Technique 4 — infinitely spacious place or Technique 6 — enter your name. Both are accessible, produce tangible results quickly, and build confidence for the subtler techniques. Spend 20–30 minutes a day for 21 days. Then return for the paradox and formlessness work.

Glossary — unfamiliar terms?

Vigyan Bhairav Tantra
A ~5,000-year-old Sanskrit text presented as a dialogue between Shiva and Devi, listing 112 meditation techniques.
imagination
Not fantasy but the creative faculty that can stretch the mind beyond its limits — used here as a tool for transcendence, not escapism.
formless
That which has no shape — cannot be imagined but can be experienced. The mind always creates form; the formless is what remains when form-making exhausts itself.
paradox
Holding two contradictions simultaneously to break the mind’s binary logic. The mind cannot hold “both and neither” — it short-circuits into silence.
am-ness
The raw feeling of existence prior to all qualities. Not “I am something” but pure “I AM” — being without description.
spaciousness
The inner correlate of outer space — the mind’s natural state when objects are removed. Not emptiness as lack but emptiness as freedom.
semantic satiation
The psychological phenomenon of a word losing meaning through repetition. When a name becomes pure sound, the naming faculty of mind breaks.
